Same question, answered from the statutes:

A DMCA notice is the wrong instrument for most counterfeits. The DMCA (17 U.S.C. §512) is a copyright notice-and-takedown regime: it reaches copied images or designs, not the fake use of your name or logo. Trademark counterfeiting runs through the Lanham Act: infringement of a registered mark under §32 (15 U.S.C. §1114) and false designation of origin under §43(a) (§1125(a)).

And you can only assert §32 with a federal registration, so the first move is confirming your marks are on the USPTO Principal Register for the right classes, not blasting takedowns. Where counterfeiting is proven, §1116–1117 unlock injunctions, seizure, and statutory damages of $1,000–$2,000,000 per mark: remedies a form notice never provides.
