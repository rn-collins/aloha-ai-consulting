Aloha AI Consulting  ·  Honolulu, HI  ·  RN Collins

For psychedelic health · biopharma · cannabis · legaltech · digital health organizations · legal teams · any business

Every organization has work that AI should be handling and isn't. For most businesses, the gap is straightforward: no one has built the system yet. This practice closes that gap — for any sector, at any stage, regulated or not.

For organizations working in regulated innovation sectors, three specific problems compound it.

The organizations doing the most credible work face three compounding problems. Almost everyone treats each one as unrelated.

Problem 01

They cannot talk about what they know. Your organization publishes clinically rigorous content that would be front-page coverage in any other era — and Instagram showed it to 60 people. No violation notice. No appeal pathway. Just a systematic reduction in reach that the platform will not acknowledge and you cannot explain to funders. Platform suppression, regulatory exposure, and communication constraints systematically silence the organizations doing the most credible work. This is a regulatory and infrastructure problem, not a content quality problem.

Problem 02

They cannot trust what AI tells them. Large language models hallucinate on the specific facts that operators need: wrong APQ figures, wrong statutory triggers, wrong CFR citations, wrong case names. The Operator Execution Gap is widest exactly where the stakes are highest.

Problem 03

They cannot build what the work requires. Most AI consultants advise. Most developers build without knowing the domain. Law firms evaluating Harvey need someone who has done actual legal research under attorney supervision — not someone who learned the platform from a dashboard. Nonprofits with the credibility and peer relationships to run a consulting practice need the AI infrastructure built before they can scale it. Any organization that needs a bespoke intelligence tool — a sector-specific monitoring system, a knowledge directory, a multi-layer AI platform — faces the same gap: technical depth without domain knowledge, or domain knowledge without technical depth. Very few practitioners hold both.

These three problems compound across regulated innovation sectors — psychedelic health, biopharma, cannabis, legaltech, digital health — for organizations ranging from a harm reduction nonprofit building its first consulting practice to an AmLaw firm deploying Harvey to a clinical-stage company that needs a purpose-built regulatory intelligence platform. For every other organization — the restaurant, the law firm, the agency, the consultant — the same practice builds what the work requires without the regulatory complexity. In every case: the focus is not the tool. It is the human system around the tool.

All three have the same answer: primary-source intelligence and purpose-built systems, delivered by someone who can read a statute, evaluate a clinical trial, navigate a regulatory docket, build the intelligence layer, and deploy the custom tool — across AI content systems, regulatory intelligence, consulting practice architecture, legal AI workflow, and bespoke intelligence builds.

Why there is no direct competitor

Any one of the five components can be found elsewhere. The combination cannot.

General AI consulting firms: cannot produce primary-source regulatory analysis in administrative law.
General legal research firms: not building AI-integrated knowledge bases or monitoring platform suppression. Psychedelic health consultants: not combining regulatory law, AI systems design, and content operations.
Biopharma regulatory consultants: experienced in FDA pathways, not in DEA scheduling or AI content systems.
Very few practitioners hold the combination — and none have deployed it as a structured practice across these five service lines simultaneously.
And most AI consultants focus on the tool. This practice focuses on the human system around the tool — how people understand it, trust it, adopt it, and use it in their real work. That is a developmental psychology and neuroscience problem. It is also the only AI problem that actually matters once the tool is deployed.

Original analytical instruments

These terms and frameworks are original to this practice. Named, defined, and documented — the intellectual property that distinguishes this from general consulting.

IDR — Ingest, Digest, Regurgitate

The core processing engine underlying all intelligence and content work. Any domain, any signal. Ingest (primary-source collection) → Digest (synthesis, analysis, pattern recognition) → Regurgitate (structured deliverable, knowledge base entry, or client output). Domain-agnostic — applied identically across regulatory intelligence, market research, content systems, legal analysis, and brand intelligence work.

Regulatory Intelligence Layer (RIL)

The primary-source data methodology — statutes, administrative records, docket filings, APQ data, CPT coding records, case law — organized to answer the specific questions operators need answered before commercial or capital decisions. Retained entirely by the builder. Licensed to clients as the ROL.

Regulatory Operator Layer (ROL)

The client-licensed implementation of the RIL inside a specific platform or workflow. Each client's ROL is scoped to their domain and built from the same underlying primary-source layer. The RIL is never exclusively licensed — it grows with each new engagement.

EDSA Framework

Evidence, Discussion, Self-Determination, Action.

A four-element script structure for YouTube-compliant content on regulated innovation sectors — psychedelic health, cannabis, biopharma, and digital health. Note: distinct from YouTube's own "EDSA" (Educational, Documentary, Scientific, or Artistic) exception, which is a platform content-policy category — this framework is a production methodology for compliance within that exception. Developed independently. IP retained by RN Collins. Licensed to each consulting client for use within their engagement.

Operator Execution Gap (OEG)

A proprietary analytical concept: the specific gap between what an operator needs to know and what current AI tools can reliably produce in the regulatory domain. Distinct from hallucination (which concerns fabrication), retrieval-augmented generation (which concerns access), and the academic "Reasoning-Execution Gap" (which concerns low-level action translation). The OEG is specifically about the regulatory knowledge layer — where primary-source precision is non-negotiable and hallucination risk is highest. This is the problem this practice was built to close.

Commercial Significance Framework (CSF)

The evaluative structure applied to each record in the regulatory data set to determine whether a data point has material commercial implications. Determines what goes into the RIL and what stays out. Ensures the intelligence layer contains only what changes decisions.

Build-Manage Loop

The iterative engagement structure: module build → management retainer → update cadence → expansion vector activation → repeat.

Self-reinforcing: more modules produce more retainer revenue, which funds more builds, which produces more modules. Engagements are designed to deepen over time.

0-to-1 Intelligence

Original analytical work that did not previously exist in any published form.

The quarterly deliverable demonstrating this is not a data aggregation service — it is an intelligence production operation. The Commercial-Launch APQ Gap Model is the first instrument of this type.

Commercial-Launch APQ Gap Model

An original analytical instrument estimating the commercial supply gap between DEA Annual Production Quota authorizations and projected treatment demand — modeled from sponsor trial dose-frequency data and addressable patient population estimates.

APQ/AAN (Assessment of Annual Needs) data is publicly available via the Federal Register (DEA publishes proposed and final aggregate quota notices annually; the 2026 final order, FR Doc 2025-24277, raised psilocybin to 50,000g — up 67% from 2025). Individual manufacturer quotas are confidential ARCOS-level data. The value of this instrument is the interpretation gap: translating public aggregate production authorizations into commercial-launch demand projections that sponsors can act on. No published template for this instrument exists in the regulatory literature. Developed in an active biopharma engagement.
