Why this exists

The confusion is usually about structure, not facts

Most people who ask "is this legal?" are actually missing the map: two governments regulate in parallel, several agencies each own a different piece, and status can move through a rulemaking process that most explainers skip. This tool teaches the map. Once you understand how a substance is classified and who controls that classification, you know exactly where to look for the current answer, and you know why a headline about one state does not settle the question.

What it explains

The five-schedule system and the criteria behind each schedule, the split roles of the DEA and FDA, the FDA-to-DEA scheduling recommendation process, the federal-state divergence pattern, and how a substance is moved between schedules through petition, review, and rulemaking.

It will not assert any substance's current legal status in any jurisdiction as authoritative, and it will not give legal advice. Status changes, and a stale claim relied on is a real risk. What it explains

Four things everyone gets wrong, made clear

Each explanation is built from these stable structural pieces. None of them depend on a specific state's current status, which is exactly why they stay useful while the headlines change.

The schedules

Five tiers, defined by criteria

Schedules I through V rank substances by accepted medical use and potential for abuse. The schedule sets the federal controls; it is not the same thing as "banned" or "allowed."

The agencies

DEA, FDA, and the states

The DEA schedules and enforces, the FDA approves drugs and makes scheduling recommendations, and each state runs its own parallel program. Different agencies, different questions.

The process

How status actually moves

A substance changes schedule through petition, FDA scientific review, a DEA recommendation request, proposed rulemaking, a public comment period, and a final rule. It is a process, not a switch.

The divergence

Why federal and state disagree

Federal and state law operate in parallel. A substance can be federally controlled while states run their own separate programs, which is why one headline never settles the question.

Verify

Where the current answer lives

Every explanation ends by pointing you to the Federal Register, the DEA, the FDA, the relevant state agencies, and a licensed attorney for the point-in-time, jurisdiction-specific answer.

Stable by design

No claims that go stale

The tool avoids stating any state's current specific status as authoritative. It teaches the pattern so you can read the current sources yourself, correctly.
