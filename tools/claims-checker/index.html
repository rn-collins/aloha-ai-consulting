Why this exists

In regulated categories, the words carry the risk

A supplement that says it "supports focus" sits in one world. The same product that says it "treats ADHD" sits in another, where the FDA reads it as an unapproved drug claim and the FTC asks for the substantiation behind it. The line between the two is language, and it moves fast. This checker gives your copy a first pass, surfacing the phrases that tend to attract a second look so a person can decide what to do about them.

What it checks

Disease and medical claims, structure-function overreach, substantiation gaps, deceptive consumer-protection patterns, and the superlatives and absolutes that draw scrutiny. Each match is tied to a category, a risk level, and the regulator whose zone it touches.

What it can't do

It cannot tell you whether copy is lawful, and it gives no legal advice. It matches language to curated patterns and explains why each pattern tends to draw attention. It misses claims phrased in ways the lexicon does not cover, and it flags some phrases that context makes fine. A qualified human decides.

What it flags

Five categories, mapped to the regulators who care

Each flag names the category the phrase falls into, a risk level, and the regulator whose attention zone it tends to touch. The overall risk score weights High findings heaviest, then Medium, then Low.

FDA

Disease / medical claims

Language that says a product treats, cures, prevents, or diagnoses a named condition. This is the fast path to being read as an unapproved drug.

FDA

Structure-function overreach

Therapeutic framing beyond a permitted structure-function statement: "heals," "clinically proven," "doctor recommended," and similar.

FTC

Substantiation gaps

Proof claims with no qualifier behind them: "studies show," "proven," "guaranteed," "100%," "#1." The FTC asks what backs them.

FTC / State AG

Deceptive patterns

Consumer-protection triggers: "miracle," "risk-free," "no side effects," and "FDA approved" used where it does not apply.

FTC / State AG

Superlatives & absolutes

Unqualified absolutes like "always," "never fails," "completely safe," and "the best." They raise the bar on what you must be able to prove.
