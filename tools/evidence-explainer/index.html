Why this exists

The claim type tells you what evidence it owes you

"People say it helped their nausea" and "it cures nausea" are different kinds of statements, and they carry very different burdens. One reports experience. The other asserts cause and effect, which is the kind of claim that a controlled trial exists to test. When you can name the claim type, you can ask the right question of the evidence behind it. This tool does that first pass for you, so a person can decide what to make of a claim before repeating it.

What it does

Reads the language of a claim for signal words, classifies it (causal, mechanism, correlation, anecdotal, or overstated), and maps that type to the tier of evidence it would take to support it. It explains the evidence hierarchy, lists common confounders to check, and hands you questions to ask before repeating the claim.

What it does not do

It does not judge whether a claim is true or false. It gives no medical advice, no diagnosis, no treatment or dosing guidance, and no health recommendation. It cannot read a study for you or grade the actual evidence that exists. It classifies the language and points you toward the primary literature and qualified people who can.

    Worked example

    Cannabinoid Hyperemesis Syndrome, read as an evidence question

    CHS comes up often in cannabis reporting, and it is a useful case for practicing evidence literacy. The panel below describes only what the published evidence base discusses and how strong that evidence is generally understood to be. It is a description of the literature, not a claim about your health.

    Not medical advice. This panel summarizes how the literature describes a topic at a high level. It is not a diagnosis, not treatment guidance, and not a recommendation. If you are unwell or have symptoms, contact a qualified clinician.

    What the evidence base discusses

    The published literature describes Cannabinoid Hyperemesis Syndrome (CHS) as a pattern of recurrent, cyclic nausea and vomiting reported in some people with long-term, frequent cannabis use. Reports in the literature also describe symptoms easing after people stop using cannabis. Some case reports note that hot showers or baths are associated with temporary symptom relief. These are the recurring features the evidence base talks about at a high level.

    How strong is that evidence base

    Much of the CHS literature is built from case reports and case series, which sit on the lower rungs of the evidence hierarchy. That body of work is useful for describing a pattern and generating hypotheses. It is weaker for establishing cause and effect than controlled studies would be. Reviews of CHS commonly note that diagnostic criteria and mechanisms are still debated and that higher-tier evidence is limited. Reading any single CHS claim well means asking which tier of evidence stands behind it.

    Questions to ask before repeating a CHS claim

    • Is the source a case report, a case series, an observational study, or a systematic review, and how far does that tier let the claim reach?
    • Does the claim describe an association that was reported, or does it assert that cannabis use caused the symptoms?
    • Were other causes of cyclic vomiting considered and ruled out in the source?
    • Is a reported improvement after stopping cannabis presented as observed, or as proven cause and effect?
    • How large and how representative was the group the claim is based on?

    Where to go next

    • Primary literature: search PubMed and Google Scholar for "cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome" and read the studies and reviews themselves rather than summaries of them.
    • Qualified people: a physician, gastroenterologist, or toxicologist can speak to an individual situation. This tool cannot.

    How it reads a claim

    Five claim types, mapped to the evidence they owe

    The analyzer looks for the signal words that mark each claim type, then explains what tier of evidence a claim of that type would need and what confounders to check. It never grades the underlying science. It grades the language and points you at the questions.

    Highest bar

    Causal claims

    "Causes," "cures," "prevents," "treats." Asserts cause and effect. Needs controlled trials, ideally more than one, not case reports or testimonials.

    Context needed

    Mechanism claims

    "Works by," "binds to," "activates receptors." A plausible mechanism is not proof of a clinical effect. Ask whether the effect itself was measured in people.

    Watch confounders

    Correlation / association

    "Linked to," "associated with," "users report lower." Association is not causation. Check for confounders, reverse causation, and selection effects.

    Lowest tier

    Anecdotal framing

    "People report," "I felt," "many say." Experience is real data, but a single tier of it cannot carry a causal or population claim.

    Overstated

    Absolute / overstated language

    "Proven," "guaranteed," "100%," "miracle." Absolutes raise the bar of proof and rarely survive contact with the actual evidence.

    Reference

    The evidence hierarchy

    Case report, then case series, then observational study, then randomized controlled trial, then systematic review and meta-analysis. Higher rungs control more bias.
