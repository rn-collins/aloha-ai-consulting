Why this exists

ABA 512 turned "govern your AI" into a conduct duty

ABA Formal Opinion 512 reads the existing Model Rules onto generative AI: the duty of competent, confidential, candid, and supervised use already applies. The hard part is showing it. A firm that uses AI well but writes none of it down cannot demonstrate the practice when it counts. This scorecard checks for the documented controls that turn good intentions into a defensible record.

What it checks

Whether the controls each duty implies actually exist in writing: a policy every timekeeper acknowledged, a required human verification step, tool vetting for client confidentiality, named supervisory accountability, and a documentation trail.

What it can't do

It cannot tell you whether your firm is compliant, and it does not give legal advice. It maps your answers to published guidance and shows where your documented practice is thin. A licensed attorney interprets what that means for your obligations.

What it measures

Five groups, mapped to the duties

Each question belongs to a duty group. Yes scores full credit, Partial scores half, No scores nothing. The groups roll into per-duty sub-scores and one overall 0–100 readiness score, weighted evenly across the duties.

Rule 1.1

Competence

Whether the people using AI understand its limits, and whether verification is required before AI-assisted work goes out.

Rule 1.6

Confidentiality

Whether approved tools are vetted so client information is not exposed to third parties or training.

Rule 3.3

Candor to the tribunal

Whether a human verification gate stands between AI output and anything filed with a court.

Rules 5.1 / 5.3

Supervision

Whether AI use is supervised with named accountability over both lawyers and non-lawyer assistance.

Governance

Policy & documentation

Whether a written, acknowledged policy and an audit trail exist, so the practice can be shown, not just asserted.
