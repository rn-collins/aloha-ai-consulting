The Operator Execution Gap · practice build

"Just hire an AI consultant" is the confident wrong answer.

The generic advice for standing up a practice is fluent and reassuring, and it skips the parts that actually determine whether the build survives its first year. Same question, two answers. The distance between them is the cost of a stalled build.

✕ Generic advice · ungrounded

"How do we build our own AI-consulting practice?"

"Hire an AI consultant or take a course, pick a few tools, and start offering services. Your domain expertise is the hard part — the rest is easy once you have someone who knows AI."

Confident. Fluent. Wrong about where practices actually fail. Tooling and a hire don't produce a practice; delivery infrastructure, a real pipeline, priced offers, and utilized capacity do. This advice sends you into a stalled build.

✓ Aloha AI · operations-grounded PS operating fundamentals

Same question, answered from how practices actually run:

A practice is an operating system, not a skill. The binding constraints are rarely AI knowledge. They are: a productized, priced offer you can sell repeatably; delivery infrastructure (scoping, QA, handoff) so quality doesn't depend on one person; a client pipeline matched to your delivery capacity; and utilization in the healthy band (roughly 70–90% billable, by firm type) that leaves room to sell and improve.

Miss the match between pipeline and capacity and you get one of two failure modes: bench risk (capacity idle, no demand) or delivery risk (sold more than you can deliver). Both stall the build. A hire fixes neither on its own.

Confidence: High  Why you can rely on it: it maps to documented professional-services operating fundamentals (utilization/capacity, pipeline-to-headcount matching, and productization) cited in the method ledger below.

OEG (the Operator Execution Gap) is the distance between what generic advice says and what an organization can actually build and run. Closing it is the entire job.
