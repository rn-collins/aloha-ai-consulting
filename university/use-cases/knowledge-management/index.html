Use case

AI for Knowledge Management

Your team already wrote the answers. They are buried in a wiki nobody updated, a Slack thread from March, and someone's inbox. This turns that pile into an answer engine that cites its own sources — and flags the ones that have gone stale.

The idea

What this use case is

Knowledge management with AI means grounding a language model in your documents, so it answers from what your company actually knows rather than from the open internet. You point it at a defined set of sources — handbooks, SOPs, contracts, past tickets, product docs — and it retrieves the relevant passage, drafts an answer, and shows you which document it came from.

The pattern has a technical name, retrieval-augmented generation, and you do not need it to run this. What you need is the discipline behind it: a fixed source set, answers that cite where they came from, and one person who owns keeping the sources current. Skip any of those three and you have built a confident liar.

Who it helps

Who this is for

Support and success teams

Drowning in repeat questions that already have a documented answer somewhere.

Operations leads

Who wrote the SOPs and are tired of being the human search bar for everyone else.

New hires

In their first 90 days, needing answers without interrupting three people to get one.

Small firms and solo operators

Whose "knowledge base" is their own memory and a folder of PDFs named final_v3.

The problem

The business problem it solves

Knowledge does not disappear because it is wrong. It disappears because it is scattered. A policy lives in a PDF, its exception lives in a Slack DM, and the current version lives in one person's head. When that person is out, the work stops — or worse, someone guesses.

This workflow attacks three costs directly: the hours senior people spend re-answering solved questions, the inconsistency of ten people giving ten versions of one policy, and the onboarding drag of new hires who cannot find anything. You are not buying a smarter chatbot. You are making your best answers reusable.

Inputs

What you need going in

A defined source set

Decide what counts as truth: which folders, which docs, which version. Exclude drafts and superseded files.

Clean, current documents

One canonical version of each policy. Archive the rest so the model cannot cite a dead one.

A named owner

One person responsible for adding, retiring, and correcting sources. Not a committee.

A list of real questions

The ones your team actually asks. Pull them from tickets, or ask three colleagues what they Slack you.

Tools

Suggested tools

Google NotebookLM

Upload your source set and ask questions grounded strictly in it. Answers link to the exact passage. Fast to start and strong for a bounded document set.

ChatGPT or Claude Projects

Attach reference files to a workspace and pin instructions so every answer draws from them. Good when the team already lives in one of these.

Notion AI or your wiki's built-in AI

Search and answer across pages you already maintain. The lowest lift when your knowledge already lives there.

On sensitive content: for regulated or confidential material, read the tool's data-handling terms before you upload. Consumer tiers may use your inputs for training; business and enterprise tiers — NotebookLM Enterprise on Google Cloud, for example — keep data in your own project and do not train on it. Match the tier to the sensitivity.

The workflow

Step by step

  1. Scope the source set. List every document that should count as an authoritative answer. Ruthlessly exclude anything outdated.
  2. Deduplicate and label versions. Keep one canonical copy of each policy, date it, and archive the rest out of the tool's reach.
  3. Load the sources into your chosen tool as a single named workspace or notebook.
  4. Write a grounding instruction that tells the model to answer only from the sources and to say plainly when something is not covered.
  5. Test with your real question list. Confirm every answer cites a source and matches the document.
  6. Fix the gaps. Where the model cannot answer, that is a missing document, not a model failure. Write it.
  7. Publish access to the team and set a monthly review to retire stale sources.

The prompt

An example prompt

Paste this as the grounding instruction for your workspace or notebook:

You are our internal knowledge assistant. Answer only using the
documents loaded into this workspace.

Rules:
- Every answer must quote or paraphrase a specific source and name
  the document it came from.
- If the answer is not in the sources, reply exactly:
  "Not covered in the current sources — flag for [owner name]."
- If two sources conflict, say so and name both. Do not pick a winner.
- Never use outside knowledge or fill gaps with assumptions.

Question: What is our refund window for annual plans, and are there
exceptions for enterprise contracts?

What good looks like back: the assistant quotes the billing policy and the MSA by name, respects the contract override, and flags that the FAQ still lists an old 14-day window. That last flag is the real payoff — it caught a documentation bug you would have shipped to a customer.

Guardrails

What a human still owns

Verify before you trust

For any answer that drives a customer promise, money, or a legal position, open the cited source and confirm it says what the model claims.

Own the conflicts

When the assistant flags contradicting sources, a person decides which is correct and fixes the losing document.

Watch for confident gaps

An answer with no citation is unverified. Treat it as the model reaching outside the sources, not as fact.

Review monthly

Retire superseded documents so the assistant can never cite a dead policy back to your team.

This is not legal, medical, or financial advice. An answer grounded in your documents is a fast first draft of the truth, not a ruling. A person owns the final word.
