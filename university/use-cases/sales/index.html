Use case

AI for Sales

Selling a service is mostly conversations, and every conversation is surrounded by admin — research before the call, notes during it, follow-ups after, a proposal to draft, a CRM to keep honest. AI is good at that surrounding work. Hand the busywork to a machine and give the actual selling back to a human. This is not autonomous outbound spam: a person approves every message and every record.

What it is

AI as the assistant around the sale, not the seller

For a service business, "AI for sales" does not mean a bot that emails a thousand strangers overnight. It means using AI to compress the repetitive work that clusters around every deal, so the person who actually sells has more time and a clearer head for the parts that need judgment.

Concretely, that covers a handful of jobs:

  • Call prep and research. Pulling public facts about a prospect into a tight brief before you dial.
  • Discovery-note summarization. Turning a messy transcript or scribbled notes into a clean, structured summary.
  • Follow-up drafting. A first draft of the email you owe someone, written in your voice.
  • Proposal first-drafts. A starting skeleton built from your own templates and the deal's specifics.
  • CRM hygiene. Extracting the fields and next steps that should have been logged but usually are not.
  • Objection-handling prep. Rehearsing the pushback you are likely to hear so you are not improvising live.

What it is not: a system that invents facts, sends without you, or pretends a template is a relationship. Every external message and every CRM update passes through a human before it counts.

Who it helps

People who sell and do the work

  • The founder-seller. You run the business and close the deals, and the admin around selling is what slips first. Prep gets rushed, follow-ups go out late, and the CRM is a graveyard. AI hands you a prep brief and a follow-up draft in the gap between meetings.
  • The small sales team (2–8 reps). You have a real motion but no operations person to keep notes clean and handoffs tidy. AI standardizes summaries and CRM fields so the pipeline reflects reality, not whoever remembered to type.
  • The fractional revenue leader. You carry several clients at once and cannot hold every account's context in your head. AI reconstructs where each deal stands from the notes you already have.
  • Not a fit: anyone hoping to automate cold outbound at volume, or to let AI negotiate, quote prices, or send without review. This use case assists a human seller, it does not replace one — and it assumes you have real conversations to support in the first place.

The problem

The selling gets crowded out by the paperwork

Watch where a service seller's week actually goes and a pattern shows up fast. Follow-ups slip because writing a good one from scratch takes twenty minutes you do not have, so the email that should have gone out Tuesday goes out Friday, or never. Prep gets rushed because you walk into the call straight off the last one, so you ask questions you could have answered yourself and burn goodwill. And notes never make it into the CRM, because logging a call properly after a long day is the easiest thing in the world to skip — which means the next conversation starts cold and the forecast is fiction.

None of these is a motivation problem. They are all the same problem: the admin around selling is real work, it lands at the worst moments, and there is no slack to absorb it. So it gets dropped, and the cost shows up later as slow deals, forgotten commitments, and a pipeline nobody trusts.

AI does not close deals for you. It removes most of the minutes between a conversation and its paperwork — the brief, the summary, the follow-up draft, the CRM fields — so the human parts actually happen, on time, at a quality you would sign your name to.

Inputs

What you feed it determines what it gives back

AI can only work from what you provide. For sales tasks, gather a few honest inputs before you ask for anything — and reuse them across every deal.

CRM records and deal notes

The current state of the opportunity — stage, prior touches, who is involved — so summaries and follow-ups build on history instead of starting blank.

Call transcripts or notes

A recording transcript from a notetaker, or the rough notes you typed during the call. Messy is fine; the point is to have the raw material.

Your ICP

A written definition of who you serve well, so prep and qualification apply a consistent standard rather than a vibe.

Product and pricing facts

What you actually sell, how it is packaged, and your real pricing — so the model never has to guess at a commitment. If you would not want it invented, write it down and hand it over.

Prior winning proposals

Two or three proposals that closed, as the pattern for first-drafts and as a sample of your voice.

Keep it in one place

Save these as a short deal brief you can paste atop every prompt. That block is the reason your summaries and drafts sound like your business rather than a stranger.

Suggested tools

Start with two, add a third if it earns its place

You do not need a stack to begin. An AI chat tool plus your existing CRM covers most of this. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm on the vendor's site before you buy. Do not take any number here as gospel.

AI assistant (required)

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, each around $20/month at the time of writing — verify current pricing before subscribing. This does the research synthesis, summarizing, and drafting. The free tiers are fine for trying the workflow; the paid tiers give you stronger models and higher limits once it is part of your week.

A meeting notetaker

A tool like Fathom records calls and produces transcripts and AI summaries. Its free tier records unlimited meetings but caps AI-generated summaries at around 5 per month; paid plans run roughly $16–20 per user/month. Pricing and caps change — verify the current terms before relying on them. Any comparable notetaker works; the value is a reliable transcript.

Your CRM

Whatever you already use — HubSpot, Pipedrive, or even a well-kept spreadsheet — as the system of record. AI helps you keep it clean; it does not replace it. If you have no CRM, a simple sheet is a fine starting point.

A note on cost and claims

Prices, free-tier limits, and features listed here can shift between when this was written and when you read it. Treat every figure as a prompt to check, not a promise. Start with the required AI tool, prove the workflow saves you time, and only then add paid layers.

Workflow

Research to logged, with a human at the wheel

  1. Research the prospect. Before the call, ask your assistant to pull public facts about the company and the person — what they do, recent news, likely priorities — into a short brief. Verify anything you will actually say.
  2. Prep the agenda. From that brief and your ICP, have it draft a handful of discovery questions and flag the objections you are likely to hear, so you walk in with a plan instead of improvising.
  3. Capture the call. Run your notetaker, or type rough notes yourself. Do not try to write and listen at the same time — capture the raw material and clean it later.
  4. Summarize and extract action items. Feed the transcript or notes to your assistant and ask for a structured summary — pains, budget signals, timeline, decision process — plus a clean list of next steps and who owns each.
  5. Draft the follow-up. Have it write a first-draft follow-up email in your voice, referencing only what was actually said, with one clear next step.
  6. Human approve. Read the draft. Fix names and figures, check every commitment against your real pricing, cut anything that sounds like a template, and only then send it yourself.
  7. Log to the CRM. Paste the approved summary and next steps into the deal record, update the stage, and set the follow-up task — the part that usually gets skipped now takes a minute.

The AI does the research and the drafting. You keep the reading, the judgment, and the send button.

Example prompt

Turn messy discovery notes into a summary plus a follow-up

Paste this into your assistant, fill the brackets, and drop your raw notes in at the end. It is written to fight the two things that make sales AI bad: fabrication and template voice.

You are helping me process notes from a discovery call.

About me / what I sell: [one or two sentences]
My voice — match this tone exactly:
[paste 2-3 real emails you've sent]

Below are my raw, messy notes from the call:
[paste transcript or rough notes]

Produce two things.

1. A structured summary with these labeled sections:
   - Pains: the problems they described, in their words
   - Budget: any budget or pricing signals mentioned
   - Timeline: when they want to act, and why
   - Decision process: who is involved and what happens next
   - Next steps: a bullet list, each with an owner (me or them)

2. A follow-up email, under 120 words, in my voice.
   - Reference one specific thing they actually said.
   - State one clear next step.
   - No flattery, no fake urgency.

Rules:
- Use ONLY facts present in my notes. Do not invent budgets,
  names, dates, or commitments.
- If something important is missing (e.g. no timeline was
  discussed), say so under that heading rather than guessing.
- Do not state any price or promise; flag where I need to
  confirm one before sending.

Example output

What good looks like

Given a real set of notes, a well-prompted assistant returns something like this — specific, honest about gaps, and ready for your review. Notice what it did not do: it did not invent a budget, quote a price, or promise a deadline.

Discovery summary — Marlowe & Field (bookkeeping firm)

Pains: Onboarding new clients eats two full days per client; owner Priya does it herself and it is the bottleneck on taking more work. Team of four, no documented process.

Budget: Not stated directly. Priya said they "just moved off spreadsheets" — some appetite for tooling spend, but no figure given.

Timeline: Wants something in place before their busy season, which she put "around February." Loose, not committed.

Decision process: Priya decides, but wants her ops lead, Devon, in the next conversation.

Next steps: Me — send a short recap and a suggested scope. Them — Priya to loop in Devon and confirm a time in the next two weeks. (No budget discussed — confirm before sending any pricing.)

Draft follow-up email

Subject: Recap + a next step on onboarding

Hi Priya,

Thanks for walking me through how onboarding works today — the two-days-per-client bottleneck landing entirely on you is exactly the kind of thing worth fixing before February hits.

I'll put together a short scope for documenting and streamlining that process, sized for a team of four. Once you've had a chance to loop in Devon, could we grab thirty minutes in the next couple of weeks with both of you? Happy to work around your calendar.

Talk soon,
[Your name]

Human review

Nothing leaves without a person's eyes on it

The approval step is not a formality. It is the reason this use case builds trust instead of corrupting your pipeline. Before anything sends or saves, a human confirms:

  • Every commitment and price is verified before sending. The model must never state a price or a delivery date on its own. Check any number against your real pricing and your actual capacity — you own what the email promises, not the AI.
  • Nothing is fabricated. If a draft implies a guarantee, a timeline, or a scope you did not agree to, cut it. A confident sentence is not a true one.
  • Names and figures are correct. AI transposes names, mishears companies, and rounds numbers. Read every proper noun and every figure against your notes before it goes anywhere.
  • A human approves every external message. No follow-up, proposal, or reply sends automatically. You read it, edit it, and press send yourself.
  • A human approves every CRM update. Summaries and extracted fields are drafts until you confirm them. A wrong stage or a fabricated next step quietly corrupts your forecast — check before you save.
