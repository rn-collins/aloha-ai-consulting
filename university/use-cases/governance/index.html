Use case

AI for Governance

The fastest way to lose the AI argument inside your company is to have no rules at all. Governance is what lets you say yes to AI with confidence — because everyone knows what is allowed, what gets checked, and who is accountable when it goes wrong.

The idea

What this use case is

AI governance is the small set of rules and habits that make AI use in your business safe, consistent, and defensible. It answers the questions people are already asking quietly: Which tools can I use? What data am I allowed to paste in? When does a human have to check the output? What do we tell a customer who asks whether AI touched their work?

This is the trust-safe angle. Governance is not a brake on AI — it is the seatbelt that lets you drive faster. It turns "we are nervous about AI" into a written standard people can follow without asking permission every time.

Who it helps

Who this is for

Owners and leaders

Who want the productivity of AI without a data leak or a reputational hit.

Regulated and professional firms

Law, accounting, healthcare-adjacent, financial services — where a mistake carries real liability.

Teams already using AI unofficially

Where use is happening in the shadows, and someone needs to make it official and safe.

Anyone facing the question

A client, insurer, or auditor now asks, "What is your AI policy?" and you need an answer.

The problem

The business problem it solves

Without governance, two bad things happen at once. The cautious avoid AI entirely and fall behind. The bold paste confidential data into free consumer tools and create risk nobody can see. Both are failures of the same missing thing: a clear line.

Governance replaces fear and recklessness with a policy. It defines approved tools, forbidden data, mandatory review points, and a disclosure stance — so your team moves fast inside safe boundaries, and you can show a client or regulator exactly how you keep their information protected.

Inputs

What you need going in

An honest inventory of current use

Which tools people are actually using, for what — including the unofficial ones nobody logged.

Your data sensitivity tiers

What counts as confidential, regulated, or client-privileged in your business.

Your real obligations

The contracts, regulations, and professional rules that already constrain how you handle information.

A named owner

One person accountable for the policy, approvals, and answering questions. Not a committee.

Tools

Suggested tools

Business-tier AI accounts

ChatGPT Enterprise or Team, Claude for Work, or Gemini business tiers keep your inputs out of model training and give you admin controls. The tier is a governance decision, not a nice-to-have.

A written AI policy doc

Kept in your wiki or Notion where everyone can find it. Living rules beat a slide deck nobody reopens.

An AI use log

A lightweight record of high-stakes AI-assisted work — what was checked and by whom. Your evidence if anyone ever asks.

The core rule: data-handling terms differ sharply between consumer and business tiers. Confirm in writing that your provider does not train on your inputs before any confidential data goes near a tool. Read the terms; do not assume.

The workflow

Step by step

  1. Inventory reality. Ask the team what AI they already use, without judgment, so the policy fits actual behavior.
  2. Sort your data into tiers: public, internal, confidential, regulated. Decide what may touch AI at each tier.
  3. Pick approved tools and the required account tier for each data level.
  4. Define review gates. Name the outputs a human must verify before use — anything customer-facing, legal, financial, or safety-related.
  5. Set a disclosure stance. Decide when and how you tell clients that AI assisted the work.
  6. Write it in one page, in plain language, and put it where people look.
  7. Review quarterly. Tools and risks change fast; a stale policy is nearly as risky as none.

The prompt

An example prompt

Use AI to draft the first version of your own policy, then let a human finish it:

Help me draft a one-page AI use policy for a [12-person accounting firm].

Base it on these facts:
- Approved tools: [ChatGPT Team, NotebookLM Enterprise].
- Confidential data: client financials, SSNs, anything under our
  engagement letters.
- Hard rule: no client-identifying data in any consumer/free tier.
- Human review required for anything sent to a client or filed with
  an agency.

Structure it as: (1) what is allowed, (2) what is forbidden,
(3) what always needs human review, (4) how we disclose AI use to
clients, (5) who owns this policy.

Write it for a non-technical reader. Flag any spot where I should get
advice from a qualified professional rather than rely on your draft.

What good looks like back: the model returns a workable skeleton — allowed uses tied to approved tools, a hard no on client-identifying data in free accounts, human review on anything that leaves the firm, a disclosure line for the engagement terms — and it marks the exact clause where a real professional has to weigh in on licensing and engagement-letter rules. That flag is governance working as intended.

Guardrails

What a human still owns

A human owns the policy

AI can draft it; a leader decides it and stands behind it.

Verify tool terms yourself

Do not take the model's word for what a provider does with your data. Read the actual terms.

Get qualified advice on obligations

Regulatory, contractual, and professional-conduct rules are for a licensed professional, not a chatbot, to interpret.

Audit occasionally

Spot-check that the policy is being followed, not just written.
