Use case

AI for Research

Market scans, competitor teardowns, long documents boiled down to what matters, interviews turned into themes, and scattered files pulled into one searchable knowledge base. AI does the reading and the first-pass synthesis; you verify every claim, keep the citations honest, and own the conclusions. Used this way it saves days. Used carelessly it invents facts with total confidence — so the whole method here is built to catch that.

What it is

Turning a pile of sources into a defensible brief

Research is one of the clearest places AI earns its keep, because so much of it is reading and re-reading. The work splits into a few recurring jobs, and AI helps with each a little differently.

  • Market and competitor scans. Pull together what a set of competitors claim, price, and position around — from their sites, reviews, and public filings — into a side-by-side you can reason about instead of forty open tabs.
  • Summarizing long documents. A dense report, a contract, a hundred-page transcript: AI produces a faithful summary that points back to the passage it came from, so you can jump to the source and check.
  • Synthesizing interviews. Feed it a batch of customer or user conversations and have it surface where people agreed, where they diverged, and which quotes actually support each pattern.
  • Extracting themes. Across many sources, name the handful of ideas that keep recurring — and, just as usefully, the ones that show up once and should not be mistaken for a trend.
  • Building an internal knowledge base. Take documents scattered across drives, email threads, and old decks and organize them into something searchable, with each answer traceable to the file it came from.

The through-line is citations and verification. Every one of these jobs is only trustworthy if each claim ties back to a real source you can open, and if a person checks the ones that matter before they go anywhere.

Who it helps

Anyone drowning in documents with a deadline

  • Founders doing their own market research. You are sizing a market, scoping competitors, or validating a wedge, and you do not have an analyst. AI gets you a credible first draft in an afternoon instead of a lost week — provided you verify the numbers before they land in a deck.
  • Strategy and consulting teams. You live in secondary research and client documents. Synthesis across many sources is exactly the slow part, and a review-first workflow lets you move faster without lowering the standard your name depends on.
  • Anyone with more reading than hours. Product managers digesting user interviews, operators reading vendor documentation, a small team standing up a knowledge base from years of scattered files. If the bottleneck is reading volume, this helps.
  • Not a fit — and this matters: AI is a drafting and synthesis aid, not a source of truth. If you need a definitive market figure, a legal reading, or a medical or financial fact to be correct, the model's output is a starting point to verify against a primary source, never the answer itself. Do not treat a confident paragraph as a citation.

The problem

Research is slow, so it quietly gets skipped

The honest failure mode is not bad research. It is no research. Doing it properly means reading widely, holding a dozen sources in your head at once, and writing down what they add up to. That takes real hours, and when client work or a launch heats up, it is the first thing to fall off. So decisions get made on a hunch, a competitor move gets missed, and a market assumption goes untested until it is expensive.

The second problem is scatter. What a company already knows is spread across drives, inboxes, old proposals, and a few people's memories. The answer to "have we looked at this before?" is usually yes, somewhere, in a file nobody can find. Reconstructing it from forty browser tabs does not scale, and it does not survive the person who did it leaving.

AI attacks the slow part directly. It reads fast, summarizes faithfully when constrained to the source text, and drafts a synthesis in minutes. That does not make it trustworthy on its own — models fabricate. What it does is shift your time from reading everything to verifying what matters, which is the part only a person can do. The research gets done, and it stays honest, because a human still checks the claims and signs off on the conclusions.

Inputs

Good research out starts with disciplined inputs in

The gap between a grounded brief and confident fiction is almost entirely the inputs. Give the model real text, a sharp question, and a way to check every claim.

The source material itself

The actual documents, transcripts, PDFs, or URLs you want synthesized. Give the model the real text wherever you can — pasted or attached — rather than asking it to recall a document from memory, which is where invention creeps in.

A clear research question

One sentence stating what you are actually trying to learn or decide. "Which three competitors overlap most with our positioning, and where?" produces a useful brief. "Tell me about the market" produces filler.

Criteria for what matters

What counts as relevant, what you can ignore, and what a strong finding versus a weak one looks like. Without this the model weights everything equally and buries the signal.

A way to check sources

Links you can open, page numbers you can turn to, or the original files on hand — so that when the output makes a claim, you can trace it back and confirm it is real. If you cannot verify a source, you cannot use the claim.

Suggested tools

A synthesis assistant, a knowledge home, and a citation habit

You need less than you would think: one strong AI assistant, somewhere to keep what you find, and the discipline to demand a source for every claim. Prices below are current as of mid-2026, change often, and should be confirmed before you buy.

AI assistant for synthesis

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both around $20/month (verify current pricing), for summarizing, comparing, and drafting across your sources. The paid tiers give you the stronger models and the larger context windows that matter when you paste in long documents. Free tiers are fine for testing the workflow first.

A notes / knowledge home

Notion is a common choice for keeping sources, briefs, and a source log in one searchable place. Its full AI features now sit in the Business tier (around $20 per user/month, verify), so price the AI layer separately from basic Notion. Any tool you will actually keep updated beats the fanciest one you abandon.

A citation discipline

Not a product — a rule. Every claim carries its source next to it, and nothing enters the knowledge base without one. This is the cheapest and most important "tool" here, and it is what separates real research from confident-sounding fiction.

The source documents themselves

Your inputs are a tool too. Well-organized, complete source files produce grounded output; a vague prompt with no attached text invites the model to fill gaps with invention. Give it the real material, not a memory test.

The hallucination warning, stated plainly. Language models generate fluent, confident text whether or not it is true. They will invent statistics, misattribute quotes, and — worst for research — fabricate citations that look completely real: plausible author names, journal titles, page numbers, and dates for sources that do not exist. A confident paragraph is not evidence. Treat every factual claim and every citation as unverified until you have opened the actual source and confirmed it. This risk does not go away with a better model; it just gets harder to spot. Build your process so a human always catches it.

Workflow

Frame it, gather it, synthesize it, verify it, store it

  1. Frame the question. Write the one thing you are trying to learn or decide, plus your criteria for what counts as a strong finding. This is your spec, and everything downstream is judged against it.
  2. Gather your sources. Collect the documents, transcripts, and URLs that could answer it. Prefer primary sources. Note where each one came from now — you will need that for the source log later.
  3. Summarize each source with citations. Feed the model one source at a time and ask for a faithful summary that references the specific passage or section each point came from. One source per pass keeps it honest and traceable.
  4. Synthesize across sources. With the individual summaries in hand, have the model name the recurring themes, the points of disagreement, and the outliers — each one tagged to the sources that support it.
  5. Flag the unsupported. Explicitly ask the model to mark anything it cannot ground in the provided text as "needs checking" rather than stating it as fact. This surfaces the gaps instead of hiding them.
  6. Verify every claim against the source. Open the actual documents and confirm each material claim and citation is real and accurately represented. This is the step that cannot be skipped or delegated. Fix or cut anything that does not hold.
  7. Write the conclusions yourself. The model drafts; you decide what it means and what to do. Ownership of the judgment stays with a person.
  8. Store it in the knowledge base. File the verified brief and its source log where your team can find it, so the next person does not repeat the work — and can trace every claim back to where it came from.

Example prompt

A synthesis prompt that refuses to invent

This turns a set of source documents into a themed brief while forcing inline references and an honest "needs checking" flag. Copy it, paste your real sources where marked, and adjust the question.

You are helping me synthesize research. Work ONLY from the
source texts I paste below. Do not use outside knowledge, and
do not invent facts, statistics, quotes, or citations.

My research question:
[one sentence: what I'm trying to learn or decide]

What counts as a strong finding:
[your criteria — e.g. supported by 2+ sources, recent, specific]

SOURCES (each with an ID):
[S1] [paste source 1 text or a clear excerpt]
[S2] [paste source 2 text or a clear excerpt]
[S3] [paste source 3 text or a clear excerpt]

Produce a themed brief:
1. 3-6 themes that answer my question. Under each theme, state
   the finding in one sentence, then list the source IDs that
   support it, like [S1][S3]. Every claim must cite at least
   one source ID.
2. A "Disagreements & outliers" section: where sources
   conflict, or a point appears in only one source.
3. A "Needs verification" section: list any statement, number,
   or quote you are NOT able to ground directly in the pasted
   text. Do not include it in the themes above — flag it here
   instead.

Rules:
- If a claim is not supported by the provided sources, do not
  make it. Say "not addressed in provided sources."
- Never fabricate a statistic, a source, or a citation.
- Quote exact wording when you attribute a quote, and cite its
  source ID.

Example output

What a grounded brief actually looks like

A realistic result from the prompt above, run over three fictional competitor sources for a scheduling-software scan. Note the source tags and the honest verification section — a plausible statistic gets held out until a human checks it.

RESEARCH QUESTION: Where do competitors A, B, and C overlap
with our positioning, and where is there open space?

THEMES

1. All three lead with "ease of setup," leaving depth unclaimed.
   Each competitor's homepage headline emphasizes fast onboarding
   over advanced capability. [S1][S2][S3]

2. Pricing is bundled, not transparent.
   A and C require a demo to see pricing; only B lists a public
   price. This is a differentiation opening for us. [S1][S2][S3]

3. None target regulated industries explicitly.
   No source mentions compliance, audit trails, or healthcare
   or finance use cases. [S1][S2][S3]

DISAGREEMENTS & OUTLIERS
- Competitor B claims a free tier; A and C do not. [S2]
- Only C mentions an API, and only in a footer link. [S3]

NEEDS VERIFICATION
- A "40% faster onboarding" figure appears associated with A,
  but I could not locate it in the provided S1 text. Confirm
  against the original source before using.
- Competitor headcount and funding were NOT in the provided
  sources; do not state them without a checked source.

Human review

Verify everything; a person owns the conclusions

The approval step is not a formality. It is the reason this method produces research you can defend instead of confident fiction. Before a brief goes anywhere, a human confirms:

  • Every factual claim is verified against the actual source. This is non-negotiable. Open the document, find the passage, and confirm the claim is real and fairly represented. Models fabricate citations that look flawless — the right author, a real-sounding journal, a specific page — for sources that do not exist. If you cannot open it, you cannot cite it.
  • Check for cherry-picking. A model asked to find a theme will find one, sometimes by over-weighting a single source or ignoring what cuts against it. Read for what was left out, not just what was included. One quote is not a pattern.
  • Confirm the "needs verification" flags were honest. Do not assume everything outside that section is safe. Spot-check claims inside the themes too, especially any number, date, or direct quote.
  • A human owns the conclusions. The model drafts the synthesis; a person decides what it means, what to trust, and what to do about it. That judgment — and the accountability for it — never transfers to the tool. If a conclusion turns out wrong, "the AI said so" is not a defense you or your client will accept.
  • Remember what this output is not. A research brief is an input to a decision, not professional advice. It is not legal, medical, or financial guidance. For those, verify against primary sources and consult a qualified professional in the relevant field.
