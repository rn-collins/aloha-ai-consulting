Use case

AI for Operations

Turn the process that lives in one person's head into a written SOP. AI drafts the checklists, runbooks, and status summaries nobody has time to write, and the person who owns the work verifies every line before it becomes canonical. The blank page is the bottleneck; correcting a draft is fast. You keep the judgment and the sign-off.

What it is

Making the undocumented documented

Every operating business runs on knowledge nobody wrote down. How you close the books. How a new client gets set up. What "done" means for a shipped order. That knowledge sits in one person's head, gets passed on by demonstration, and disappears the day they take a long vacation or leave.

AI for operations is the practice of using a capable AI assistant to turn that tribal knowledge into usable documentation. You describe or record how a task actually gets done, and the assistant produces a first draft you can correct: a numbered standard operating procedure, a pre-flight checklist, a runbook for a recurring task, or a clean summary of a messy status thread. It standardizes the workflows you keep re-explaining, so the explanation happens once.

The point is not to replace judgment. It is to kill the blank-page problem. Drafting is the part that stalls; correcting a draft is fast. AI handles the slow first draft so a busy operator can do the part only they can do: verify it.

Who it helps

The person the whole operation quietly depends on

  • Operations leads. You know the processes cold, but writing them up keeps losing to the next fire. AI hands you a draft to react to instead of a blank document to dread.
  • COOs of small firms. You are trying to make the business run without you in the room for every decision. Documented process is how you get there, and this is the fastest honest path to it.
  • Founders wearing the ops hat. You do finance, hiring, delivery, and vendor management on the same afternoon. You need repeatable procedures so the next hire can take one of those off your plate cleanly.
  • Not a fit: anyone hoping AI will document a process it has never observed. If you cannot describe or show how the work is done, the assistant will guess, and a confident, wrong SOP is worse than none. This use case needs a real walkthrough as its raw material.

The problem

When a process lives in one head, the business is fragile

Undocumented process creates three predictable costs, and they compound. Onboarding is slow, because every new person learns by interrupting a busy expert and absorbing knowledge in fragments. Quality varies, because two people doing the "same" task each do it their own remembered way, and the customer feels the difference. And the business carries key-person risk: if the one who knows how invoicing works is out sick during close, the wheels wobble.

This stays unsolved not because operators undervalue documentation, but because writing it is genuinely tedious, never urgent until it is a crisis, and always loses to billable or customer-facing work. The documentation nobody has time to write stays unwritten for years.

AI changes the math. When the first draft takes minutes instead of an afternoon, the task stops being something you avoid and becomes something you finish. The procedure gets written, onboarding gets faster, and quality gets consistent, because there is finally a written standard to hold work against.

Inputs

Give the assistant something real to work from

The quality of the SOP depends entirely on the quality of what you feed in. Gather these before you start, and the draft comes back accurate instead of plausible.

A walkthrough of the real task

The best input is a recording. Screen-record yourself doing the task and talking through it, then transcribe. A spoken transcript captures the real steps, including the "oh, and then I always check…" asides that never make it into a tidy write-up.

Existing scattered notes

The half-finished checklist in someone's notes app, the email where you explained the process to a new hire, the sticky note on the monitor. Messy is fine; the assistant consolidates.

The tools involved

Which systems the task touches — the accounting software, the CRM, the shared drive — and roughly where each step happens.

Who owns what

Which role is responsible for the process, who has to approve, and who gets notified. This is what turns a list of steps into an accountable procedure.

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, each around $20/month (verify), for turning walkthroughs into drafts. The free tiers are fine for a first experiment; the paid tiers give you stronger models and higher limits once this becomes a regular habit.

A home for the docs

Somewhere your team already lives — Notion or Google Docs. A note on Notion: its full AI features now sit in the Business tier (roughly $20 per user/month, verify), so if you want AI inside Notion itself, price that in. For simply storing and sharing finished SOPs, the standard plans are plenty.

Automation glue (optional)

Zapier or Make, only once a procedure is stable, to trigger a review reminder or route a new document for sign-off. Skip this at the start. Automate a process after you have documented it, never before.

Honest caveat

Tool prices, plan names, and where AI features live change frequently across every vendor here. Confirm the numbers above before you buy. The cheapest viable setup — one paid AI chat and a doc you already have — is a perfectly good place to begin.

Workflow

From a spoken walkthrough to a living document

  1. Capture the process as it really runs. Do the task once and narrate it. Screen-record or voice-record yourself, then transcribe. Do not clean it up first; the messy version holds the exceptions a polished summary would drop.
  2. Have AI draft the SOP. Paste the transcript and any scattered notes into your assistant with the prompt below. Ask for a numbered procedure with prerequisites, an owner, and common failure points. Expect a solid first draft, not a final one.
  3. The process owner corrects it. Read every step. Fix anything the model reordered, softened, or misunderstood, and delete anything that was never in your walkthrough. This is where accuracy is won.
  4. Add exceptions and a RACI. Layer in the edge cases the happy-path draft missed — what to do when the payment fails, when the client is missing a document — and note who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed.
  5. Publish where the team works. Put the finished SOP in Notion or Google Docs, link it from wherever the task starts, and tell the people who run it that it now exists and is the standard.
  6. Schedule a review. Set a recurring reminder — quarterly is a reasonable default — to check the procedure against how the work is actually done now. Processes drift; an SOP that is never revisited quietly becomes fiction.

Example prompt

Turn a rough walkthrough into a clean SOP

Copy this, paste your transcript where indicated, and adjust the labels to your business. The two rules at the bottom matter most: they stop the model from smoothing a real process into a plausible-but-wrong one, and they surface the gaps for you to close rather than hiding them.

You are helping me turn a rough spoken walkthrough into a clean
standard operating procedure. Below is a transcript of me talking
through how I actually do this task. It is messy, out of order in
places, and full of asides.

Task name: [e.g. Client offboarding]
Who owns this process: [role/title]
Tools involved: [systems this task touches]

Transcript:
[paste the raw transcript]

Produce an SOP with these sections:
1. Purpose — one sentence on what this procedure achieves.
2. Prerequisites — what must be true or ready before starting.
3. Steps — a clear numbered list, in the correct order, each step
   a single concrete action.
4. Owner — the role responsible, plus who approves if approval is
   mentioned.
5. Common failure points — the mistakes or edge cases I mentioned,
   and what to do about each.

Hard rules:
- Only include steps that appear in my transcript. Do NOT invent
  steps, tools, or approvals I did not mention.
- If a step is unclear or seems to be missing, do not fill the gap
  yourself — list it under "Questions for the owner" instead.
- Keep my terminology and tool names exactly as I said them.

Example output

What a first draft actually looks like

A realistic excerpt from a client-offboarding SOP produced from a transcript. Notice it is specific, it names the owner, and it flags a question rather than guessing.

SOP: Client offboarding (draft — pending owner review)

Purpose: Close out a completed client engagement cleanly so nothing is left owed, exposed, or forgotten.

Prerequisites: Final invoice sent and paid; engagement marked "Complete" in the CRM.

Steps:

  1. Confirm the final invoice shows Paid in QuickBooks. If it shows Overdue, stop and route to the finance owner before continuing.
  2. Export the client's active files from the shared drive into the "Archived Clients" folder, then remove their edit access.
  3. Revoke the client's logins to any shared tools (project board, staging site) and note the date in the CRM.
  4. Send the offboarding email from the saved template: final deliverables recap, where their assets now live, and the support-window end date.
  5. Schedule the 30-day check-in and tag the account "Offboarded — nurture."

Owner: Account manager. Finance owner approves any outstanding-balance exception.

Common failure points: Access left active after handoff; offboarding email sent before final payment clears.

Questions for the owner: You mentioned a "handover doc" in passing but did not say what goes in it or who writes it — please confirm before this is finalized.

Human review

The owner signs off, or it is not an SOP yet

A drafted procedure is a proposal, not a policy. Before it becomes the canonical way the team does something, the person who owns the process verifies it line by line. There is no shortcut, and skipping it is how a wrong instruction becomes an institutionalized mistake.

  • Verify accuracy. The owner reads every step against how the work is truly done and corrects order, wording, and detail. The transcript may have missed something; the model may have smoothed something.
  • Check the edge cases. Confirm the failure points and exceptions are complete and correct. A happy-path SOP that ignores what happens when things break is the one that fails you at the worst moment.
  • Catch anything invented. Read specifically for steps, tools, approvals, or numbers the model added that were never in your input. If it is not something you actually do, cut it.
  • Sign off explicitly. Record who approved it and when, right in the document. That sign-off is what makes it canonical — the standard the team is now accountable to, not just a draft floating in a folder.
