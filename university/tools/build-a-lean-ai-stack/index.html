Tool guide

How to build a lean AI stack

The trap is not too few AI tools — it is too many, half-used, quietly billing. A lean stack is the smallest set that clears your real bottlenecks. Here is a framework to build one, and a starter stack that works for most.

Every week a new tool promises to change everything. Chasing them leaves you with a drawer of subscriptions and no clear workflow. A lean stack flips the question: instead of "what can this tool do," ask "what specific work is eating my week, and what is the fewest tools that removes it." This page gives you a five-question framework, then a concrete starter stack you can adapt.

The framework

Five questions before you add any tool

1 · What is the actual bottleneck?

Name the specific work that costs the most time or friction. "We spend hours retyping data between apps" is a bottleneck. "We should use more AI" is not.

2 · Can a tool I already pay for do this?

Before buying, check whether your general assistant or existing software already covers it. Most teams underuse the tools they have.

3 · Does the value clearly beat total cost?

Total cost is subscription plus setup time plus the ongoing attention to keep it working. A cheap tool nobody maintains is expensive.

4 · Is the data safe to route through it?

Check training use, retention, and access scope. If the tool touches customer, financial, or confidential data, that answer gates everything else.

5 · Who owns it, and when do we review it?

Every tool needs an owner and a review date. A stack without maintenance decays into subscriptions nobody remembers.

If a candidate tool cannot clear all five, it is not lean — it is clutter.

Where the framework helps

Best use cases

  • Solo owners and lean teams who need leverage without a tools budget that balloons.
  • Teams drowning in subscriptions who want to consolidate to what actually earns its seat.
  • Anyone starting out who wants to begin with three or four tools instead of guessing among fifty.
  • Businesses with real data-sensitivity who need privacy to gate the stack, not follow it as an afterthought.

The starter stack

Strengths of a lean stack

For most small businesses, this four-part stack covers the majority of real needs. Substitute freely — the shape matters more than the specific brands.

LayerTypical pickWhat it does
1. One general assistantChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini (~$20/mo)Your default for drafting, analysis, summarizing, and reshaping content. Replaces the need for many niche tools.
2. One knowledge homeNotion, or the docs tool you already useWhere SOPs, decisions, and notes live so answers are searchable in one place.
3. One automation layerZapier or MakeAdded only once you have identified a repetitive workflow worth automating.
4. One specialistNote-taker, research, or clip toolChosen for your single biggest job — not one of each.

The strength of this shape is restraint: four tools you use daily beat a dozen you touch monthly, and the whole stack stays cheap enough to justify and small enough to maintain.

Where lean stacks go wrong

Limitations

Under-tooling is real too

  • If a genuine bottleneck needs a specialist, skipping it to stay "lean" just moves the cost to your time.
  • A single general assistant will not do everything well; know when a job truly needs a purpose-built tool.

Lock-in and upkeep

  • Consolidating onto one vendor creates lock-in. Keep your data exportable and avoid deep dependence on any one platform.
  • A lean stack still needs upkeep. Automations break, plans change, and unused seats accumulate if nobody reviews them.

Bake it in from the start

Privacy considerations

  • Prefer business tiers of the general assistants, which are likelier to exclude your inputs from model training by default. Confirm the setting.
  • Map your data before connecting tools: know which tool touches customer, financial, or confidential information, and apply stricter controls there.
  • Grant least privilege. Every connection should have the narrowest access that does the job, reviewed periodically.
  • Keep a signed agreement in place for any tool handling regulated data, and keep regulated data out of tools that cannot offer one.

Budgeting the stack

Pricing considerations

  • A lean starter stack often lands near $20–$60/month per person: one assistant around $20, an optional knowledge add-on around $10, and one specialist as needed. Confirm each on the vendor page.
  • Automation platforms usually start free and scale with volume — add cost only when the workflow justifies it.
  • The biggest hidden cost is forgotten seats. Put a quarterly audit on the calendar and cancel what nobody opens.
  • Annual billing usually discounts the major assistants, but commit annually only once daily use is proven.

How to roll it out

Workflow fit

  • Adopt the assistant first. Use it daily for a week before anything else. The habit is the foundation.
  • Centralize knowledge. Move SOPs and key docs into one searchable home.
  • Automate one workflow. Pick your worst repetitive task and automate just that, tested with real data.
  • Add one specialist. Solve your single biggest remaining job, then stop and let the stack settle.
  • Review quarterly. Keep what earns its seat, cut what does not, and only then consider adding.

Other paths

Alternatives & questions

An all-in-one suite

Microsoft 365 Copilot or Google Workspace with Gemini, if your business already lives in one ecosystem — integration can beat assembling best-of-breed pieces.

A single super-tool

For very small operations that need only one capable assistant and nothing else.

A guided roadmap

If you would rather answer a few questions and get a stack matched to your goals than build the framework yourself.

What if choosing feels overwhelming?

A lean stack is a decision, not a purchase. The discipline is saying no to good tools that do not clear a real bottleneck. A short call can narrow fifty options down to your four.
