Playbook · Growth

AI lead generation that earns the reply

A fit-first system that turns your ideal-client definition into a short list of real people worth talking to — enriched with genuine context and reached with a message you would be glad to receive. No scraped-then-blasted lists. No mail-merge pretending to be a relationship.

01 · The business problem

You need the right conversations, not more noise

Agencies, consultancies, fractional operators, boutique firms — they live or die by pipeline. But the two usual ways of building it both break. Referrals are wonderful and completely unpredictable; you cannot forecast a quarter on hope. Bought lists blasted through a cold-email tool feel efficient right up until your domain reputation tanks, your reply rate sits at half a percent, and the few replies you get are annoyed.

The real problem is not volume. It is that finding people who genuinely fit your offer, learning enough to say something true, and doing that every week is slow manual work — the kind that gets dropped the moment client work heats up. So pipeline arrives in feast-or-famine waves, and the famine always shows up at the worst time.

AI does not fix this by letting you send more. It fixes it by collapsing the hours between "here is my ideal client" and "here is a warm, specific first message to a person who matches" — so the work actually gets done, week after week, at a quality you would put your name on.

02 · Why it matters

Consistency compounds; spikes evaporate

A predictable trickle of qualified conversations changes how you run everything else. You price with confidence instead of discounting out of fear. You turn down bad-fit work because another conversation is already booked. You plan hiring against a number you trust rather than a feeling.

The economics are stark. If sourcing and personalization take forty-five minutes per prospect done well, you will realistically touch a handful of people a week and then stop. Compress that to five minutes of review over an AI draft, and the same person reaches thirty or forty fitting prospects a week without lowering the bar. That is the difference between a channel that works and one that stays a good intention.

There is a reputational dividend too. Outreach that clearly understands the recipient earns replies even when the answer is "not now" — and "not now, but stay in touch" is a pipeline asset. Spray-and-pray earns spam complaints, which quietly poison every future email from that domain.

03 · Who this is for

Owners and small teams who sell expertise

  • The founder-seller. You are the person who closes, and prospecting is what falls off the calendar first. You want a review-ready short list you can act on in a coffee break.
  • The small agency or consultancy (2–20 people). You have a clear ideal-client profile but no full-time SDR, and you want outreach that reads like a senior person wrote it, because one effectively did.
  • The fractional executive or specialist. Your market is narrow and named — a few hundred companies, not a few million — so precision beats volume every time, and generic lists are useless to you.
  • Not a fit: high-volume transactional B2C, or anyone hoping to send ten thousand identical emails a day. This is built for quality and defensibility, not blast radius.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From a definition to a warm first message, repeatably

By the end of a build, you have a loop that runs on a schedule you choose:

  • Define a precise ideal-client profile (ICP) and disqualifiers, written down so the system and any teammate apply the same standard.
  • Source companies and named contacts that match, from a real data provider rather than guesswork.
  • Enrich each one with genuine context — recent news, role, a plausible trigger — so there is something true to say.
  • Qualify against your disqualifiers so bad-fit names never reach the outreach step.
  • Draft a personalized first message and one follow-up, in your voice, that a human reviews before anything sends.
  • Track what happens so you learn which segments and angles actually convert.

A human always approves before a message goes out. The AI does the ninety percent that is research and drafting; you keep the ten percent that is judgment.

05 · Tools you will need

Pick a tier and start; you can grow into more

You do not need all of these. The manual version needs one AI chat tool and a spreadsheet. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm before you buy.

AI assistant required

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both about $20/month, for research synthesis, qualification, and drafting. Free tiers work for testing; paid tiers give you the stronger models and higher limits you will want once this is real.

A place to keep the list

Google Sheets or Airtable (free tiers are fine to start) as your working pipeline: one row per prospect, columns for fit signals, status, and the drafted message.

Data / enrichment source

Apollo.io is a common choice; paid plans run roughly $49/user/month (Basic) to $79 (Professional) on annual billing, and the credit system — not the sticker price — is the real cost driver, so watch usage. LinkedIn Sales Navigator (about $99/month) is an alternative for people-first sourcing.

Sending + sequencing

Your normal email plus a light sequencing layer. Apollo includes sequencing; a standalone tool works too. Keep daily volume low and human-approved — deliverability is a reputation you are renting, not a dial to crank.

Automation glue optional

Zapier or Make to move rows between steps. Zapier's Professional plan starts around $19.99/month annual for 750 tasks; a multi-step Zap burns one task per step per run, so budget by steps, not runs.

Guardrail note

Whatever you use, respect anti-spam law and platform terms: honor unsubscribes, identify yourself, and do not scrape where terms forbid it. This is not legal advice — see the risks section.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same loop, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — doing it by hand teaches you what "good" looks like, and that judgment is what you encode later.

Manual

One chat, one sheet, forty-five focused minutes

Write your ICP with the prompt below. Pull ten fits by hand. Research each in the assistant, draft in your voice, read every word, and send a few a day from your own inbox.

Honest expectation: the first pass is slow. That is the tuition you pay to build the automated version well.

Semi-automated

The tools source; you review at two gates

Save your ICP as a search in Apollo or Sales Navigator. Export a batch of 25–50 with enrichment. The AI runs a qualification pass (gate one) and batch-drafts messages. You approve and send (gate two).

Where most service businesses should live for a while: fast enough to be consistent, slow enough to stay excellent.

Fully implemented

A scheduled loop with a mandatory approval gate

A weekly schedule pulls new matches, dedupes, qualifies, and drafts into a database. The batch lands in a "Needs review" view and pings you. Nothing sends until you flip a status to Approved.

"Fully implemented" does not mean "sends by itself." It means everything except the judgment is automated.

Reality check: the full build has real maintenance. Enrichment data drifts, prompts need re-tuning, and a broken Zap fails silently. Budget a few hours a month, and never remove the approval gate to "save time." The gate is the whole point.

07 · Step-by-step build

From zero to the semi-automated loop

  1. Write and store the ICP. Run the ICP prompt. Paste the result into a doc titled "ICP + Disqualifiers" and treat it as version-controlled: date it, update it when a segment converts unusually well or badly.
  2. Build the pipeline sheet. Columns: Company, Contact, Title, Link, Fit signals, Trigger, Disqualifier check, Draft message, Draft follow-up, Status (New / Reviewed / Approved / Sent / Replied / Meeting), Notes.
  3. Configure your data source. Translate the ICP into filters in Apollo or Sales Navigator and save the search. Confirm your export/credit allowance before batching, since credits expire per cycle.
  4. Export a starter batch. Pull 25 matches with available enrichment. Spot-check five rows by hand — is the data actually right? Bad inputs make confident, wrong drafts.
  5. Run the qualification pass. Review the flags, delete the true fails, and note patterns (a disqualifier you keep hitting may belong in your saved-search filters).
  6. Draft with your voice guide. Paste two or three emails you have actually sent as a voice sample, then run the personalization prompt per row. Edit until it sounds like you, not a tool.
  7. Approve and send. Read every draft. Send a small daily batch from a warmed inbox, log outcomes, and only scale volume once replies look healthy.
  8. Add automation last. Once the manual loop is smooth, wire the repetitive moves (export → dedupe → draft → review view) in Zapier or Make, keeping the approval gate as a hard stop. Automate what you have validated, never the judgment.

08 · Prompts and templates

Copy these, then make them yours

ICP + disqualifiers

You are helping me define an ideal client profile for my service business.

My business: [what you do, in one sentence]
My best past clients: [2-3 examples and why they were great]
My worst-fit clients: [1-2 examples and what went wrong]

Produce:
1. A tight ICP: industry, company size, revenue band, and the
   buyer role who signs off.
2. A "why now" trigger list: 4-6 observable signals that a
   company is likely ready for what I offer (e.g. new funding,
   a specific hire, a public initiative).
3. Hard disqualifiers: 4-6 signals that mean I should NOT reach
   out, even if other things match.
Format as three labeled lists. Be specific and observable, not
aspirational. Ask me one clarifying question if my inputs are thin.

Qualification pass

Here is my ICP and my hard disqualifiers:
[paste ICP + disqualifiers]

Below is a batch of prospects with enrichment data:
[paste rows: company, title, headcount, notes]

For each row, output: KEEP or CUT, and one short reason tied to
a specific ICP criterion or disqualifier. If a row lacks the
data to judge, mark NEEDS-CHECK rather than guessing. Do not
invent facts about any company.

Personalized outreach draft

Write a first-touch email and one follow-up.

About me / my offer: [one or two sentences]
My voice — match this tone exactly:
[paste 2-3 real emails you've sent]

The prospect:
Company: [name]  Contact: [name, title]
Genuine context I found: [the real trigger or detail]

Rules:
- Under 90 words for the first email. One clear, low-friction ask.
- Open with the specific context, not "I hope this finds you well."
- No flattery, no fake urgency, no "quick question" subject lines.
- Only reference facts I gave you. If context is weak, say so and
  suggest what I should find before sending.
- Follow-up: 2-3 sentences, adds a new angle, easy to ignore.

Reply triage (optional)

Classify this reply as: Interested / Not now / Not a fit /
Referral / Unsubscribe. Then draft a two-sentence response that
matches the category. Reply text:
[paste]

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

Fabricated context

The biggest failure mode: the model invents a "recent funding round" that never happened. The prompts forbid invention, and your approval gate is where you verify claims before they embarrass you.

Deliverability collapse

Volume without warming, honest unsubscribes, and real personalization will burn your domain. Keep daily sends low, authenticate your domain (SPF/DKIM/DMARC), and never send cold at scale from your primary domain.

Legal and platform compliance

Anti-spam rules (such as CAN-SPAM in the US and similar regimes elsewhere), data-protection law, and platform terms all constrain what you may collect and send. This is educational, not legal advice; RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. Get qualified counsel for your jurisdiction and industry.

Sameness at scale

If every message uses the same skeleton, personalization becomes theater. Review for variety, and rotate openings and angles.

Bad data in, confident junk out

Enrichment providers are often wrong. Spot-check, and treat every field as a claim to verify, not a fact.

Review cadence

Read a sample of sent messages weekly, revisit your ICP monthly against who actually replied, and audit disqualifier hits quarterly.
