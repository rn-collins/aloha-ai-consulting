Playbook · Client Work

Onboard clients like a firm that has its act together

The first two weeks decide how a client judges everything after. This playbook collapses the hours between "client signed" and "client feels taken care of" — intake to structured profile, kickoff prep, a welcome sequence, document collection, and a personalized onboarding plan. A person still approves every client-facing word.

01 · The business problem

The handoff after "yes" is where good firms quietly lose ground

A client signs. Then momentum stalls. Someone has to send the welcome email, collect documents and logins, schedule the kickoff, write up notes, and turn a signed proposal into an actual plan of work. For agencies, consultants, bookkeepers and accountants, coaches, and the many firms doing legal-adjacent work without being law firms, this is where things go wrong.

The intake questionnaire comes back half-filled and lives in an inbox. The kickoff runs long because nobody had time to prep. Assets trickle in over three weeks of reminders. Expectations get set on a call, then remembered differently a month later. None of this is a talent problem. Onboarding is repetitive, detail-heavy work that always competes with billable delivery, and delivery wins.

AI does not fix this by replacing the relationship. It reads the intake form into a structured profile, drafts the welcome sequence, preps the kickoff, and generates a personalized onboarding plan you edit rather than write from scratch. The client experiences a firm that is organized — because now you are.

02 · Why it matters

A calm start protects revenue, referrals, and reputation

The onboarding window sets the tone a client uses to judge everything afterward. A confident, organized first two weeks builds trust that survives the rough patch later. A chaotic start — missed logins, an improvised kickoff, an expectation nobody wrote down — plants doubt that resurfaces months later as slow payment, scope arguments, or a quiet non-renewal. You rarely get a second chance to feel dependable.

The economics are concrete. If assembling a profile, prepping a kickoff, and writing an onboarding plan takes three or four hours of senior time per client done well, that work gets rushed or skipped exactly when you are busiest winning clients. Compress it to thirty minutes reviewing AI-drafted material and the standard holds even in your worst week. Every client should get the good version, not just the ones who signed during a slow month.

There is a retention dividend too. Clients who feel oriented and clear about what happens next ask fewer anxious questions, escalate less, and refer more. Onboarding is not overhead. It is the first delivery you make, and the one that decides whether the client believes the rest of your promises.

03 · Who this is for

Service businesses that sell expertise and repeat the same handoff

  • The solo consultant or fractional operator. You do the selling and the delivery, so onboarding gets crammed into the gap between calls. You want a profile, a kickoff brief, and a plan generated from the intake form so you show up prepared instead of scrambling.
  • The small agency (2–20 people). You have an intake process, but it lives in one person's head and varies by who runs it. You want a repeatable system that produces a consistent, high-quality start regardless of who is on point that week.
  • Accountants, bookkeepers, and legal-adjacent firms. Your onboarding involves sensitive documents, engagement scope, and precise expectations, so structure and a clear record matter. Standardize intake and paperwork collection while keeping a human on every judgment call.
  • Coaches and boutique practices. The relationship is the product, and a warm, personalized welcome is part of the value. Let AI handle the assembly so your attention goes to the human part.
  • Not a fit: anyone hoping to fully automate client-facing onboarding with no human review. The point is to remove the busywork, not the person. Send everything unsupervised and you have automated away the trust you were building.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From a signed proposal to a client who feels taken care of

By the end of a build, you have a repeatable onboarding loop that runs the same way for every new client:

  • Intake. One well-designed form captures the facts you need once, so you never ask the same question twice across three emails.
  • Structured profile. AI reads the raw intake answers into a clean, consistent profile — goals, scope, stakeholders, constraints, and open questions — that any teammate can pick up.
  • Kickoff prep. A kickoff brief and draft agenda generated from the profile, so whoever runs the call walks in informed rather than reading the form live.
  • Welcome sequence. Drafted onboarding messages — welcome, what-happens-next, document requests, kickoff confirmation — in your voice, for a human to approve and send.
  • Document and asset collection. A trackable checklist of what you need, with requests drafted and receipts logged so nothing sits half-collected.
  • Personalized onboarding plan. A tailored two-to-four-week plan with milestones, owners, and expectation-setting that you edit and share, turning a signed scope into a visible path.

Every client-facing message and the final plan pass through a person before the client sees them. The AI does the assembly and drafting; you keep the judgment, the tone, and the accountability.

05 · Tools you will need

Pick a tier and start; you can grow into more

You do not need all of these. The manual version needs one AI chat tool, a form, and a document to hold the profile. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm before you buy.

AI assistant required

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both about $20/month, for turning intake answers into a profile, prepping the kickoff, and drafting the welcome sequence and onboarding plan. Free tiers are fine for testing; paid tiers give you stronger models and higher limits once this is a real part of your week.

An intake form

Typeform, Tally, or Google Forms to capture client answers in one structured place. Google Forms and Tally have genuinely usable free tiers; Typeform is more polished but paid. Whatever you pick, the value is one clean set of fields feeding everything downstream.

A home for the profile and plan

Notion is a common choice — the Business tier runs about $20/user/month, and AI is now bundled into that tier rather than sold as the old separate add-on that was removed. Google Docs plus a spreadsheet works perfectly well too and costs nothing extra.

Document collection and e-sign

A storage or e-sign tool to gather sensitive files and engagement paperwork — a shared drive folder, or a light e-sign product for the engagement letter. Keep client documents somewhere access-controlled, not scattered across email attachments.

Automation glue optional

Zapier or Make to move a form submission into your profile doc and kick off the drafts. Zapier's Professional plan starts around $19.99/month annual for 750 tasks; a multi-step Zap burns one task per step per run, so budget by steps, not runs.

Guardrail note

You will handle client personal and business information, so treat data handling seriously: access controls, retention limits, and care about what you paste into AI tools. This is educational, not legal advice — see the risks section before you wire anything to sensitive documents.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same loop, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — running onboarding by hand teaches you what a good profile and plan actually contain, and that judgment is what you encode later.

Manual

One form, one AI chat, one profile document

Build a single intake form and send it the moment a client signs. Paste the answers into your assistant to get a structured profile, prep the kickoff, draft the welcome sequence in your voice, and generate the plan. Read every word before it goes out.

Honest expectation: the first client is slow, because you are still deciding what "good" looks like. That time is the tuition you pay to build the automated version well.

Semi-automated

The form and AI assemble; you review at two gates

The intake form feeds a profile automatically and the AI drafts everything. You keep two checkpoints: verify the profile against the raw answers (gate one), then read, edit, and approve each client-facing message and the plan before anything sends (gate two).

Where most service businesses should live for a good while: fast enough to be consistent across every client, slow enough to stay warm and correct.

Fully implemented

A triggered loop with a mandatory approval gate

A signed proposal or submitted intake form kicks off the loop. It assembles the profile, drafts the welcome sequence, checklist, kickoff brief, and plan, and sets up a secure collection folder — then lands the package in a "Needs review" view. Nothing reaches the client until you flip the status to Approved.

"Fully implemented" does not mean "onboards clients by itself." It means everything except the judgment and the human warmth is automated.

Reality check: the full build has real maintenance. Intake forms change, prompts need re-tuning as your services evolve, and a broken Zap fails silently the week you are too busy to notice. Budget a few hours a month, and never remove the approval gate to "save time." A client who receives a hallucinated commitment because a bot sent it unsupervised is a client you have damaged. Automate the assembly, never the accountability.

07 · Step-by-step build

From zero to the semi-automated loop

  1. Design the intake form. List every question you currently ask across scattered emails and calls, then group them into one form: goals and success criteria, scope and deliverables, key contacts and decision-makers, deadlines, existing assets, access and logins needed, and service-specific fields. Keep it as short as it can be while capturing what you actually use.
  2. Build the profile template. Create a document or Notion template with fixed sections: Client & contacts, Goals & success criteria, Scope & out-of-scope, Constraints & risks, Assets received, Open questions, and Onboarding status (Intake sent / Profile ready / Kickoff done / Plan shared / Onboarded). A consistent shape is what makes the system repeatable.
  3. Save the prompts. Keep the four prompts below somewhere reusable, dated, and easy to paste. Treat them as version-controlled: when an onboarding goes unusually smoothly or badly, update the relevant prompt to encode what you learned.
  4. Run one client fully by hand. Take your next real client through the whole manual loop — intake, profile, kickoff brief, welcome sequence, plan. Note every place the AI got something wrong or you rewrote heavily. Those notes are your tuning list.
  5. Generate and verify the profile. Run the completed intake through the profile prompt. Read the result against the raw answers line by line. Confirm the model did not invent scope, deadlines, or commitments the client never made. Answer the open questions before moving on.
  6. Draft the sequence and plan with your voice. Paste two or three real onboarding emails you have sent as a voice sample, then run the welcome-sequence and onboarding-plan prompts from the verified profile. Edit until it sounds like your firm, not a tool.
  7. Approve, send, and collect. Read every client-facing draft, send the welcome sequence, and set up the document checklist and a secure folder. Log what arrives. Share the plan so expectations are written down.
  8. Add automation last. Once the manual loop is smooth and the prompts are tuned, wire the repetitive moves — form submission creates the profile, AI drafts the package into a review view — in Zapier or Make, keeping the approval gate as a hard stop. Automate what you have validated, never the judgment or the tone.

08 · Prompts and templates

Copy these, then make them yours

Intake to structured profile

You are helping me turn a new client's intake answers into a
clean, structured client profile for my service business.

My business: [what you do, in one sentence]
The service this client bought: [engagement type / scope]

Raw intake answers:
[paste the client's completed intake form responses]

Produce a profile with these labeled sections:
1. Client & key contacts (names, roles, decision-maker)
2. Goals & success criteria (what "done well" looks like)
3. Scope & explicit out-of-scope
4. Constraints, deadlines, and risks
5. Assets/access provided vs. still needed
6. Open questions — anything the intake did not answer or
   answered ambiguously, phrased so I can ask the client.

Only use facts present in the answers. Do NOT invent scope,
deadlines, or commitments. If something is unclear, put it in
Open questions rather than guessing.

Kickoff brief generator

Using the client profile below, write a one-page kickoff brief
and a draft agenda for a 45-minute kickoff call.

Client profile:
[paste the verified profile]

Produce:
1. A 4-6 line summary someone could read right before the call
   to sound fully prepared.
2. The three most important things to confirm or align on.
3. A time-boxed agenda (intros, goals, scope confirmation,
   logistics, next steps).
4. 3-5 smart questions to ask, based on the open questions and
   any risks in the profile.

Keep it practical and specific to this client. Do not add
generic filler. Flag anything in the profile that looks
unresolved before the call.

Personalized onboarding-plan generator

Create a personalized onboarding plan for this client covering
the first [2-4] weeks.

Client profile:
[paste the verified profile]
My standard onboarding steps (adapt these):
[paste your usual steps, or leave blank to have them proposed]

Produce a week-by-week plan with, for each item:
- The action, the owner (me / client / shared), and the due
  point relative to kickoff.
- A short milestone or check-in at the end of each week.
- An "expectations" note per week: what the client should
  expect from us and what we need from them.

Only commit to things supported by the scope in the profile.
If the plan implies work outside the stated scope, flag it
instead of including it. Keep the tone warm and clear.

Welcome / onboarding sequence (optional)

Draft a short onboarding message sequence for this client.

My voice — match this tone exactly:
[paste 2-3 real onboarding emails I've sent]
Client profile:
[paste the verified profile]

Write four short messages:
1. Welcome + what happens next.
2. Document & access request (list exactly what we need,
   based only on the profile's "still needed" items).
3. Kickoff confirmation with the agenda highlights.
4. A day-3 check-in offering help.

Rules: no fake urgency, no filler, no commitments beyond the
scope in the profile. If a needed detail is missing, tell me
what to fill in rather than inventing it.

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

Hallucinated commitments

The most dangerous failure mode: the model writes a deadline, deliverable, or promise the client never agreed to, and it looks confident enough to send. The prompts forbid inventing scope, and your approval gate is where you verify every commitment against the signed agreement before a client sees it.

Client data handling and PII

Intake forms and onboarding documents contain personal and sensitive business information. Store it with access controls and a retention limit, be deliberate about what you paste into AI tools and which tools you trust, and check whether your provider's terms and your client agreements allow it. Do not route confidential documents through automations you have not vetted.

Legal, financial, and compliance limits

Engagement scope, data-protection rules, and industry-specific obligations constrain how you collect, store, and act on client information. This playbook is educational, not legal, medical, or financial advice; RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. Get qualified counsel for your jurisdiction and industry before relying on any of this for regulated work.

Over-automation losing the human touch

Onboarding is where the relationship is built. If a client receives a sequence that is obviously bot-assembled and unsupervised, you have automated away the exact warmth you were selling. Keep a person on the tone, and personalize beyond the template.

Garbage intake in, confident junk out

A half-filled or misread form produces a wrong profile that poisons everything downstream. Verify the profile against the raw answers, and resolve open questions before drafting.

Review cadence

Read every client-facing message before it sends during onboarding, review the profile against source answers for each new client, and audit your intake form and prompts quarterly against where onboarding actually snagged.
