Playbook · Governance

The AI policy that makes everything else defensible

Your team is already using AI — you just cannot see how. This is the trust layer underneath every other playbook: the minimum viable internal policy a small team can actually keep. A one-page use policy, a clear line between approved and prohibited uses, data-handling rules, a human-review gate before anything reaches a client or regulator, and an audit trail you can maintain without a compliance department. Enough structure to protect you, light enough that people follow it. Educational, not legal advice.

01 · The business problem

Your team is already using AI; you just cannot see how

Here is the uncomfortable truth about most small businesses in 2026: AI adoption already happened, and nobody signed off on it. Someone in accounts pastes a client contract into a free chatbot to summarize the tricky clause. A junior staffer drops a spreadsheet of customer emails into a consumer tool to draft a mailer. A contractor runs a patient intake note or a candidate's résumé through whatever app they had open. None of it is malicious. All of it is invisible to you.

This is shadow AI: real work getting done through unsanctioned tools, on unknown terms, with sensitive data you are contractually and sometimes legally responsible for. The moment client PII, a signed NDA, source code, or a trade secret lands in a consumer tool's input box, you may have created a disclosure you cannot take back — and you will not know it happened until a client asks how their data is handled and you have no answer.

Governance is not about slowing your team down. It makes the AI use that is already happening visible, safe, and defensible — so you can say yes to more of it with a straight face, instead of pretending it is not going on.

02 · Why it matters

Trust is the thing you are actually selling

Every other AI playbook — lead generation, client deliverables, operations — assumes you can put AI output in front of a client without embarrassing yourself or breaching an agreement. Governance is what makes that assumption true. It is the layer underneath everything else, and skipping it does not make you faster; it makes you exposed.

Start with your contracts. Many client agreements, especially in professional services, healthcare, and finance, include confidentiality and data-handling terms written before anyone imagined a staffer pasting the deliverable into a public model. A clear internal policy — sanctioned tools only, no client data in consumer tiers, a review gate before anything ships — is often the difference between honoring those terms and quietly breaching them.

Then there is risk reduction that pays for itself. A single leaked dataset or a fabricated figure that reaches a regulator can cost more than a year of careful process. Governance lowers the odds and, just as importantly, gives you a record showing you took reasonable care — which matters enormously when something does go wrong.

The counterintuitive part: good governance enables adoption, it does not block it. When people know exactly which tools are safe and where the guardrails are, they stop hiding their AI use and start doing more of it openly. A policy that says "here is how to use AI well" gets far more real work done than a nervous, unwritten "please be careful."

03 · Who this is for

Small teams handling other people's trust

  • The owner-operator (1–20 people). You have no legal or compliance staff, but you have clients who assume you are handling their information responsibly. You want the smallest policy that actually protects you, not a fifty-page document nobody opens.
  • The professional-services firm. Agencies, consultancies, bookkeepers, clinics, boutique law-adjacent and advisory shops — anyone whose contracts include confidentiality terms and whose reputation rests on discretion.
  • The team already deep into AI. You leaned into AI for real output and now realize you never wrote down the rules. This is the retrofit: getting the guardrails in place before an incident forces the issue.
  • Not a fit: large enterprises with existing compliance functions and legal teams — you need something heavier and jurisdiction-specific. And this is not a substitute for qualified counsel in regulated industries; it is a starting point that helps you have a smarter conversation with the professionals you do hire.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From invisible risk to a written, followable standard

By the end of a build, you have a lightweight governance system that a small team can actually maintain:

  • A one-page AI use policy in plain language, so every person on the team applies the same standard instead of their own judgment call.
  • An approved-and-prohibited list: named uses that are fine, named uses that are banned, and a simple path for classifying anything in between.
  • Data-handling rules everyone can recite: never paste client PII, secrets, or confidential material into consumer tools, and a short list of sanctioned tools cleared for real work.
  • A mandatory human-review gate for anything that goes to a client, a regulator, or the public — nothing AI-assisted ships without a named person checking it.
  • An audit trail that records what AI produced, who reviewed it, and when, so you can reconstruct any decision later.
  • Disclosure norms and a review cadence so the policy stays honest with clients and keeps up with a fast-moving field.

Crucially, none of this is a tool you buy. It is people and process, lightly supported by a shared document and a simple log. The AI can help you draft and triage; the judgment, the enforcement, and the ownership stay human.

05 · Tools you will need

Mostly a document and a log, not a purchase

Governance is people plus process, not software. The core of this playbook is a shared doc and a simple log. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — and tool terms change even faster, so verify current terms before you rely on them.

Shared policy document required

Notion, Google Docs, or any shared doc your whole team can reach. This holds the one-page policy, the approved/prohibited list, and the data-handling rules. Free tiers are fine. The requirement is not the tool — it is that the policy is written down, findable, and versioned.

Audit log required

A Google Sheet or a Notion database with one row per client-facing AI-assisted output: what was produced, which tool, who reviewed it, when, and the outcome. That is the entire audit trail. Do not over-engineer it; a log people actually fill in beats a perfect one they abandon.

Sanctioned AI tools

Enterprise and team tiers generally keep your inputs out of model training by default — ChatGPT Team/Enterprise and Claude Team, for example — while consumer free tiers may not. Team seats commonly run in the low tens of dollars per user per month, but pricing and, more importantly, the data-handling terms change; confirm the current terms in writing before you treat any tool as sanctioned.

Tool sanctioning process

Sanctioning is a decision, not a download. Before a tool goes on the approved list, someone checks: does it train on our inputs, where is data stored, does it meet our contracts? Keep a short list of cleared tools and a "not approved" list, and revisit both when terms change.

Team communication optional

Slack, Teams, or email for the review-gate ping and incident reporting — whatever your team already uses. The point is a clear, fast channel to flag "this needs review" and "I think something leaked."

Honest limits

No tool makes you governed. Software can hold the policy and the log, but enforcement, review, and ownership are human work. Anyone selling you a "governance platform" as a substitute for that work is selling you theater — and this is not legal advice; see the risks section.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same policy, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — writing it by hand forces the decisions that matter, and that judgment is what you encode later. Governance cannot be automated away; the "fully implemented" version automates the paperwork, never the judgment.

Manual

One page in a doc, written this afternoon

Draft the one-pager with the policy prompt below: approved uses, prohibited uses, data rules, the review gate. Name your sanctioned tools. Put the one data rule in bold at the top. Start the log by hand for the next few client-facing outputs.

Honest expectation: the first draft takes an afternoon and feels awkward. That awkwardness is the tuition — a standard you can point to beats good intentions nobody can find.

Semi-automated

The policy lives in a system; you hold the gate

Move the policy into a structured doc with version history, an owner, and a "last reviewed" date. Turn the log into a database with a form. Add a risk-triage step for new uses (green/yellow/red), then a human confirms. Wire the review gate into your workflow.

Where most small teams should settle: fast enough that governance is not a tax, structured enough that you can show your work if anyone asks.

Fully implemented

An owned, enforced, regularly revisited standard

A named owner. An onboarding hook so every hire and contractor acknowledges the policy. A structured review gate — drafted, reviewed, approved, sent — that cannot be skipped. A living audit trail. A scheduled quarterly revisit. Disclosure norms baked in.

"Fully implemented" does not mean "runs itself." Governance is ongoing work — enforced, revisited, actually followed — or it is just a file.

Reality check: a policy nobody reads is theater — worse than nothing, because it creates false comfort. 07 · Step-by-step build

From zero to an owned, enforced policy

  1. Draft the one-page policy. Run the policy prompt below, then rewrite the output in your own words. Put it in a shared doc titled "AI Use Policy," date it, and lead with the single data rule in bold. Keep it to one page — length kills adoption.
  2. Define the approved and prohibited list. Name the approved uses (drafting internal notes, summarizing your own documents, brainstorming) and the prohibited ones (pasting client PII or secrets into unsanctioned tools, sending unreviewed AI output to clients or regulators). Anything not listed defaults to "ask first."
  3. Set your data tiers. Sort information into a few tiers — public, internal, confidential/regulated — and state which tier is allowed in which tool. Confidential and regulated data goes only into sanctioned, terms-verified tools, if at all.
  4. Define the human-review gate. Write the one-sentence rule: anything AI-assisted going to a client, regulator, or the public gets reviewed and signed off by a named person first. Decide who reviews, what "reviewed" means (facts checked, no leaked data, tone right), and where drafts wait.
  5. Set up the audit-trail log. Create a sheet or database from the template below: date, item, tool, who produced it, who reviewed it, outcome, notes. Make logging faster than skipping it, and log every client-facing item from day one.
  6. Train the team. Walk everyone through the policy in a short session — not an email they will not read. Cover the one data rule, the sanctioned tools, the review gate, and how to log. Have each person acknowledge they have read it.
  7. Set the review cadence. Put a recurring hold on the calendar (quarterly works for most small teams) to re-verify tool terms, update the approved list, refresh the "last reviewed" date, and review any incidents. A cadence is what keeps the policy alive.
  8. Appoint an owner. Name one accountable person for the whole thing. Without an owner, every other step slowly rots. The owner does not have to do all the work — they have to make sure it keeps happening.

Draft a one-page internal AI use policy

small business. This is a starting draft for me to edit, not final
legal advice.

My business: [type of business, e.g. a 6-person bookkeeping firm]
The sensitive data we handle: [e.g. client financials, PII, contracts]
Tools we want to sanction: [e.g. ChatGPT Team, Claude Team]

Produce a one-page policy with four labeled sections:
1. Approved uses — 4-6 specific, allowed AI uses for our work.
2. Prohibited uses — 4-6 specific, banned uses (be explicit about
   never pasting client PII, secrets, or confidential material into
   tools not on our approved list).
3. Data rules — plain-language rules for what data may go into which
   tool, tied to sensitivity tiers.
4. Review gate — the rule that anything AI-assisted going to a client,
   regulator, or the public must be reviewed and signed off by a named
   person first.

Keep it plain, short, and readable. Flag anywhere I should consult a
qualified attorney for my jurisdiction, industry, or contracts.

Use-case risk triage (green / yellow / red)

Classify a proposed AI use for my business as GREEN, YELLOW, or RED.
This is a starting assessment for a human to confirm, not a final ruling.

Our approved uses: [paste]
Our prohibited uses: [paste]
Our data sensitivity tiers: [paste]

Proposed use: [describe: what someone wants to do, which tool, what
data is involved, and where the output goes]

Output:
- Rating: GREEN (clearly approved), YELLOW (needs a human decision and
  possibly extra safeguards), or RED (prohibited or high-risk).
- Reason: 2-3 sentences tied to a specific policy point or data tier.
- If YELLOW: the exact questions a human should answer before deciding.
- If RED: the specific rule or risk that makes it off-limits.
Do not guess about tool terms or the law; flag where I need to verify
current terms or consult qualified counsel.

Incident quick-write (optional)

Help me write a short, factual internal record of a possible AI-related
data incident. No blame, just facts.

What happened: [e.g. a contract was pasted into a consumer tool]
When and who: [date, role — not for punishment, for the record]
Data involved: [what kind, whose]

Produce: a 4-6 sentence factual summary, a list of immediate steps to
take, and a note on whether this may need to be escalated to qualified
legal/compliance counsel. Do not offer legal conclusions.

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

This is not legal, compliance, medical, or financial advice

Read this twice. This playbook is educational. RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney, and nothing here creates an attorney–client relationship. You must get qualified counsel for your specific jurisdiction, industry, and contracts before relying on any policy you build from this — especially in regulated fields like healthcare, finance, and law.

Over-restriction kills adoption

A policy so strict that people cannot get real work done just pushes AI back into the shadows where you cannot see it. The goal is safe, visible use — not a ban dressed up as governance. If your rules are being ignored, they may be wrong, not your team.

An unenforced policy is theater

Writing the document is the easy part. If nobody reviews outputs, nobody logs, and nobody owns it, the policy creates false comfort and protects you less than an honest "we have no policy yet." Enforcement and ownership are the whole game.

Data leakage is the core danger

The failure you are guarding against is sensitive data landing in a tool that trains on it or stores it insecurely. Verify tool terms in writing, keep the one data rule front and center, and treat every consumer free tier as unsafe for confidential material until proven otherwise.

Regulatory and tool terms change

AI law and tool data-handling terms are moving fast. A policy that was correct last quarter can be stale now. This is exactly why the review cadence exists — a frozen policy quietly goes wrong.

Review cadence

Spot-check the audit log monthly for gaps, re-verify sanctioned-tool terms and refresh the approved list quarterly, and review any incidents and your disclosure norms with qualified counsel at least once a year or whenever your contracts or regulations change.
