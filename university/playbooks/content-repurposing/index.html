Playbook · Marketing

One asset, a week of content — still in your voice

You already made the hard thing: the webinar, the podcast episode, the long essay, the recorded talk. This turns that one asset into a week of LinkedIn posts, a newsletter, short-form video scripts, and threads that still sound like you and never claim something you did not actually say. AI does the drafting; a voice guide and a fact-preservation rule keep it honest; you do the final edit.

01 · The business problem

You make one good thing, then it disappears

You spent weeks on a webinar. Two hundred people saw it live, a few dozen watched the replay, and then it went quiet. The insight is still good — but it is trapped in a sixty-minute recording nobody has time to rewatch. The same fate meets your best podcast episodes, your longest and most careful blog posts, and the talk you gave at that conference. You built assets with a long shelf life and then let them expire in a week.

The obvious fix is to chop each asset into many smaller pieces for the channels where your audience actually is. The problem is that doing this by hand is grinding work. Pulling the quotable moments, rewriting them for LinkedIn versus a newsletter versus a thirty-second video, and matching each to the platform's format takes hours per source — hours that never make the calendar, because the next webinar is already being planned.

AI closes that gap. But it introduces two new dangers that most people ignore: the drafts come out sounding like generic AI content, and they quietly invent statistics, quotes, and claims you never made. This playbook exists to get the speed without either of those failures.

02 · Why it matters

Repetition builds a reputation; one-shot content builds nothing

Nobody remembers a single post. People remember a person who shows up saying consistent, useful things over and over, in the places they already scroll. Repurposing is how one hour of real thinking becomes ten touchpoints — and ten touchpoints is how a stranger starts to recognize your name.

The arithmetic is what makes it worth systematizing. If turning a webinar into a full week of channel content takes you six hours by hand, you will do it maybe once and then quietly stop. Compress the drafting to twenty minutes of review over AI output, keep an hour for real human editing, and the same asset now yields eight to twelve finished pieces at a quality you would sign. That is the difference between a content habit and a content wish.

There is a quieter benefit too. When you repurpose from a real source you actually created, every derivative piece is grounded in something you genuinely believe and said out loud. That is the opposite of the empty, everywhere-at-once content that makes audiences tune out — and it is why the fact-preservation rule below is not a nicety but the whole engine.

03 · Who this is for

People who create long things and want them to travel

  • The founder or expert who publishes. You write essays, host a podcast, or give talks, and you know each one should become a dozen posts — but the repurposing never happens because you are busy making the next big thing.
  • The solo marketer or small team. You are responsible for a content calendar with no staff to feed it. One strong source per week, well repurposed, can fill most of it.
  • The consultant or coach with recorded calls and talks. Your best material is already spoken and captured. You want it working for you on LinkedIn and in a newsletter, in your voice, without hiring a ghostwriter.
  • Not a fit: anyone who wants a bot to autopost AI slop with no human review, or who has no genuine source asset to repurpose from. This playbook amplifies real thinking, not the appearance of it.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From one source to a channel-ready set, in your voice

By the end of a build, you have a repeatable loop that runs each time you publish something long:

  • Capture a clean transcript of the source asset, so the AI works from your actual words, not a summary of them.
  • Extract a voice guide once, from writing you have already published, so every draft is measured against how you actually sound.
  • Mine the transcript for atomic ideas — the standalone points, stories, and quotable lines worth their own piece.
  • Repurpose each idea per channel — LinkedIn post, newsletter section, short-form video script, X thread, quote-graphic caption, SEO snippet — under a rule that forbids inventing facts.
  • Generate hook and headline variants so you can pick the strongest opening rather than settling for the first.
  • Edit every piece by hand before it publishes, because the human pass is where voice and truth are protected.

The AI does the ninety percent that is transcribing, mining, and drafting. You keep the ten percent that is judgment: does this sound like me, and is every claim in it true?

05 · Tools you will need

Pick a tier and start; you can grow into more

The manual version needs one AI chat tool, a transcript, and somewhere to keep your drafts. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm before you buy.

AI assistant required

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both about $20/month, for mining transcripts, drafting per channel, and generating hook variants. The paid tiers give you the stronger models and the longer context you will want when feeding in a full hour-long transcript.

Transcription

A transcription tool to turn the audio or video into text. Otter and Descript are common; many podcast hosts and video platforms now include built-in transcripts, and Descript doubles as an editor if you also want to cut short-form clips. Accuracy on names and jargon varies, so always proofread.

A place to keep drafts

Notion is a natural home — one page per source, with the transcript, voice guide, and each channel's drafts together. Notion's AI features are bundled into the Business plan (about $20/user/month); the older standalone $10 AI add-on has been removed. A plain Google Doc works fine to start.

Scheduling / publishing

Buffer or Hypefury to queue and space out posts across channels, or each platform's native scheduler if you prefer to stay in-app. Scheduling is optional for the manual version — you can just post by hand — but it is what makes a weekly cadence stick.

Automation glue optional

Zapier to move a finished transcript into your drafting workspace and route approved pieces to your scheduler. Zapier's Professional plan starts around $19.99/month annual for 750 tasks; a multi-step Zap burns one task per step per run, so budget by steps, not runs.

Honest limits

None of this removes the editing. AI repurposing is a strong first-draft engine, not a publish button. Budget real human time on every piece — that is where the voice stays yours and the facts stay true. See the risks section before you automate anything.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same loop, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — doing it by hand teaches you what "sounds like me" and "provably true" actually look like, and that judgment is what you encode later.

Manual

One transcript, one AI chat, one focused afternoon

Build your voice guide once from published work. Get a clean transcript. Mine it for atomic ideas, run the per-channel prompt that forbids inventing facts, then edit every draft against your voice guide and verify every claim before you post.

Honest expectation: the first pass is slow, maybe an afternoon for one source. That is the tuition you pay to build the automated version well.

Semi-automated

The tools transcribe and draft; you review at two gates

Store your voice guide once. Auto-transcribe each source and proofread names and jargon. The AI batch-mines and batch-drafts. Then a fact-check pass (gate one) and a voice-and-send pass (gate two) before anything queues.

Where most creators should live for a while: fast enough to be consistent, slow enough to stay unmistakably yours.

Fully implemented

A scheduled loop with a mandatory editing gate

A new transcript or episode triggers the loop in Zapier or Make. It cleans the transcript, attaches your voice guide and fact-preservation rule, drafts per channel plus hooks into a "Needs review" view, and pings you. Nothing publishes until you edit and approve.

"Fully implemented" does not mean "posts by itself." It means everything except the judgment is automated.

Reality check: this is a real build with real maintenance — transcription mishears names, models drift toward generic phrasing, and a broken Zap fails silently. Budget a few hours a month, and never remove the editing gate to "save time." The gate is where your voice and credibility are protected. An unedited AI post that invents a statistic will cost you more trust than the whole system saves you in hours.

07 · Step-by-step build

From zero to the semi-automated loop

  1. Extract your voice guide. Run the voice-guide prompt over three or four published pieces. Save the result in a doc titled "Voice Guide" and treat it as living: revise it when your writing shifts or when a draft sounds off despite following it.
  2. Set up your source workspace. Create a template — a Notion page or doc — with sections for Transcript, Atomic ideas, LinkedIn, Newsletter, Video scripts, Thread, Captions, SEO snippets, and Status (Draft / Fact-checked / Edited / Scheduled / Published).
  3. Transcribe and clean. Transcribe your source asset and read the whole thing once, fixing wrong names, terms, and mishears. This is the single most important quality step — every downstream piece inherits these words.
  4. Mine atomic ideas. Run the idea-extractor prompt over the cleaned transcript. Review the list, keep the ideas that stand on their own, and star the two or three strongest to lead with.
  5. Draft per channel. For each starred idea, run the per-channel prompt with your voice guide attached and the fact-preservation rule in force. Generate one piece per channel you actually use — do not spread yourself across platforms you will not maintain.
  6. Generate hook variants. Run the hook-and-headline prompt on your lead pieces to produce several openings each. Pick the strongest by hand; a good hook is the difference between a read and a scroll.
  7. Fact-check and edit. With the transcript open, verify every quote, number, and claim in every draft, then edit each piece into your voice. Cut generic phrasing on sight. Only then mark it Edited.
  8. Add automation last. Once the manual loop is smooth, wire the repetitive moves (transcript in → mine → draft → review view → scheduler) in Zapier or Make, keeping the editing gate as a hard stop. Automate what you have validated, never the judgment.

08 · Prompts and templates

Copy these, then make them yours

Voice guide from your own writing

Below are 3-4 pieces I have actually written and published:
[paste your writing samples]

Study them and produce a reusable "voice guide" I can attach to
future drafting tasks. Include:
1. Tone in 3-5 adjectives, with a one-line reason for each.
2. Sentence rhythm and length patterns you notice.
3. Words and phrases I clearly favor.
4. Words, cliches, and patterns I clearly avoid.
5. How I open and how I close.
6. A short "do NOT sound like this" list of generic phrasings
   that would break the voice.

Base every point ONLY on the samples. Do not invent traits I did
not demonstrate. Output as a labeled list I can paste later.

Transcript to atomic ideas

Below is a transcript of something I created:
[paste transcript]

Extract the standalone ideas worth their own piece of content.
For each, output:
- A one-line summary of the idea.
- The type: insight / story / framework / quotable line / data point.
- The exact wording from the transcript it came from (verbatim),
  with a rough location marker.

Rules:
- Only use what is actually in the transcript. Do not add ideas,
  examples, or claims that are not present.
- For quotable lines and data points, quote them EXACTLY. If a
  number or claim is ambiguous in the transcript, flag it rather
  than cleaning it up.
Return a numbered list, strongest ideas first.

Per-channel repurposing (fact-preservation rule)

Repurpose ONE idea into content for the channel I name.

My voice guide:
[paste voice guide]

The source idea and its exact transcript wording:
[paste one idea + verbatim source]

Channel: [LinkedIn post | newsletter section | 30-sec video
script | X thread | quote-graphic caption | SEO snippet]

Hard rules:
- Use ONLY facts, numbers, quotes, and examples present in the
  source above. Do NOT invent statistics, studies, quotes, names,
  or outcomes. If a claim would strengthen the piece but is not in
  the source, leave it out and tell me what I would need to add.
- Match my voice guide. Avoid generic AI phrasing.
- Format natively for the channel (length, line breaks, hook).
Output the piece, then a one-line "claims used" note listing every
factual claim and where in the source it came from.

Hook and headline variants

Here is a finished piece:
[paste the draft]

My voice guide:
[paste voice guide]

Write 6 alternative opening hooks (or headlines) for it. Vary the
angle: curiosity, a concrete number from the piece, a contrarian
take, a direct question, a short story open, a plain-value open.

Rules:
- Every hook must be true to the piece — no bait the content does
  not deliver, no invented stats.
- Keep my voice. No "unlock", "game-changer", or hollow hype.
Number them and mark the one you think is strongest, with a reason.

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

Fabricated stats and quotes

The single biggest failure mode: the model invents a statistic, a study, or a quote you never said to make a piece punchier. The prompts forbid invention and require a "claims used" trace, and your editing gate is where you verify every claim against the transcript before publishing.

AI-tell and sameness

Even truthful drafts can come out sounding like everyone else's AI content — the same rhythms, the same empty transitions. The fixes are the voice guide, a ruthless human edit, and cutting any line that could have been written about anything.

Copyright and attribution

Repurposing your own work is straightforward. Repurposing someone else's talk, article, or podcast is not — quoting, clipping, and reframing others' material raises copyright and attribution questions. When in doubt, attribute clearly, quote sparingly, and get permission; do not pass another person's ideas off as your own.

Platform authenticity

Audiences and platforms increasingly penalize content that reads as automated or inauthentic. Post as a human who reviewed the work, keep a real cadence rather than a firehose, and do not disguise AI-assisted drafts as spontaneous.

Review cadence

Read a sample of published pieces weekly for voice drift, re-check your fact-preservation discipline monthly, and revisit your voice guide quarterly as your writing evolves.
