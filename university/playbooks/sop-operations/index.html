Playbook · Operations

SOPs that run the business without you

Get the work out of your head and onto the page. AI drafts your Standard Operating Procedures from a walkthrough — clean steps, roles, and decision points — so the business runs when you are not in the room. The AI drafts fast; a human who owns the process verifies that every step is true before anyone follows it.

01 · The business problem

The business runs on knowledge only you have

In most owner-led businesses, the real operating manual lives in one person's head. You know the exact order to onboard a client, which supplier to call when the first one is out, how to close the books, what "done" looks like for a deliverable. None of it is written down, because writing it down is tedious and the work is always more urgent than documenting the work.

So the business is quietly hostage to your presence. A new hire takes months to get useful because you are their only reference. You cannot take a real vacation without your phone lighting up. Quality drifts depending on who happened to do the task and what they remembered. When someone leaves, a chunk of how the business actually works walks out the door with them.

AI does not fix this by writing generic templates you will never use. It fixes it by collapsing the hours between "I know how to do this" and "here is a clear, step-by-step SOP a competent person could follow" — so the documentation actually gets created, and stays created, instead of remaining a someday project.

02 · Why it matters

Documented work is transferable work

A process that only exists in your memory cannot be delegated, audited, improved, or sold. A process written down as a good SOP can be handed to a new hire, checked against reality, refined when it breaks, and repeated at a consistent standard whether you are watching or on a plane. That is the whole difference between owning a job and owning a business.

The economics are plain. Writing a solid SOP by hand — thinking through every step, every role, every branch, then formatting it so someone can follow it — is a half-day of focused work per procedure, which is exactly why the backlog never shrinks. Feed a recorded walkthrough or a short interview into an AI assistant and you get a clean first draft in minutes. Your job shifts from author to editor: verifying, correcting, and filling the gaps the AI could not know. That is the difference between an SOP library that exists and one that stays a good intention.

There is a compounding benefit too. Once a process is written down, you can see it — and seeing it is the first step to improving it. Documented steps expose the redundant approval, the handoff that always stalls, the check nobody actually does. You cannot streamline what you have never made visible.

03 · Who this is for

Owners and teams whose operations live in their heads

  • The owner who is the bottleneck. Everything routes through you because nothing is written down. You want a documented playbook so the team can act without asking, and you can step away without the wheels coming off.
  • The small team about to hire (2–30 people). You are bringing people on and re-explaining the same tasks over and over. You want onboarding that scales past your patience — SOPs a new hire can follow on day one.
  • The operations lead standardizing quality. The same task gets done three different ways depending on who does it. You want one documented standard, with the checklists and decision points that make the outcome consistent.
  • Not a fit: anyone hoping to publish AI-written procedures for safety-critical, regulated, or high-liability work without expert sign-off. This playbook speeds up the drafting; it never replaces the qualified human who owns the process and stakes their name on it.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From a walkthrough to a living, followable SOP

By the end of a build, you have a repeatable loop that turns tacit knowledge into documentation you actually maintain:

  • Capture a process by screen-recording yourself doing it, walking through it aloud, or interviewing the person who owns it — no blank page required.
  • Draft that raw capture into a structured SOP: purpose, prerequisites, numbered steps, decision points, and a roles table so everyone knows who does what.
  • Verify every step against reality, because the process owner — not the AI — is accountable for accuracy. This is the non-negotiable gate.
  • Standardize format, naming, and structure across every SOP so the library reads as one system, not a pile of documents.
  • Maintain each SOP with a documented owner and a review date, so it gets updated when the process changes instead of quietly going stale.
  • Convert finished SOPs into quick-reference checklists and onboarding guides, so the documentation becomes something people use daily, not a binder nobody opens.

A human who owns the process always verifies before an SOP is published. The AI does the ninety percent that is drafting and formatting; you keep the ten percent that is knowing whether it is actually true.

05 · Tools you will need

Pick a tier and start; you can grow into more

You do not need all of these. The manual version needs one AI chat tool and somewhere to keep the document. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm before you buy.

AI assistant required

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both about $20/month, to turn a messy transcript into a structured SOP, audit it for gaps, and generate checklists. Free tiers work for testing; the paid tiers give you the stronger models and longer context you will want once you are feeding in real walkthroughs.

Capture tool

A screen recorder or step-capturer so you never start from a blank page: Loom or Descript to record and auto-transcribe a walkthrough, or Scribe or Tango to auto-generate step-by-step captures as you click through a task. Several have usable free tiers; paid plans typically run $12–$24/month.

A home for your SOPs

Notion Business (about $20/user/month, with AI bundled into the Business tier now that the old $10 AI add-on has been removed) makes a strong searchable SOP library with owners and review dates as properties. Google Docs plus a shared folder works perfectly well and costs nothing extra if you already use Workspace.

Transcription

If your capture tool does not transcribe, a dedicated transcription pass turns an expert interview or a screen recording into text the AI can work from. Descript doubles as this; most recorders now include a transcript export.

Automation glue optional

Zapier to nudge the maintenance loop — for example, flag any SOP past its review date. Zapier's Professional plan starts around $19.99/month annual for 750 tasks; a multi-step Zap burns one task per step per run, so budget by steps, not runs.

Guardrail note

Whatever you use, the AI drafts and a qualified human verifies. For anything safety-critical, regulated, legal, medical, or financial, the process owner or a licensed professional must sign off before the SOP is used. This is educational, not professional advice — see the risks section.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same loop, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — documenting one process by hand teaches you what a good SOP looks like, and that judgment is what you encode later.

Manual

One recording, one AI chat, one afternoon

Pick your most-repeated process. Capture it while you do it, narrating each choice. Paste the transcript into the assistant with the drafting prompt, verify every step as the owner, and save it with an owner and a review date.

Honest expectation: the first SOP takes an afternoon, mostly in the verify step. That is the tuition you pay to build the library well.

Semi-automated

The AI drafts and audits; you verify at two gates

Standardize one template. Capture related processes in batches, draft each into the template, then run the gap auditor (gate one). The owner corrects the flags; the owner signs off and publishes (gate two).

Where most owner-led businesses should live for a while: fast enough to clear the backlog, rigorous enough to stay trustworthy.

Fully implemented

A maintained library with a mandatory verification gate

SOPs live in one searchable system with Owner, Last verified, and Review date. A capture-to-draft pipeline and automated gap audit feed a "Needs verification" view. A scheduled check flags overdue SOPs. Nothing publishes until the owner marks it Verified.

"Fully implemented" does not mean "writes and publishes itself." It means everything except the accuracy judgment is systematized.

Reality check: a wrong SOP is worse than none — people trust it, follow it precisely, and reproduce the mistake at scale. Processes drift too: the SOP describes last quarter's workflow while the team quietly does something else, so the document lies without anyone deciding to lie. Give every SOP a documented owner and a review date, and never automate away the verification gate to save time. The gate is the whole point.

07 · Step-by-step build

From zero to a standardized, maintained SOP library

  1. Capture the process. Screen-record yourself doing the task while narrating, use a step-capturer like Scribe or Tango, or interview the owner. Aim for a complete, honest record of what actually happens — including the exceptions and the "oh, and when X, we do Y."
  2. Transcribe the capture. Turn the recording or interview into text. Most recorders export a transcript; if not, run it through a transcription tool. This transcript is the raw input the AI drafts from.
  3. Define one standard template. Have the assistant produce a reusable SOP structure: Purpose, Prerequisites, Roles (a RACI-style table), Steps (numbered), Decision points, and Related checklists. Reuse it for every SOP so the library is consistent.
  4. Draft the SOP. Run the SOP-drafting prompt on the transcript. It returns a structured first draft in your template — numbered steps, prerequisites, decision points, and a roles table.
  5. Verify against reality. The process owner reads every step and confirms it is exactly how the work is done. Correct wrong steps, add tacit knowledge the AI could not know, and remove anything invented. Accuracy is a human responsibility here.
  6. Run the gap and ambiguity audit. Paste the corrected draft into the SOP-audit prompt to catch missing steps, vague instructions ("handle it appropriately"), undefined roles, and decision points without stated criteria. Resolve each flag.
  7. Publish with an owner and review date. Store the SOP in your central library. Attach the responsible owner and a review date, and mark it Verified. An SOP with no owner will not survive contact with a changing process.
  8. Convert into checklists and onboarding. Once verified, run the checklist prompt to generate a quick-reference version for daily use, and adapt it into an onboarding guide for new hires. Keep all artifacts pointing back to the source SOP so there is one place to update.

08 · Prompts and templates

Copy these, then make them yours

Walkthrough transcript → structured SOP

You are turning a messy walkthrough transcript into a clean
Standard Operating Procedure. Do not invent steps. If something
is unclear or missing, list it under "Questions for the owner"
instead of guessing.

Process name: [name]
Transcript of the walkthrough / interview:
[paste the raw transcript]

Produce an SOP with these sections:
1. Purpose — one sentence on why this process exists and what
   "done" looks like.
2. Prerequisites — access, tools, inputs, or approvals needed
   before starting.
3. Roles — a RACI-style table (Responsible, Accountable,
   Consulted, Informed) for the key activities.
4. Steps — numbered, one action each, in the order they happen.
   Name the tool or place for each step.
5. Decision points — where the path branches, with the exact
   criterion for each option (if X then A, if Y then B).
6. Questions for the owner — anything ambiguous, missing, or
   assumed that a human must confirm before this is trusted.

Use plain, specific language. Do not fill gaps with generic
best-practice filler.

SOP gap and ambiguity auditor

You are auditing a draft SOP for gaps and ambiguity before it
is published. Be strict — a wrong or vague SOP is worse than none.

Draft SOP:
[paste the SOP]

Return a numbered list of issues, each tagged:
- MISSING STEP — a gap where a reader would get stuck or improvise.
- AMBIGUOUS — vague wording like "handle appropriately",
  "as needed", or "check it" with no concrete criterion.
- UNDEFINED ROLE — a step where it is unclear who does it or
  who signs off.
- MISSING CRITERION — a decision point with no stated rule for
  choosing a path.
- UNSTATED ASSUMPTION — knowledge the SOP relies on but never
  states.

For each issue, name the step number and suggest a specific fix
in the form of a question the process owner should answer. Do
not rewrite the SOP; just surface what a human must resolve.

SOP → quick-reference checklist

Convert this verified SOP into a quick-reference checklist for
someone who already knows the process and just needs to not miss
a step.

Verified SOP:
[paste the SOP]

Rules:
- One checkable item per line, in order, phrased as an action
  ("Confirm client contract is signed").
- Group under short headings if the process has clear phases.
- Mark any step that requires a sign-off or approval with
  "[APPROVAL]".
- Mark any decision point with the branch condition in brackets.
- Keep it to a single page. Do not add steps that are not in the
  SOP. Do not restate the full instructions — this is a memory
  aid, not the SOP itself.

SOP → onboarding guide (optional)

Adapt this verified SOP into an onboarding version for a brand-new
hire who has never done this task and does not know our tools yet.

Verified SOP:
[paste the SOP]

Add, without changing the actual steps:
- A one-line "why this matters" for context.
- A note on each tool the first time it appears (what it is, where
  to find it).
- A "common mistakes to avoid" box at the end, drawn only from
  the decision points and warnings in the SOP.
Keep every operational step identical to the source SOP. Flag
anything a new hire could not do without access or training they
may not have yet.

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

Hallucinated or incorrect steps

The single biggest failure mode: the AI confidently writes a step that is plausible but wrong, or invents a check that does not exist. The prompts forbid invention and route uncertainty to "Questions for the owner," and the verification gate is where a human who knows the process confirms every step before anyone trusts it.

A wrong SOP is worse than none

An undocumented process makes people ask; a wrong documented one makes them proceed confidently in the wrong direction, at scale. Never publish an unverified draft, and treat every SOP as a claim to be checked, not a fact.

Process drift and staleness

Processes change; SOPs do not update themselves. An out-of-date SOP quietly describes work nobody does anymore. Give every SOP a documented owner and a review date, and hold the review.

Compliance and safety-critical processes

For anything regulated, safety-critical, or high-liability, an AI draft is a starting point only. The process owner or a licensed professional must review and sign off before the SOP is used.

False confidence

A polished, well-formatted SOP reads as authoritative whether or not it is correct. Do not let good formatting substitute for verification, and mark clearly which SOPs are Verified versus Draft.

Educational, not professional advice

This playbook is educational and does not provide legal, medical, or financial advice. RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. For legal, regulatory, safety, medical, or financial procedures, get qualified counsel or the relevant licensed professional for your jurisdiction and industry.

Review cadence

Verify each new SOP before publishing, re-review every SOP on its stated review date, and audit the whole library quarterly for anything past due or owner-less.
