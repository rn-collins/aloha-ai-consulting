Playbook · Operations

An internal brain your team can actually ask

Turn scattered docs, buried Slack threads, and the answers that live in one person's head into a knowledge base your team queries in plain language — one that quotes your own documents, cites where each answer came from, and admits when it does not know. No mystery answers. No pretending a search box is the same as knowing something is true.

Start with the manual version Have us build it

01 · The business problem

The answer exists somewhere — nobody finds it in time

Every growing team runs up the same quiet debt. The onboarding steps live in a Google Doc someone wrote two roles ago. The real reason you switched vendors is in a Slack thread from March. The way you actually handle a refund sits in one person's head, and when they are on vacation the question just waits. The knowledge is not missing — it is scattered, undated, and unfindable at the exact moment someone needs it.

So the same questions get asked over and over in chat. New hires spend their first month interrupting senior people. Two teams do the same task two different ways because each found a different half-true doc. And when the one person who knows leaves, part of how the company works leaves with them.

AI does not fix this by generating more content. It fixes it by turning what you already have into something you can question in plain language — and, done right, by refusing to make things up when the answer genuinely is not written down. The hard part was never the model. It is deciding what counts as the truth, and keeping that truth current.

02 · Why it matters

A findable answer is a decision made faster, the same way twice

When the right answer is one question away, the whole team moves differently. New hires get productive in weeks instead of quarters because they can ask instead of wait. Support and ops give consistent answers because everyone reads from the same source. And the senior people who used to be a human help desk get their focus back, because the routine questions no longer route through them.

The arithmetic is plain. If a recurring question costs someone ten minutes to track down, and it gets asked twenty times a week across the team, that is a few hours a week evaporating into a search you already paid to write down once. A knowledge base that answers in seconds does not just save the time — it stops the wrong answer from spreading when someone gives up and guesses.

There is a durability dividend too. Knowledge that lives only in conversation walks out the door with the person. Knowledge that is collected, structured, and queryable becomes an asset the company keeps — one that gets more valuable as it gets more complete, not less valuable as people turn over.

03 · Who this is for

Teams whose knowledge outgrew the people holding it

  • The scaling small business or startup (5–100 people). You have crossed the size where everyone just knows how things work. Questions now get asked in three channels and answered three ways. You want one place people trust.
  • The operations or people lead. You own onboarding, process, and "how do we do X," and you are tired of being the search engine. You want new hires answering their own questions correctly by day two.
  • The support or customer-facing team. You need consistent, correct answers fast, and you need to know which policy or doc an answer came from so you can trust it before repeating it to a customer.
  • Not a fit: anyone hoping to point AI at a pile of contradictory, undated docs and get reliable answers without curating first. Garbage in is confident garbage out — the curation is the work, not the shortcut.

04 · What this workflow accomplishes

From scattered sources to a cited, current, plain-language answer

By the end of a build, you have a loop that keeps your internal brain honest and up to date:

  • Collect the real sources of truth — docs, wikis, key Slack decisions, the process a person carries in their head — into one inventory instead of five scattered homes.
  • Curate them: pick the one canonical version of each thing, mark the rest archived, and date what is current so the system never quotes a doc you retired.
  • Structure the content into clear, titled, single-topic pages so both a person and a model can find the right passage fast.
  • Ground answers strictly on those sources, so the assistant quotes what you wrote rather than inventing plausible-sounding filler.
  • Cite every answer back to the specific doc it came from, so anyone can verify before they act.
  • Maintain the source of truth on a cadence — because a knowledge base that stops being updated becomes a confident source of wrong answers.

The system's job is to retrieve and quote your knowledge, not to author it. The truth stays something humans own and keep current; the AI just makes it findable and readable.

05 · Tools you will need

Pick a tier and start; you can grow into more

You do not need all of these. The manual version needs one AI chat tool and the docs you already have. Prices below are current as of mid-2026 and change often — confirm before you buy.

AI assistant required

ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro, both about $20/month. This is what you use to clean up messy docs, draft FAQs, and — in the manual version — answer questions from context you paste in. Free tiers work for testing; paid tiers give you the stronger models and higher limits you will want once this is real.

A doc hub with AI search

Notion is a common home for this. Notion Business runs about $20/user/month, and the AI features (Ask Notion / AI search across your workspace) are bundled into the Business tier now — the old separate ~$10 AI add-on has been removed. Custom Agents, if you build them, draw on metered credits (roughly $10 per 1,000 credits) on top of the seat price, so watch usage.

Alternative doc hubs

Google Drive works if your docs already live there. Purpose-built knowledge tools like Slab or Guru add cleaner structure, verification workflows, and freshness reminders — useful once your library grows past what a folder can manage.

A real RAG assistant advanced

To ground an assistant strictly on your own docs, you need a vector store to index your content plus an assistant or API layer that retrieves the relevant passages and answers only from them. This is developer-level effort — cost varies widely with volume and how much you build versus buy — and it is genuinely more work than turning on AI search. Do not start here.

Sync / automation glue optional

Zapier or Make to keep sources in sync — for example, pushing a resolved Slack decision into your doc hub. Zapier's Professional plan starts around $19.99/month annual for 750 tasks; a multi-step Zap burns one task per step per run, so budget by steps, not runs.

Guardrail note

Whatever you use, keep confidential and personal data inside an environment you control and check what your tools do with your content. Set permissions so the assistant surfaces only what a given person is allowed to see. This is not legal advice — see the risks section.

06 · Three ways to run it

Manual, semi-automated, fully implemented

Same loop, three levels of build. Start at the left even if you plan to end at the right — doing it by hand teaches you which docs are actually canonical and how bad the contradictions are, and that judgment is what you encode later.

Manual

One AI chat, the docs you have, an afternoon

Inventory your top five to ten sources. Pick one truth per topic. Clean one messy doc into a FAQ, answer a real question grounded in it, and run the gap-finder to see what your docs cannot answer yet.

Honest expectation: the first pass is slow, and most of the time goes to deciding what is true. That is the tuition you pay to build the grounded version well.

Semi-automated

A doc hub with AI search finds it; you keep it curated

Your cleaned, canonical pages live in a hub with built-in AI search, so anyone asks in plain language and gets an answer with a link back to the source. You keep two jobs: curation and freshness.

Where most teams should live for a long time: fast, cheap, and honest enough — without the real engineering of a custom RAG build until you need it.

Fully implemented

A grounded RAG assistant, cited, permission-aware

A retrieval-augmented assistant indexes your docs, retrieves the passages relevant to each question, and answers only from them — quoting sources, respecting who is asking, and declining when the answer is not there.

"Fully implemented" is not a weekend project. It means real engineering plus ongoing curation, with the freshness routine treated as load-bearing.

Reality check: grounding reduces hallucination but does not eliminate it — and it does nothing for a stale source. If a document is out of date, the assistant will cite it and give a confident, wrong answer with a straight face. The maintenance that keeps your source of truth current is not overhead you can cut later; it is the load-bearing part of the whole system.

07 · Step-by-step build

From scattered docs to a curated, AI-searchable hub

  1. Inventory and pick the truth. List every source that answers a common question, note its owner and last-trusted date, and choose one canonical version per topic. Archive the losers so nothing can quote a doc you retired.
  2. Clean each source into single-topic pages. Run the FAQ extractor to turn rambling docs into clear, titled, one-question-per-answer pages. A model retrieves a tight, well-titled passage far better than a wall of text.
  3. Add owner and freshness fields. Give every page an owner and a "last reviewed" date. This is the discipline that keeps the source of truth true, and it is the field the whole system leans on.
  4. Load into your doc hub. Put the curated pages into Notion or your chosen hub in a clear structure — by team or by topic — so both people and AI search can navigate it.
  5. Turn on AI search and test it. Enable Ask / AI search across the workspace, then throw your team's real questions at it. Log every wrong answer; the fix is usually a content gap, not a settings tweak.
  6. Run the gap-finder. Use the gap-finder prompt to list the questions your library cannot answer yet. Prioritize by how often each is asked, and write the missing pages.
  7. Set permissions and a review cadence. Restrict pages so people see only what they should, and schedule owners to re-confirm or update their pages when review dates pass. Retire stale material on sight.
  8. Consider a grounded RAG build only if you need it. If AI search over curated docs is not enough — you need strict citations, customer-facing reliability, or permission-aware retrieval at scale — scope a RAG assistant. Automate the parts you have already validated by hand; never automate away the curation.

08 · Prompts and templates

Copy these, then make them yours

Doc-to-clean-FAQ extractor

You are cleaning up an internal document into a clear FAQ so my
team and an AI search tool can both find answers fast.

Here is the document:
[paste the messy doc]

Produce a set of Q&A pairs where:
- Each question is one a real teammate would actually type.
- Each answer is self-contained and uses ONLY facts stated in
  the document. Do not add best practices or outside knowledge.
- If the document is ambiguous or contradicts itself, do not
  smooth it over — list the ambiguity under "Needs a human
  decision" instead of guessing.
- Give each Q&A a short, descriptive title.

End with a one-line note on anything that looks out of date.

Grounded-answer system prompt

You answer questions using ONLY the context documents provided
below. Follow these rules exactly:

1. Base every answer solely on the provided context. Do not use
   outside or prior knowledge, even if you think you know it.
2. Cite the source: after each answer, name the specific document
   (and section, if given) the answer came from.
3. If the answer is not fully contained in the context, say
   exactly: "I don't know based on the current documents."
   Then say what document would need to exist to answer it.
4. If two documents disagree, say so and quote both — do not
   pick a winner on your own.
5. Never invent policies, numbers, names, or dates.

Context documents:
[retrieved passages go here]

Question:
[user question]

Knowledge-gap finder

Here is the full list of topics and pages currently in our
internal knowledge base:
[paste page titles, or a summary of what's covered]

Here are the questions our team actually asks most often:
[paste real questions from Slack/support/onboarding]

Do two things:
1. List the questions that our current pages CANNOT fully answer,
   ranked by how often they seem to be asked.
2. For each gap, name the single page we should write to close it
   and who is the likely owner (a role, not a guess at a person).

Do not invent answers to the gaps. Only identify what's missing.

Freshness reviewer (optional)

Review this internal page for signs it may be out of date:
[paste page + its "last reviewed" date]

Flag: references to old tools, people, prices, dates, or policies
that may have changed; anything that contradicts a newer page I
paste; and any claim that should have an owner re-confirm it.
Do not rewrite the page — just list what a human should verify.

09 · Risks and review points

Where this goes wrong, and how to catch it

Hallucination when ungrounded

An assistant left to answer from general knowledge will invent plausible policies and numbers. Ground it strictly on your own documents, require citations, and make "I don't know" an acceptable, expected answer.

Stale sources, confident wrong answers

Grounding does not help if the grounded doc is out of date. A retired policy that stays in the index gets cited with total confidence. Every page needs an owner, a review date, and a way to fall out of the system when retired.

Confidential or personal data leaving your environment

Feeding sensitive docs or PII into a tool means trusting where that content goes. Check your tools' data handling, keep sensitive material in environments you control, and exclude what should not be indexed at all.

Access control and permissions

If the assistant can surface a document to anyone who asks, it becomes a leak. Retrieval must respect who is asking so people see only what they are allowed to see.

Over-reliance

A confident, cited answer can still be wrong if the source was wrong. Teach the team to click through to the source for anything consequential, rather than treating the assistant as an oracle.

Legal, medical, and financial limits

This playbook is educational, not legal, medical, or financial advice; RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. Do not let an internal assistant stand in for qualified professional advice, and get counsel for your jurisdiction and industry on data and privacy obligations.

Review cadence

Spot-check answers weekly, have page owners re-confirm freshness on each page's schedule, and audit permissions and the gap list quarterly.

10 · KPIs that tell the truth

Measure trust and freshness, not just usage

MetricWhat it tells youHealthy signal
% of questions answered correctlyWhether the base is actually trustworthy, verified against sourcesHigh and rising as content is curated; spot-checked, not assumed
Time-to-answerHow fast someone gets a usable, cited answer versus hunting for itSeconds to a cited answer, down from minutes of searching
Deflected repeat questionsWhether the same questions stop clogging chat and interrupting peopleFalling volume of "where do I find…" in team channels
Coverage (questions with a source)How much of what people ask your docs can actually answerGap list shrinking as missing pages get written
Freshness (pages past review date)Whether the source of truth is being kept currentLow and stable; owners clearing reviews on cadence
"I don't know" rateWhether the system is honestly declining vs. guessingPresent and healthy — a well-placed "I don't know" beats a confident wrong answer

Watch answer quality, not just query count. A base people trust because it says "I don't know" honestly is worth far more than a busy one that guesses.
