Lesson

When not to use AI

Knowing where a model does not belong is what separates people who use AI well from people who get burned by it. This lesson draws the honest boundaries.

Get Your AI Roadmap Browse all lessons

What this is

The moments to close the chat window

A clear list of the situations where the right move is to stop and hand off to a person.

AI is a strong drafting and thinking partner, useful across a wide range of work. But it predicts plausible text. It does not know what is true, it does not carry responsibility, and it does not understand the stakes of your specific situation. This lesson names the categories where those limits turn from harmless into dangerous.

The goal is not to make you afraid of the tool. It is to give you a short set of tripwires, so you can use AI aggressively everywhere else, knowing you have marked off the places it should not go.

Who this is for

Anyone drawing the line

  • Business owners and teams who have started using AI and now need to know where to stop.
  • Anyone tempted to ask a chatbot a question whose wrong answer could cost real money, health, or legal standing.
  • Managers writing an AI policy who want the "do not" list to be concrete instead of a vague warning.

When to use this list

Four features that should make you slow down

Pull up these boundaries whenever a task has one of these features: a decision you cannot easily reverse, a fact you cannot check, a person who could be harmed by an error, or a rule you are legally bound to follow. If any are present, read the next section carefully.

The boundaries

When not to use AI — specific boundaries

The reliable test: could a confident, wrong answer hurt someone or expose you? If yes, AI is a research assistant at most, and a qualified human owns the decision.

High-stakes legal, medical, and financial decisions

Do not treat a model's output as legal, medical, or financial advice. It can explain concepts and help you prepare questions for a professional. It cannot account for your jurisdiction, your full history, current regulation, or the cost of being wrong. A confident paragraph about your tax situation, a medication interaction, or a contract clause is a starting point for a conversation with a qualified human, never the final word. Nothing on this site is legal, medical, or financial advice, and RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney.

Facts you cannot verify

If the model states a statistic, a case citation, a quote, a date, or a source and you have no way to check it, do not use it. Models produce fluent, confident text that is sometimes simply wrong, and the fabricated version looks identical to the real one. Invented legal citations have already gotten lawyers sanctioned. If verification is impossible, the output is not usable.

Confidential or regulated data

Do not paste client records, patient details, trade secrets, unreleased financials, or anything covered by a confidentiality agreement into a tool that is not contractually cleared for it. See the Privacy and Confidentiality lesson for the full breakdown.

Genuine human-judgment calls

Firing someone, resolving a conflict between two people, deciding whether to trust a partner, comforting someone in crisis, making an ethical call where reasonable people disagree: these need a human who carries the weight of the outcome. A model can help you think through options, but the choice, and the accountability, stay with you.

Anything you could not defend

If a regulator, a client, or a court asked "why did you say this," and your only answer is "the AI wrote it," you should not ship it. Ownership of the work has to sit with a person who understands it and can stand behind it.

Tools that fit

Tools for the safe version of these tasks

When a task sits near a boundary, the fix is often to change what you ask for, not to abandon AI entirely.

  • Perplexity, or a model with browsing and citations: better when you need sources you can click and verify, rather than an unsourced claim.
  • Claude and ChatGPT: strong for drafting the list of questions to bring to your lawyer, doctor, or accountant, so you arrive prepared rather than relying on the model's answer.
  • Your professional's own portal, or a human specialist: for the actual decision. No general model replaces this.

Use AI to prepare for the expert conversation. Do not use it to skip the expert.

Workflow

The boundary check, step by step

  1. Name the task in one sentence. Vague tasks hide their stakes.
  2. Ask: is this reversible? If undoing a mistake is hard or impossible, raise your caution level.
  3. Ask: can I verify the output? If not, it is not usable as fact.
  4. Ask: could a wrong answer harm a person or expose me legally? If yes, a qualified human owns the decision.
  5. Ask: is confidential or regulated data involved? If yes, apply the privacy rule before anything else.
  6. Decide the role. Full draft, research assistant only, or hands off entirely. Write down which one.

Prompts

Example prompts you can copy

These keep AI in a safe, boundary-respecting role.

I have a meeting with my [accountant / attorney / doctor] about [topic].
Do not give me advice or tell me what to do. Instead, write a list of
smart questions I should ask so I understand my options and risks. Flag
anything where the answer depends on my specific situation.
I'm about to rely on the claim below. List every specific fact, number,
citation, or source in it that I would need to independently verify
before trusting it. Mark which ones you cannot confirm.
Help me think through a difficult people decision. Lay out the possible
approaches, the tradeoffs of each, and questions I should sit with.
Do not decide for me; I own the outcome.

Outputs

What a good response looks like

From the verification prompt, a strong response reads like this:

Claims to verify before you rely on this:

  • "The deadline is 30 days" — I cannot confirm the current rule for your jurisdiction. Check the governing agency's site or ask a professional.
  • "Studies show a 40% improvement" — no source given; treat as unverified until you find the primary study.
  • The cited case name — I may have this wrong or invented it. Confirm it exists in an official reporter before citing it anywhere.

A weaker model answers with breezy confidence and no caveats. That contrast is the whole point: the useful output is the one that helps you check, not the one that sounds sure.

Pitfalls

Risks and common mistakes

Treating fluent as correct

Confidence in the writing tells you nothing about accuracy.

Using AI advice to skip a professional

The model does not carry the liability. You do.

Citing what you did not verify

Fabricated citations have real, documented consequences.

Letting convenience override the boundary

The riskiest moment is when you are busy and the shortcut is tempting.

Assuming a newer, bigger model removed these limits

It reduced some errors. It did not make the model accountable or all-knowing.

Before you ship

Human review checklist

  • This decision is reversible, or I have accepted the risk of it not being.
  • Every fact I am relying on has been verified at a real source.
  • No high-stakes legal, medical, or financial call is being made on the model's say-so.
  • A qualified human owns any decision that could harm a person or expose the business.
  • No confidential or regulated data went anywhere it should not.
  • I can explain and defend this output without saying "the AI wrote it."
