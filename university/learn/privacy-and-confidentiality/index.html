Lesson · Trust & Safety

Privacy and confidentiality basics

Before you paste one more thing into a chat box: here is what the tool keeps, who can see it, and the real gap between a free login and a business contract.

Get Your AI Roadmap Browse all lessons

What this is

A working rule for what goes in the chat box

Every time you paste text into ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot, it leaves your computer and lands on a vendor's servers. Three questions decide whether that is fine or a problem.

Is it stored? For how long? And can it be used to train a future model that other people will use? The answers depend almost entirely on which tier you are on. A personal free login and a paid business plan from the same company can handle your data in completely different ways.

Most people never learn that difference. That is how confidential client details end up in a system nobody at the company controls.

Vendor policies described here were checked in mid-2026. Data terms change often, so treat this as a starting map. Confirm each vendor's current policy at the source before you rely on it.

Who this is for

People who handle other people's data

  • Owners and managers about to let a team use AI tools, who want one clear rule instead of a vague worry.
  • Anyone in a regulated or contract-bound field: healthcare, law, finance, education, or any business that signed confidentiality terms with clients.
  • Solo operators who handle client lists, health details, or financials and want to keep using AI without breaking a promise they made.

When to use it

Run this thinking before anything non-public

  • Before you draft your team's AI usage rule or hand anyone a login.
  • Before pasting a customer record, contract, medical note, financial statement, résumé, or anything with a name attached to a fact.
  • When choosing between a free tool and a paid business tier, so privacy terms drive the decision instead of price.

When NOT to rely on it alone

What this lesson does not replace

This gives you a sensible default. It does not stand in for these.

A signed contract

If your client agreement, HIPAA obligations, or a data processing agreement forbid sending data to third parties, this lesson does not override that. Read the contract.

Legal review of vendor terms

Nothing here is legal advice. RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. For anything high-stakes, have a qualified lawyer read the vendor's data terms and your own obligations.

The assumption that a paid plan is a vault

Paid tiers reduce risk; they do not make a tool private by default. Human reviewers can still see flagged content, and misconfigured settings are common.

Tools

How the tiers actually differ

The pattern is consistent: consumer plans may use your chats to improve models unless you opt out, while business and enterprise plans do not train on your data by default. Retention also drops as you move up.

Simplified comparison, verified mid-2026. Always re-check current terms at each vendor's own privacy page.

One nuance to know: even when a vendor says it does not train on your data, most reserve the right to have a human review content flagged for safety or abuse. Anthropic's mid-2026 policy is explicit that conversations flagged for safety review can be used in ways that override a stated training preference, and it does not spell out what triggers a flag. Treat "not used for training" as strong protection, not an absolute guarantee of secrecy.

Step-by-step workflow

Sort, set the rule, then paste

  1. Sort your data into three buckets. Green: already public, no identifiable person or secret. Yellow: internal but not sensitive (draft copy, generic process notes). Red: any real name tied to health, money, legal status, or a promise of confidentiality.
  2. Set the rule. Green goes anywhere. Yellow goes only into a business or enterprise tier with training off. Red does not go in at all unless a contract and configuration specifically permit it.
  3. Strip before you paste. For yellow items, remove names and identifiers first. "Client A, a 54-year-old" carries far less risk than a real name.
  4. Check the account settings once, in writing. Confirm the tier, the training toggle, and the retention setting. Screenshot it. Settings get changed and reset.
  5. Turn off training and history where you can. On consumer tiers, use data controls and temporary or incognito modes for anything you would not want retained.
  6. Write it down for the team. A one-page rule everyone can follow beats a perfect policy nobody reads.

Example prompts

Copy-pastable starters

Use these to have the tool help build your own policy. None of them contain real confidential data.

You are helping a small business write a plain-language AI usage rule.
Our team uses [ChatGPT Business / Gemini in Workspace]. Draft a one-page
policy covering: what data is never allowed in AI tools, what must be
anonymized first, which tier and settings are required, and who to ask
when unsure. Keep it under 300 words and write it for non-technical staff.
I'm about to paste the text below into an AI tool. Before I do, tell me:
does it contain any personal identifiers, health, financial, or legal
details that would make it risky to send to a third party? List anything
I should remove or mask first. Do not store or repeat the content back.

[paste ONLY non-confidential sample text here to test the process]
Rewrite this internal note so it keeps the useful structure but removes
every name, account number, and detail that could identify a real person
or client. Replace them with generic placeholders like [Client A].

Example output

What the policy prompt might return

AI Usage Rule — [Company]

  1. Never paste into any AI tool: client names tied to health or financial details, Social Security or account numbers, passwords, signed contracts, or anything covered by a confidentiality agreement.
  2. Internal drafts and generic notes are allowed only in our approved Business plan, with model training turned off.
  3. Before pasting anything about a specific person, replace their name with a placeholder.
  4. When unsure, ask [name] before pasting. A five-minute question beats a data leak.

Realistic, but the model may invent settings names or overstate a protection. That is exactly why the review step exists.

Risks & common mistakes

Where this goes wrong

Assuming free equals private

On most consumer tiers, your chats can train the next model unless you opt out. This is the single most common mistake.

Pasting client data to save five minutes

A confidentiality breach is not undone by deleting the chat afterward.

Trusting a browser extension or unknown app

Third-party wrappers may route your text through servers with no clear policy. Prefer the vendor's own app.

Letting the model describe its own privacy terms

Models frequently get their own vendor's policy wrong. Verify at the source, not by asking the chatbot.

Setting the policy once and forgetting it

Vendors update terms and defaults reset. Re-check quarterly.

Human review checklist

Confirm before you paste

  • I confirmed which tier we are actually on, not the one we think we bought.
  • I verified the training and retention settings at the vendor's own page, today.
  • No red-bucket data (names tied to health, money, legal status, or a confidentiality promise) is in this prompt.
  • Yellow-bucket data has been anonymized before pasting.
  • I have written approval where a contract or regulation is involved.
  • The team rule is written in plain language and someone owns questions about it.
