Lesson

How to choose your first AI stack

You don't need ten subscriptions. You need one assistant, one tool that already lives where you work, and maybe one specialist. Here's how to pick, without the hype.

Get Your AI Roadmap Browse all lessons

The idea

What this is

A method for assembling a small, deliberate set of AI tools that fits a small business, instead of collecting subscriptions because a newsletter told you to.

A good starter stack has three slots at most: a general assistant for thinking and drafting, a tool built into the software you already use, and possibly one specialist for a job the first two do poorly. That's the whole thing. Restraint here saves money and, more importantly, saves the attention it takes to actually get good at a tool.

Fit

Who this is for

  • Owners of small businesses and solo operators making their first real AI purchase.
  • Teams already paying for three or four AI tools nobody has learned, who want to cut back to what earns its keep.
  • Anyone stuck on the number of options who wants a decision rule, not a 40-tool comparison spreadsheet.

Timing

When to use it

  • You're about to spend money on AI and want the decision to be intentional.
  • You already run one tool well and are wondering whether to add a second.
  • Your team's tools have crept upward and you want to prune to a stack you can defend.

Guardrails

When not to use it

Three moments where building a stack works against you.

Before you have a real task

Don't build a stack in the abstract. Pick one recurring job that eats your time, then choose a tool for it. Tools chosen without a task go unused.

When your data rules aren't settled

If you handle regulated or confidential data, decide your privacy and tier requirements first. A cheap consumer tool that violates a client agreement is not a bargain.

When you're chasing the newest release

Switching stacks every time a model tops a benchmark burns the time you should spend getting fluent. For a first stack, stability beats novelty.

The three slots

Tools that work well

Match a tool to each slot. You won't fill all three on day one, and that's fine.

Pricing and plan names below were checked mid-2026 and shift often. Confirm current numbers on each vendor's site before you buy.

SlotWhat it doesCommon picks
General assistantDrafting, brainstorming, summarizing, thinking out loudChatGPT (Free, Plus ~$20/mo, or Business), Claude (Free or paid), Gemini
Workspace-nativeAI inside the email, docs, and files you already useMicrosoft 365 Copilot (add-on, ~$30/user/mo) if you're on Microsoft; Gemini in Google Workspace if you're on Google
Specialist (optional)One job the generalists do poorlyPerplexity for sourced research; a meeting-notes tool; a design tool like Canva's AI features

The biggest lever most people miss: if you already pay for Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace, your workspace-native slot is often the highest-value add. It works on your real documents and, at the paid business tier, doesn't train on your data by default. Start there before buying a fourth standalone tool.

Workflow

Step by step

  1. List your top three time-eaters. The recurring tasks that drain hours: email, proposals, notes, research, scheduling.
  2. Pick the one worth automating first. Highest frequency times highest annoyance wins.
  3. Set your data floor. Decide the minimum tier and privacy setting that task requires. Regulated data raises the floor.
  4. Fill the general-assistant slot. Try two free tiers on the same real task for a week. Keep the one whose output you trust and whose interface you enjoy.
  5. Check your workspace-native option. If you're already on Microsoft or Google, test their built-in AI on your actual files before buying anything else.
  6. Add a specialist only if a real gap remains. No gap, no purchase.
  7. Set a 30-day review. If a tool isn't used weekly, cancel it.

Prompts

Example prompts to copy

Use a general assistant to pressure-test your own choices.

I run a [type] business with [N] people. We use [Microsoft 365 / Google
Workspace]. Our three biggest time-drains are [A], [B], [C]. Recommend a
lean AI stack of no more than three tools, explain what each is FOR, and
tell me which ONE to start with this week. Flag anything I'd be paying
for twice.
Compare [Tool A] and [Tool B] for this exact task: [describe task]. Give
me a short table of the tradeoffs that actually matter for a small
business, not a feature dump. End with a one-line recommendation and the
main risk of that choice.
Here's my current list of AI subscriptions and what I use each for:
[list]. Which are redundant? What could I cancel without losing a
capability I actually rely on?

What good looks like

A realistic example output

For a 4-person services firm on Google Workspace:

  • Start here: Gemini in Google Workspace. It already sits in your Gmail and Docs, and at your paid tier it won't train on your data. Use it for email drafts and document cleanup.
  • General assistant: One of Claude or ChatGPT, paid tier, for brainstorming and longer drafting where you want a separate scratchpad.
  • Specialist: Hold off. Add a meeting-notes tool only if you run enough calls to justify it.
  • Skip for now: A second general assistant. Two do the same job; pick one and get fluent.

Treat any specific price or plan name a model gives you as a claim to verify. Models routinely state outdated pricing.

Guardrails

Risks and common mistakes

Buying breadth instead of depth

Three well-used tools beat eight half-learned ones. Depth is where the payoff lives.

Paying twice for the same capability

Your workspace suite may already include what you're about to buy separately. Check before you subscribe.

Ignoring the data tier to save $10

The cheap plan may train on your inputs or violate a client agreement. The savings aren't worth the exposure.

Chasing benchmarks

The model that won this month's leaderboard is rarely worth re-training your team on.

No cancel-by date

Unused subscriptions renew silently. Set a review and hold the line.

Before you commit

Human review checklist

  • Each tool maps to a specific recurring task, not a vague hope.
  • I confirmed I'm not paying twice for a capability already in my suite.
  • The tier and privacy settings meet my data obligations.
  • I verified today's pricing at the vendor's own site.
  • I picked one tool to start with this week, not all of them.
  • A 30-day usage review is on the calendar.
