Lesson

How to verify AI outputs

A fast, repeatable way to check AI answers against primary sources, and why fluent output slips past your judgment so easily.

Get Your AI Roadmap

The basics

What verification is

Verification is the deliberate step of confirming an AI claim against a trustworthy source before you act on it or pass it along. It is a discipline, not a feeling. It has to be a discipline because the natural human response to fluent, confident writing is to believe it, and AI produces fluent, confident writing by design.

Why fluent output lowers your guard

Your brain treats things that are easy to process as more likely to be true. Psychologists call this processing fluency, and it is well documented: text that reads smoothly, in a clean font, in a confident voice, feels more credible than the same claim delivered awkwardly. AI output is tuned to be maximally fluent. It is grammatical, organized, and self-assured, so it presses the exact button that lowers your guard. You are not gullible for trusting it. You are human, and the output is tuned to that humanity. The fix is to move the trust decision out of your gut and into a checklist you run every time. That is what the rest of this lesson gives you.

Fit

Who this is for

  • Anyone publishing, sending, or deciding based on AI output.
  • Teams setting a standard so verification does not depend on who happens to be careful that day.
  • Professionals in fields where a wrong fact carries real consequences.

Timing

When to use it

Verify before anything factual leaves your hands: a claim a customer will read, a number in a proposal, a name or quote attributed to a real person, a citation, a legal or regulatory statement, a statistic. The trigger is simple. If it is a fact and being wrong would cost something, verify it. The higher the stakes, the more independent your source needs to be.

Timing

When to skip it

You do not need to verify subjective or generative work where no external fact exists to check: the tone of a draft email, brainstormed slogan options, a rewrite of your own paragraph. If the output makes no claim about the world, there is nothing to verify, only quality to judge. Spending verification effort on taste questions wastes the effort and dulls the habit for the claims that need it. Reserve verification for facts.

Sources

Tools that work well

To check…Go to…
A law, regulation, or government ruleThe official government site (.gov), the actual statute or agency page
A research finding or statisticGoogle Scholar, the original study, the primary data source
A quote or who-said-whatThe original speech, article, or a reputable outlet reporting it
A product spec, price, or policyThe vendor's own current page
A calculationA spreadsheet or calculator; redo it yourself
A recent eventTwo independent, reputable news sources

Tools that cite sources (Perplexity, search-enabled chat) speed the first step by handing you a link. They do not finish the job. You still open the link and confirm it says what the tool claims, because models sometimes cite a real page that does not actually support the statement.

Star of the lesson

Human review checklist

Run this every time before a factual output leaves your hands.

  • ✓ Did I list every checkable claim, not just the ones that jumped out?
  • ✓ Did I go to a primary source rather than another AI or a secondhand summary?
  • ✓ For each citation, did I confirm both that it exists and that it supports the claim?
  • ✓ Did I redo any math myself?
  • ✓ Did I flag, not bury, anything I could not verify?
  • ✓ Did I resist the pull to trust it just because it read well?

Workflow

Step by step

  1. Separate facts from framing. Underline every checkable claim: names, numbers, dates, citations, quotes, rules.
  2. Rank by stakes. Which of these, if wrong, actually hurt? Check those hardest.
  3. Go to the primary source, not another summary. The government page, the study, the original document.
  4. Confirm the source exists and says it. For citations, this is two checks: does the source exist, and does it support the claim?
  5. Redo any math yourself. Do not trust arithmetic done in the model's head.
  6. Note what you could not verify. Flag it clearly rather than letting an unverified claim ride along as if confirmed.
  7. Only then act, send, or publish.

Prompts

Example prompts

Enlist the model in its own verification. Its answer is a starting point, not proof.

For every factual claim, citation, statistic, and proper name in your
previous answer, add a bracketed confidence note: [high], [medium],
or [low], and say what a human should check to confirm it. Do not
soften low-confidence items.
You cited a source. Quote the exact sentence from that source that
supports the claim. If you cannot quote it, say so plainly and tell
me the claim is unverified.

This surfaces the shaky parts fast. It does not replace opening the source yourself.

Example

What a good self-audit looks like

Asked to self-audit, a well-behaved model flags its own weak points:

"The three-year retention rule [high, from federal labor regulations, confirm on dol.gov]. The claim that penalties start at $1,100 per violation [low, I'm not confident in this exact figure, verify on the agency's penalty schedule]. The 2019 date I gave [medium, check the agency's amendment history]."

That output does not end your work. It tells you where to point it. You open dol.gov, confirm the retention rule, and either fix or delete the penalty figure the model flagged as low confidence. The unverified number never reaches your customer.

Pitfalls

Risks and common mistakes

Trusting fluency

The core trap. Smooth prose feels true, and that feeling is not evidence.

Verifying with the same tool that made the claim

Asking the model "are you sure?" often just yields a new confident answer. Go outside the model.

Accepting a citation without opening it

A link is not a fact-check. The page may not say what the tool claims, or may not exist.

Checking a summary against another summary

Go to the primary source, or you are just comparing two guesses.

Verifying only the obvious claims

The dangerous errors are the plausible ones you did not think to question.
