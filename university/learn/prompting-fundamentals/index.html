Lesson

Prompting fundamentals

Most bad AI output comes from thin prompts, not weak models. Learn the five moves that reliably raise quality: role, context, examples, structure, and iteration.

Get Your AI Roadmap

Foundations

What this is

A prompt is the instruction you give an AI model. Good prompting is the skill of describing a task so clearly that a capable but literal assistant, one with no memory of your world, produces what you actually wanted on the first or second try. The published guidance from the major labs converges on a small set of techniques. This lesson covers the ones that pay off for everyday business work.

The core idea: the model cannot read your mind, and it will not ask for missing details unless you invite it to. Everything you leave unsaid, it either omits or invents. Good prompting is mostly the discipline of saying the unsaid things.

The five moves

MoveWhat it does
RoleTells the model the perspective to write from ("You are a careful bookkeeper explaining to a first-time owner").
ContextSupplies the facts it needs: your audience, your constraints, the source material.
ExamplesShows one or two samples of the output you want. Often the single highest-leverage move.
StructureSpecifies format, length, and sections. Use clear delimiters to separate instructions from data.
IterationTreats the first output as a draft and refines with specific feedback.

Fit

Who this is for and when to use it

Who this is for

  • Anyone whose AI results feel generic, off-tone, or wrong, and who suspects the problem is the prompt.
  • Team members who write prompts other people rely on and want a repeatable method.
  • Owners building templates so the whole team gets consistent output.

When to use it

Use these techniques on any task you will repeat, any output that must match a specific tone or format, and any request where a vague prompt already gave you a disappointing result. The payoff scales with repetition: a well-built prompt you reuse fifty times is worth the ten minutes it takes to write carefully.

When not to use it

For a quick one-off ("what's a synonym for reliable?"), an elaborate prompt is wasted effort. Just ask. And no amount of prompting fixes a task the model fundamentally cannot do. If the job requires a guaranteed-correct fact the model does not have, or precise math, better wording will not make an invented answer true. Prompting improves how well the model does what it can do. It does not extend what it can do.

Tools

Tools that work well

These techniques transfer across every major assistant: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity. Two practical notes.

First, Claude and ChatGPT both respond well to clear structure and delimiters, and both vendors publish prompt guides worth reading (Anthropic's prompt engineering documentation and OpenAI's prompting guidance). Second, "Projects" or "custom instructions" features let you store role and context once so you do not repeat them every message. Use them for anything recurring.

Method

Step-by-step workflow

  1. State the role and goal in one line. Who is the model being, and what is the single outcome?
  2. Paste the context and source material, clearly separated. Use a label like SOURCE: or triple quotes so the model knows what is data versus instruction.
  3. Add one or two examples of the output you want, if you have them. This anchors tone and format better than description alone.
  4. Specify structure: format, length, sections, and what to leave out.
  5. Invite honesty: tell it to ask questions or flag uncertainty rather than guess.
  6. Read the output as a draft. Give specific feedback ("tighten the second paragraph; the tone is too formal") and run again.
  7. Save the winner as a reusable template with [brackets] for the parts that change.

Examples

Example prompts

A thin prompt versus a built one, same task.

Thin: "Write a cancellation email."

Built:

ROLE: You are a warm but professional customer-service lead for a
small yoga studio.

TASK: Write an email to a member who is canceling their membership.
Goal: keep the door open without pressure.

CONTEXT: Member's name is Priya. She's been with us 14 months. No
complaint on record; she mentioned moving. Our policy: cancellation
takes effect at the end of the current billing cycle. We offer a
"pause" option for up to 3 months.

FORMAT: Under 130 words. No discount offers. Mention the pause option
once, gently. End with a genuine thank-you.

If you're missing any fact you need, ask me instead of inventing it.

A structure-and-delimiter example for working over source text:

Rewrite the SOURCE below as three bullet points a busy owner can scan.
Keep every number exactly as written. Do not add claims.

SOURCE:
"""
[paste the raw text here]
"""

Results

Example outputs

The thin prompt returns a generic template full of blanks and a tone that could belong to any company. The built prompt returns something close to send-ready:

"Hi Priya, thank you for letting us know, and for the fourteen months you spent practicing with us. Your cancellation will take effect at the end of your current billing cycle, so nothing changes before then. If your move turns out to be temporary, we can pause your membership for up to three months instead, whenever you like. Either way, you're always welcome back on the mat. Wishing you a smooth move. Warmly, The Studio Team"

The difference is not a smarter model. It is a prompt that supplied the role, the facts, the format, and permission to ask rather than guess.

Pitfalls

Risks and common mistakes

Leaving out the facts and hoping

Unstated details get invented. Supply them.

Mixing instructions and data

Without delimiters, the model may treat your source text as a command. Separate them clearly.

Over-stuffing one prompt

Ten competing instructions produce mush. Prioritize, then iterate for the rest.

Prompting a factual claim into existence

Asking confidently does not make an answer true. Verify facts regardless of prompt quality.

Not saving what works

A great prompt you retype from memory each time is a great prompt you will slowly ruin. Template it.

Before you send

Human review checklist

  • Did I give a role, the real context, and a clear format?
  • Are instructions and source data separated so the model can tell them apart?
  • Did I include an example when tone or format matters?
  • Did I invite the model to ask questions or flag uncertainty instead of guessing?
  • Did I still verify every fact in the output, since good prompting does not guarantee truth?
  • Did I save the working prompt as a reusable template?
