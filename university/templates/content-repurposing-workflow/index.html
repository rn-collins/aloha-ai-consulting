Content Repurposing Workflow

You already made the hard thing — a blog post, a talk, a webinar. This workflow turns that one pillar into a week of assets across email, LinkedIn, video, and more, using a repeatable map and six copy-paste prompts. You still write the voice; the draft just starts faster.

Get the workflow Get Your AI Roadmap

What this is

A two-part template. The first part is a map: one pillar asset in, a set of derivative assets out, with the angle, length, and voice notes spelled out for each. The second part is a prompt pack — six real prompts you paste into the AI tool you already use, each with a "when to use" line and blanks you fill in.

The point is not more content for its own sake. It's getting the full return on work you already did, without letting a machine flatten how you sound or invent facts you'll have to walk back.

Who it's for

  • Solo operators and small teams who publish one good thing a week and want it to travel further.
  • Marketers tired of staring at a blank post when the source material is right there.
  • Founders and experts who write well but don't have time to reformat the same idea five ways by hand.

What's inside

  • A one-source-to-many-assets map you can reuse every week.
  • A brand voice card you fill in once and paste into every prompt.
  • Six copy-paste prompts, each with a title and a "when to use" line.
  • A human-check step for every asset, so nothing goes out unverified or off-brand.

How to use it

  1. Pick your pillar. One substantial asset — a blog post, a recorded talk, a webinar. It needs enough substance to break into pieces.
  2. Fill the voice card once. Describe how you sound. You'll paste it into every prompt so the drafts start in your register, not the tool's.
  3. Extract the core ideas. Run prompt (a) to pull 3-5 ideas and the best quotable lines. Everything downstream builds on one idea at a time.
  4. Generate the derivatives. Work down the map. Use prompts (b) through (e) for each format; use (f) whenever a draft sounds generic.
  5. Human-check, then publish. Verify every claim and link, confirm nothing is fabricated or off-brand, and only then schedule.

Part 2 · The prompts

Fill the voice card once and paste it into every prompt where you see [brand voice]. Each prompt has its own Copy button. Replace anything in [brackets] before you run it.

Your brand voice card (fill once, paste everywhere)

When to use: Fill this in one time, then paste it into any prompt below that references [brand voice].

BRAND VOICE CARD
Who we are: [one line — what you do and for whom]
How we sound: [3-5 adjectives, e.g. warm, plain, direct, no hype]
Words we use: [terms/phrases that sound like us]
Words we avoid: [buzzwords, clichés, anything that sounds generic or AI-written]
Reading level: [e.g. clear enough for a busy owner skimming on a phone]
Point of view: [first person "I/we" or brand voice]
One-line example of our voice: [paste a sentence you're proud of]

a) Extract the core ideas and quotable lines

When to use: First, right after you pick your pillar. Everything else builds on this.

Here is a pillar piece of content: [paste the blog post, transcript, or webinar notes].

Read it closely and give me:
1. The 3-5 core ideas, each as one plain sentence a reader would remember.
2. For each idea, the single best quotable line already in the text (exact words, no paraphrasing).
3. Any claim, number, or link in the piece that I should fact-check before I reuse it.

Do not invent ideas or quotes that aren't in the source. If something is unclear, flag it instead of guessing.

b) Turn one idea into 3 LinkedIn posts

When to use: Once you've picked one idea from prompt (a) and want three distinct posts, not three versions of the same one.

[brand voice]

Take this one idea: [paste one core idea and its quotable line].

Write 3 distinct LinkedIn posts from it, each a genuinely different angle:
1. A story — a short, specific moment that makes the point.
2. A how-to — a concrete, numbered or stepped takeaway.
3. A contrarian take — the common belief, then why it's wrong here.

Rules: 120-220 words each. One idea per post. No hashtag walls, no "thrilled to announce," no filler. Sound like the voice card above. Keep any factual claim I can verify; if you're unsure of a fact, mark it [CHECK].

c) Write a newsletter section

When to use: When you want an email section that expands one idea and drives readers back to the pillar.

[brand voice]

Write a newsletter section built around this idea: [paste one core idea]. It should link back to the full pillar here: [paste pillar URL].

Give me:
- A subject line (under 55 characters).
- Preview text (under 90 characters) that isn't a repeat of the subject.
- A 200-350 word section that expands the idea, adds one concrete example, and ends with a clear link back to the pillar.

Write it like an email from a person, not a broadcast. Keep any fact I can check; flag anything you're unsure of with [CHECK].

d) Draft a 30-60s short-video script

When to use: When you want a spoken script for a short reel or TikTok from one idea.

[brand voice]

Write a 30-60 second short-form video script from this idea: [paste one core idea].

Structure:
- HOOK (first 3 seconds): one line that earns the next 5 seconds. No clickbait, no exaggeration I can't back up.
- 3 BEATS: three short spoken points that build the idea.
- CTA: one clear next step (watch the full piece, comment, follow).

Write it to be spoken, not read — short sentences, natural rhythm, ~90-150 words total. Mark any factual claim I should verify with [CHECK].

e) Repurpose into an X / Twitter thread

When to use: When one idea deserves a threaded argument rather than a single post.

[brand voice]

Turn this idea into an X/Twitter thread: [paste one core idea and its quotable line].

Give me:
- 1 hook tweet that makes someone stop scrolling (no "a thread 🧵" cliché unless it truly fits).
- 5-7 follow-on tweets, one thought each, that build the argument.
- A final tweet with a clear CTA or link back to the pillar: [paste pillar URL].

Keep each tweet under 280 characters. Short lines. No hype words. Flag any claim I should verify with [CHECK].

f) Adapt tone and fix generic drafts

When to use: Any time a draft reads flat, generic, or obviously machine-written.

[brand voice: warm, plain, no hype]

Here is a draft: [paste the draft].

Rewrite it to match the brand voice card above. Specifically:
- Cut anything that sounds generic, corporate, or AI-written (empty openers, "in today's fast-paced world," "unlock," "seamless," "game-changer," "leverage," "delve").
- Keep every fact and link exactly as written — do not add claims or numbers that weren't there.
- Prefer plain words and short sentences. Say the real thing.

Then list, in one or two lines, what you changed and why — so I can spot patterns and edit the next draft myself.

A note on the human check

Every asset in the map has a human-check column for a reason. Before anything publishes: fact-check the claims, verify the links actually go where they should, and confirm nothing is fabricated or off-brand. AI is happy to invent a statistic or a quote that reads perfectly and never existed. The person hitting publish owns what it says — not the tool.

Before you publish: AI-repurposed content can drift from the facts, invent statistics or quotes, or flatten your voice into something generic. A human verifies every claim and link, every time, before it goes out. Don't republish someone else's content as your own — repurpose only what you made or have the right to use. This is general education, not legal advice; check your own obligations before you publish.
