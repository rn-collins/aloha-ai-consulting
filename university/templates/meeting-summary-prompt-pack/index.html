Meeting Summary Prompt Pack

A transcript is not a summary. These eight copy-paste prompts take the raw, rambling record of a meeting and hand back the parts that matter: a clean recap, a decision log, and action items with a named owner and a due date — and nothing the room never actually said.

Get the prompts Get Your AI Roadmap

What this is

Eight prompts you paste into whatever AI tool you already use, each doing one job in the flow from raw transcript to shared follow-up. Start with the master summary, then reach for the extractors, the email draft, or the privacy pass depending on what you need. Every prompt carries the same rule: work only from what's in the transcript, and flag anything unclear instead of filling the gap with a plausible guess.

You bring the transcript — from your notetaker, your recorder's auto-caption, or notes you typed live. The prompts bring the structure. The one thing they can't bring is a human who confirms the owners actually agreed to their tasks. That part stays yours.

Who it's for

  • Whoever got stuck writing up the meeting: the organizer, the chief-of-staff, the person who happened to hit record.
  • Managers who run a lot of standing meetings and want the recap out the door in minutes, not the next morning.
  • Teams tired of the "wait, who was doing that?" message three days after every call.

What's inside

  • A master summary prompt that produces a TL;DR, decisions, action items, open questions, and risks in one pass.
  • Focused extractors for action items, decisions, chase lists, and a leadership-ready one-paragraph version.
  • A follow-up email draft prompt and a gap-finder that tells you exactly what the transcript left ambiguous.
  • A redaction pass to catch anything sensitive before you hit send, plus a short worked example so you can see the shape of good output.

How to use it

  1. Get a transcript. Any format works — timestamps and speaker labels help, but plain text is fine. Clean up obvious garble if you can; you don't have to.
  2. Run the master summary first. Paste your transcript where each prompt says [paste transcript here], and read what comes back before you trust it.
  3. Reach for a focused prompt when you need one. Want just the action items as a table? Just the decisions? Just an email? Grab the matching prompt below.
  4. Run the gap-finder, then verify. It surfaces every missing name, date, or owner. Fill those in yourself, and confirm each owner actually agreed before you assign them anything.
  5. Do a privacy pass before sharing widely. Especially if the meeting touched money, people, or anything under NDA.

The prompts

Eight prompts, each in its own box with its own Copy button. Replace the [bracketed] placeholders with your own text. They are written to be strict about invention on purpose — an AI would rather give you a tidy, confident answer than an honest "the transcript doesn't say," so each prompt pushes hard in the other direction.

1. Master summary

When to use: your first pass on any transcript — the one prompt that gives you the whole picture.

You are summarizing a meeting from its transcript. Use ONLY what appears in the transcript below. Do not add, infer, or smooth over anything. If a detail (a name, a date, an owner, a decision) is missing or unclear, write [UNCLEAR] in its place rather than guessing.

Produce the summary in exactly these sections:

TL;DR
- Three bullets, one line each, capturing what this meeting was actually about and what changed as a result.

DECISIONS MADE
- List each concrete decision the group reached. If nothing was decided, write "No decisions recorded."

ACTION ITEMS
- One line each, formatted: Owner — Task — Due date
- If the owner or due date was never stated, write [UNCLEAR] in that slot.

OPEN QUESTIONS
- Anything raised but not resolved.

RISKS / FLAGS
- Concerns, blockers, or dependencies mentioned. If none, write "None raised."

2. Action items as a table

When to use: you only need the "who does what by when," and you want it clean enough to paste into a doc or tracker.

From the transcript below, extract every action item — a task someone committed to or was assigned. Use only what the transcript states.

Return a table with these columns:
Owner | Task | Due | Source quote

- Owner: the person responsible. If never named, write [UNCLEAR].
- Task: the specific thing to do, in a few words.
- Due: the stated deadline. If none was given, write [UNCLEAR].
- Source quote: the short line from the transcript where this was said, so I can verify it.

Do not invent tasks that were only vaguely gestured at. If someone said "we should probably look into that" with no owner, list it with [UNCLEAR] owners rather than skipping or assigning it.

3. Decision log

When to use: you need a record of what was actually decided and why — for the wiki, the board update, or your own memory.

From the transcript below, build a decision log. A decision is a choice the group actually settled on — not a topic discussed, not an option floated and dropped.

Return a table with these columns:
Decision | Rationale | Who decided | Date

- Rationale: the reason given in the meeting, if any. If none was stated, write [UNCLEAR].
- Who decided: the person or group who made the call. If unclear, write [UNCLEAR].
- Date: use the meeting date if that's the only date available: [meeting date].

If a discussion ended without a clear decision, do not manufacture one. It's fine to return only the decisions that were genuinely made.

4. Follow-up email to attendees

When to use: right after the meeting, when you want to send everyone a recap that reminds each person of their own commitments.

Draft a short, professional follow-up email to the people who attended this meeting. Base it only on the transcript below — do not add commitments or details that weren't discussed.

The email should:
- Open with one or two sentences on what the meeting covered and any key decisions.
- List action items grouped so each attendee can find their own, in the form "Name — task — due date."
- Where an owner or date is missing, write [UNCLEAR] so I can fill it in before sending, rather than you guessing.
- Close with a brief, friendly line inviting corrections.

Keep it concise and warm — the tone of a helpful colleague, not a legal memo. Sign it from [your name].

5. "Who owns what" chase list

When to use: you're the organizer and need a person-by-person view of who you're waiting on.

From the transcript below, build a chase list for the meeting organizer, grouped by person. For each person who took on any commitment, list their name as a heading, then their tasks beneath it with due dates.

Format:
[Person's name]
  - Task — Due date
  - Task — Due date

Rules:
- Use only what the transcript states. Missing owner or date → [UNCLEAR].
- Add a final group titled "UNASSIGNED" for any task with no clear owner, so nothing falls through the cracks.
- Do not assign a task to someone unless the transcript shows they accepted it.

6. Ambiguity & gap finder

When to use: before you trust any summary — this tells you exactly what a human needs to fill in.

Read the transcript below and act as a careful editor whose only job is to find gaps. Do NOT summarize. Do NOT fix anything. Just list every place where a name, a date, an owner, or a decision is missing, vague, or ambiguous, so a human can resolve it.

For each gap, give:
- What's unclear (e.g., "owner of the pricing task")
- The nearby transcript line where it comes up
- What question a human should ask to resolve it

Be thorough — it's better to flag a borderline case than to let a wrong assumption slip into the summary. If the transcript is actually clear and complete, say so plainly.

7. Executive one-paragraph version

When to use: a leader who wasn't in the room needs the gist in fifteen seconds.

Write a single paragraph — no more than five sentences — summarizing this meeting for [leader's name/role], who was not present and has limited time.

Cover: what the meeting was about, the most important decision or outcome, and anything that needs their attention or approval. Use plain language, no jargon, no bullet points.

Base it only on the transcript below. If something a leader would want to know is genuinely unclear from the transcript, say so in the paragraph rather than inventing it.

8. Redaction & privacy pass

When to use: right before you share the summary beyond the people who were in the room.

Review the meeting summary or transcript below and act as a privacy reviewer. Your job is to flag — not remove — anything that probably should not leave the room before it's shared more widely.

Flag and list:
- Named individuals whose identity may be sensitive in this context (personnel issues, performance, health, legal).
- Financial figures, pricing, salaries, or commercial terms.
- Anything that sounds confidential, under NDA, or legally sensitive.
- Off-the-record asides or "don't repeat this" moments.

For each flag, quote the relevant line and suggest whether to remove it, generalize it, or keep it only for a smaller audience. Do not rewrite the document — just give me the list so I can decide.

See the shape of good output

Here is a short raw transcript snippet and the clean action-item table it should turn into — so you know what "good" looks like before you run your own.

Raw transcript snippet

[00:12] Priya: I'll get the revised budget over to finance by Friday.
[00:14] Marcus: Good. And someone needs to loop in legal on the vendor contract.
[00:15] Priya: Dana, can you take that? Maybe by end of next week?
[00:15] Dana: Yep, I'll reach out to legal — end of next week works.
OwnerTaskDueSource quote
PriyaSend revised budget to financeFriday"I'll get the revised budget over to finance by Friday."
DanaLoop in legal on the vendor contractEnd of next week"I'll reach out to legal — end of next week works."

Notice what the table did not do: it didn't turn Marcus's "someone needs to loop in legal" into an action item on its own, because that line had no owner — Dana only became the owner once she accepted. That's the difference between reading the transcript and inventing from it.

Before you send anything out: AI models invent owners, dates, and even whole decisions that were never said — confidently and in clean formatting, which makes the invention easy to miss. Always read the summary against the transcript, and confirm each owner actually agreed to their task before you assign it. Don't paste confidential transcripts — anything with client names, financials, personnel matters, or NDA'd material — into consumer AI tiers where your text may be used for training; use a private or approved tool for those. This is general education, not legal advice; check your own obligations before you move sensitive material anywhere.
