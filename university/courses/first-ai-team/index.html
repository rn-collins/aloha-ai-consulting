Why start here

The gap is not knowledge. It is permission to begin.

You have watched people build with AI and felt behind. The block is rarely the concepts — it is not knowing where to start, and fear of doing it wrong. This course removes both. You pick one workflow, build a small team around it, and finish the weekend with a working system and your own hands on the controls.

It is deliberately small. That is the point. A tiny team you actually understand beats a sprawling one you cannot govern. When you outgrow it, the deeper courses are waiting.

The artifact you keep

A named team is cute. The contract under the name is the product.

Anyone can give agents fun names. What makes a team usable — for a founder, a clinic, a law practice — is the contract under each name: what it may touch, what it may never do, and what it must route to you.

Curriculum

A weekend, in four build-blocks

You do not watch and forget. You leave each block with a working piece of your team.

  1. See your work as a system. Pick one workflow and map it — trigger, inputs, steps, decisions, output, accountability. Clarity before agents.
  2. Decide what AI should and shouldn't do. Sort every step into off-limits, augmentation, or automation, and set the rule that nothing consequential happens without your approval.
  3. Design the team and write role contracts. Choose your agents, give each one a job and a boundary, and write the one-line contract that governs it. Name them last.
  4. Build it, run it once, and know when to level up. Build one agent at a time, wire the approval step, run the whole team on a real task, and learn the signals that mean it is time for a deeper build.

Tutor · demo

Should I let an agent send the emails automatically?
Not yet. At this stage, sending is a consequential action, so it stays behind your approval. Let the team draft up to "ready to send," then you sign off. You can revisit automation later, once the workflow is low-stakes and well-tested.Source: Decide what AI should and shouldn't do
Can one agent just do my whole job?
No — and I would not build it to. An agent does bounded tasks; a job is tasks plus judgment, relationships, and accountability. Give each agent one job and a boundary, and keep yourself as the owner who approves what matters.Source: Design the team and role contracts
