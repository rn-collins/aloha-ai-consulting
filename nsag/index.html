NSAG · Nervous-System-Aware Governance

Institutions shape how people think, trust, and decide.

The advisory practice for nervous-system-aware institutional design and governance.

Law, healthcare, technology, cities, and brands run on architecture, procedures, communications, and technology, and every one of those choices lands on a human nervous system. Most systems fail at that point: they overwhelm people, confuse stakeholders, erode trust, or ask for behavior change without supporting the people expected to change. NSAG is a research-and-advisory practice that designs institutions, policies, and environments around how people actually process information and decide to trust. The method is a 14-dimension measurement framework, applied across fifteen modules spanning legal and justice, healthcare and clinical, civic and built-environment, and space and extreme-environment work.

The thesis

Why a rule on paper is not the same as a rule people follow

Most governance is designed for a reader who does not exist: a calm, fully informed person weighing options at leisure. Real people meet rules while stressed, uncertain, and scanning for threat. Whether they comply, trust, and adopt is decided in the body first.

NSAG starts from a claim I keep proving out in regulated work: adoption, compliance, and legitimacy are behavioral before they are technical. A person under threat narrows attention, discounts the future, and reads ambiguity as danger. Ask that same nervous system to trust a new policy, a new tool, or a new institution, and the paper design of the rule matters far less than whether the person feels safe enough to engage with it.

So NSAG treats governance as a design problem for the nervous system. It asks how a space, a process, a disclosure, or an institution can lower threat and raise the felt sense of safety, so people can actually understand what is being asked and decide it is safe to act on. Each module below applies that same lens to a different domain where the stakes are high and the trust is fragile.

The through-line

Three ideas run through every module

Safety comes first
A person who does not feel safe cannot learn, trust, or comply. Governance has to earn regulation of the nervous system before it asks for anything else.
Design for the body, not the memo
Environments, processes, and disclosures are read by the body as threat or safety cues. Those cues are designable, and usually left to chance.
One lens, many domains
Law, cities, healthcare, and civic life differ in surface, not in mechanism. The same nervous-system-aware method carries across all of them.

The framework

A 14-dimension framework, fifteen modules

One 14-dimension measurement framework carries across all fifteen modules. Each module takes the nervous-system-aware lens into a domain where trust is fragile and the cost of getting it wrong is real.

M1
Trauma-Informed Legal Space

Designing legal settings so people under stress can understand, participate, and act, instead of shutting down.

M2
AI Legal Navigation

Guiding people through legal systems with AI built around comprehension and trust, not just retrieval.

M3
Psychedelic Harm-Reduction Governance

Governance for psychedelic settings grounded in safety, consent, and how the nervous system responds under altered states.

M4
Cannabis Public-Health Infrastructure

Building cannabis policy and public-health structures around real behavior and harm reduction rather than stigma.

M5
Biophilic Civic Infrastructure

Civic spaces designed with biophilic, salutogenic principles so the built environment itself lowers threat.

M6
Ethical Civic Sponsorship

Structuring sponsorship of civic life so influence is transparent and public trust is protected rather than eroded.

M7
Conscious Cities

Applying neuroscience and environmental psychology to urban design so cities support how people feel, focus, and connect.

M8
Burnout Recovery Infrastructure

Institutional structures that treat burnout as a nervous-system problem and build recovery into how work is governed.

M9
Cannabis Healthcare Destigmatization

Reworking how cannabis is discussed and governed in healthcare so patients and clinicians can engage without shame.

M10
Healthcare Built Environment

Governing hospital acoustics, lighting, single-room standards, and nature access so the physical setting supports patient recovery and staff wellbeing.

M11
Medical Technology & Evidence Standards

Governance for clinical AI and medical technology, so tools are inventoried, tested for demographic bias, and kept accountable after adoption.

M12
Nervous-System-Aware Education Systems

Designing school environments, schedules, and belonging around how students and teachers actually process safety, focus, and stress.

M13
Traditional & Complementary Medicine Governance

Governing how integrative and traditional medicine is adopted, with evidence standards, cultural sovereignty, and patient safety built into the decision.

M14
Space Governance & Interplanetary Jurisdiction

Governance for crewed spaceflight covering jurisdiction, crew rights, medical authority, and commercial accountability beyond Earth.

M15
Isolation, Confinement & Extreme Environment Governance

Governance for isolated and confined settings, from long-term care to correctional units, built around psychological safety, autonomy, and predictability.

Sister practices

NSAG and Aloha AI

NSAG and Aloha AI are sibling practices under RN Collins LLC. They share one lens, that systems land on a human nervous system and should be designed for it, but they sell different value to different buyers, and they are kept as separate storefronts. Shared DNA, separate doors.

The decision rule is simple. If the value is AI as the solution, that is Aloha AI. If the value is institutional design, governance, space, harm reduction, or burnout, that is NSAG. A trauma-informed legal space and an AI rollout people actually adopt draw on the same nervous-system-aware method, and the two practices cross-reference each other, but neither sits underneath the other and neither is a service of the other.

Scope & limits

What this is, and what it is not

NSAG is a governance framework and a body of thinking. It is presented here as method and philosophy, not as advice for any specific situation.

The modules describe an approach to designing governance around how humans process safety and trust. Applying any of it to a real legal, clinical, or civic decision calls for the licensed professionals who carry that responsibility.

Clear guardrails

  • RN Collins is a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney. Nothing here is legal advice, and nothing creates an attorney–client relationship.
  • Nothing here is medical, financial, or tax advice.
  • NSAG is presented as a governance framework and method, to support the licensed professionals who make the calls.
