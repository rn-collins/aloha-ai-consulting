The Policy & Advocacy Stack, built on sources a committee can check.

Advocacy runs on credibility. One wrong statutory citation in written testimony and the whole brief is suspect. A working AI stack for a policy shop separates the layers: a monitoring layer that watches the bills, a drafting layer for testimony and briefings, a research layer for coalition work, and a public-education layer that turns all of it into something a supporter can act on. Here is that stack, and the real tools behind each layer.

Layer by layer

What each layer does, and where AI breaks

For every layer: the job, where AI earns real time, where it fails in ways that damage credibility, and the live monitors and tools that cover it.

Layer 01

Legislative monitoring

The job. Track bills, rulemakings, and agency actions across the jurisdictions you work in, and know what moved this week before an opponent does.

Where AI helps. Summarizing a long bill, clustering which measures touch your issue, and drafting a plain-language what-changed digest an analyst verifies.

Where it fails. General models miss newly filed bills, misread effective dates, and confidently describe a version of a bill that was already amended. Coverage has to run off primary sources, not model memory.

Maps tothe Regulatory & Market Intelligence service, which runs standing primary-source monitors, plus Regulatory Bill Analyzer to read a single bill into issue tags, affected stakeholders, and compliance triggers.

Layer 02

Testimony & briefing support

The job. Turn a position into written testimony, a one-page briefing, or talking points that hold up under questioning from staff and members.

Where AI helps. First drafts from an outline, tightening for length, adapting one argument for several audiences, and building a questions-you-will-be-asked list.

Where it fails. Invented citations and misquoted statutes are the fastest way to lose a hearing. A model will also soften or overstate a legal claim in ways a careful reader catches.

Maps toRegulatory Bill Analyzer to ground the analysis in the actual text, and the Regulatory & Market Intelligence service to keep the underlying facts sourced and dated.

Layer 03

Coalition research

The job. Map who else is working the issue, what the opposition is arguing, and where the evidence base actually stands, so the coalition speaks with one accurate voice.

Where AI helps. Synthesizing across many sources, drafting shared research memos, and spotting gaps and contradictions in a stack of positions.

Where it fails. Fabricated studies and misattributed statistics spread fast inside a coalition and are hard to walk back once a partner has repeated them publicly.

Maps tothe Regulatory & Market Intelligence service for sourced, dated research streams the coalition can share, with Regulatory Bill Analyzer for shared reads of specific measures.

Layer 04

Public-education outputs

The job. Translate the policy work into explainers, social content, and supporter materials that are accurate and still land with a non-expert audience.

Where AI helps. Reformatting one brief into many outputs, adjusting reading level, and drafting supporter-facing copy from an approved source document.

Where it fails. Oversimplification that crosses into inaccuracy, and claims that drift from the sourced position as content is reshaped for reach. The public version has to trace back to the same facts.

Maps tothe Regulatory & Market Intelligence service as the sourced spine for every output, and Content & Adoption Systems to build a repeatable, on-message production process.
