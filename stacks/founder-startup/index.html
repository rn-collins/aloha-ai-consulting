Layer by layer

What each layer does, and where AI breaks

For every layer: the job, where AI earns real time for a small team, where it fails in ways that cost money, and the tools and services that cover it.

Layer 01

Policy monitoring

The job. Know which rules, agencies, and pending changes actually touch your product, without a full-time compliance hire watching them.

Where AI helps. Summarizing a rule, flagging what changed, and clustering which changes matter to your specific product and market.

Where it fails. General models miss recent changes, misread effective dates, and reassure you that something is fine when it is not. Monitoring has to run off primary sources, not model memory.

Maps tothe Regulatory & Market Intelligence service for standing primary-source monitors, plus Regulatory Bill Analyzer to read a specific bill or rule into triggers that affect you.

Layer 02

Product-claim review

The job. Catch a medical, therapeutic, or unsubstantiated marketing claim before it ships on the site, the label, or an ad.

Where AI helps. Scanning copy for high-risk language, flagging claims by regulator zone, and drafting safer alternatives your team and counsel review.

Where it fails. A model will wave through a claim that a regulator would not, or invent a substantiation standard that does not exist. This layer flags risk; it does not replace a lawyer signing off.

Maps toRegulated Claims Checker, which flags medical, therapeutic, substantiation, and consumer-protection claims by risk level and regulator zone, backed by the Regulatory & Market Intelligence service.

Layer 03

Internal knowledge management

The job. Stop re-answering the same questions and losing decisions in chat threads, so a new hire can find how things work.

Where AI helps. Retrieval over your own docs, drafting SOPs from how the team actually works, and answering internal questions with a source pointer.

Where it fails. Overbuilding a heavy system a five-person team cannot maintain, or letting the model answer from general training instead of your real playbook.

Maps toKnowledge Base Readiness Checker to get a straight answer on folders vs. Notion vs. a base vs. a custom app, plus AI Workflow Audit to find where handoffs leak.

Layer 04

Customer education

The job. Turn how the product works into onboarding, help content, and explainers that reduce support load and build trust.

Where AI helps. Drafting help articles from source docs, reformatting one explainer into many, and adjusting reading level for different users.

Where it fails. Confident wrong instructions and claims that drift past what the product actually does. Customer-facing content has to trace back to something true.

Maps tothe Regulatory & Market Intelligence and Regulated Claims Checker pairing to keep outward claims accurate, and Content & Adoption Systems to build a repeatable production process.

Layer 05

Diligence prep

The job. Have the documentation, policies, and answers ready when an investor, partner, or acquirer starts asking hard questions.

Where AI helps. Drafting policy documents, assembling a data-room checklist, and turning scattered decisions into a coherent written record.

Where it fails. Fabricated specifics in a document that a diligence process will test, and governance claims the company cannot actually back up. This is where a real audit trail matters.

Maps toAI Workflow Audit to score and document your AI workflow across six dimensions, with the Regulatory & Market Intelligence service where the diligence turns on regulatory posture.
