The story

One question, followed all the way down

Whether an idea takes hold has almost nothing to do with how true it is, and almost everything to do with how a person receives it. That question has organized every step since.

  1. Shanghai · the classroom

    My first classroom was in Shanghai. For three years I built early-education curricula and taught, and the real lesson was the one I was learning, not teaching: whether an idea takes hold has almost nothing to do with how true it is, and almost everything to do with how a person receives it. Whether they understand it, remember it, and decide it's safe to act on. That question has organized everything I've done since.

  2. The lab · where the question lives

    So I went looking for where the question actually lives, which is the brain. My master's is in Anatomy & Neurobiology from Boston University School of Medicine. My thesis measured how chronic stress and discrimination leave a physical mark on memory structures in the aging brain. Neuroscience taught me that trust and adoption are not soft ideas. They are mechanisms, and you can design for them or design against them without meaning to.

  3. Regulated science · reading the primary source

    From the lab I moved into regulated science and policy, working on psychedelic and cannabis regulation, where the rules are unsettled, the stakes are real, and one sloppy claim can end a program. That's where I learned to read a statute the way I read a study: against the primary source, never on someone else's summary. It's also where I saw how much of the risk inside a serious organization is quietly informational. A wrong date. A stale citation. A regulatory trigger nobody flagged.

  4. Law school · AI, made verifiable

    Then AI arrived in earnest, and I went to law school for it. I'm a JD candidate at Northeastern, in administrative and regulatory practice, and I do legal research inside firms while building AI workflows on Harvey and adjacent platforms. Working around legal AI teaches you one thing fast: a confident wrong answer is not a quirk, it's a sanctions risk. My job was never to make AI faster. It was to make it verifiable, and to build the workflow so speed never outruns the checking.

  5. The conviction · Aloha AI

    Underneath all of it is a single conviction I keep proving out. AI adoption, governance, and organizational change are behavioral problems before they are technical ones. Organizations succeed when people understand, trust, remember, adopt, and act on what they're given. Aloha AI is what happens when the neuroscience of how humans trust, a working command of what the rules actually are, and the hands-on ability to build the system all sit in one person at once.

The clearest proof of the standard is a real artifact. As law-clerk work product prepared under attorney supervision, I produced a legal research memorandum on clergy counseling in entheogenic churches: roughly eight sections and twenty-nine citations, every one checked against the primary source rather than a summary.

That is the same discipline the IDR Engine enforces inside the AI builds. When a wrong date or a stale citation can end a program, the answer has to be verifiable on its face, and the workflow has to catch the error before you do.

How I work

Primary sources, real adoption, clear limits

Two commitments run through everything. First: every figure is grounded in a primary source you can check. I'd rather show the citation than ask you to trust me. Second: every system is designed around how your people will actually use it, because a tool no one adopts is a cost, not an asset.

Engagements are scoped and priced up front, never "let's talk." I stay small on purpose, so the person you scope with is the person who builds.

A note on scope: NSAG (Nervous-System-Aware Governance) is my adjacent practice under the same LLC, serving cities, institutions, and foundations on the human side of governance and adoption. It runs on the same neuroscience-of-trust method, and it is a separate engagement from Aloha AI. If your work belongs there, I'll tell you.
