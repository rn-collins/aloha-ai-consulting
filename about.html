The story

Three fields, one question

I started in the brain. My master's is in Anatomy & Neurobiology from Boston University, where the working question was less about neurons in isolation and more about behavior: how people learn something new, when they decide to trust it, and what makes a habit finally stick. That question never left. It turns out to be the same one that decides whether a piece of software gets used or quietly abandoned.

From the lab I moved into regulated science, doing regulatory and compliance work inside psychedelic clinical research, where the rules are unsettled, the stakes are real, and a sloppy claim can end a program. That's where I learned to read a statute the way I'd read a study: carefully, against the primary source, never on someone else's summary. It's also where I saw how much of the risk in a serious organization is informational: a wrong date, a stale citation, a regulatory trigger nobody flagged.

Then AI arrived in earnest, and I went to law school for it, at Northeastern Law, focused on administrative and regulatory practice, while working as an AI Workflow Developer inside an Am Law firm, building on Harvey and adjacent platforms. Inside a firm, you learn fast that a confident wrong answer isn't a curiosity; it's a sanctions risk. The job wasn't to make AI faster. It was to make it verifiable, and to build the workflow so speed never outran the checking.

Alongside the applied work, I write peer-reviewed articles and book chapters, in press, because thinking in public keeps the reasoning honest. And I build: forty-plus tools and live intelligence systems, shipped, not slideware. Somewhere in the middle of all of it the pattern became obvious. The neuroscience of how humans trust, plus a working command of what the rules actually are, plus the hands-on ability to build the system, held together, at once. That combination is Aloha AI.

How I work

Primary sources, real adoption, no hand-waving

Two commitments run through everything. First: every figure is grounded in a primary source you can check. I'd rather show the citation than ask you to trust me. Second: every system is designed around how your people will actually use it, because a tool no one adopts is a cost, not an asset.

Engagements are scoped and priced up front, never "let's talk." Most start with a Systems Audit: the fastest proof, and it points to whatever comes next. I stay small on purpose, so the person you scope with is the person who builds.

Two things I will always tell you

  • Where the AI is likely to be wrong, and how the workflow catches it.
  • Where I'm not the right person, and who is. I'm a JD candidate, not a licensed attorney; this is regulatory intelligence, not legal advice.
