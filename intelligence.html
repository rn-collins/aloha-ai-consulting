How this works

Each monitor sweeps; the digest reports the deltas

No magic, and nothing hidden. Three plain steps keep the hub honest and current.

A scheduled sweep

Each monitor re-checks its primary sources on a set cadence: some track the Federal Register as it publishes, others run a weekly pass across agency pages, dockets, and bar guidance.

What changed surfaces

The sweep compares the record against the last pull and flags what moved: a new posting, a shifted status, a fresh opinion. Every entry keeps its source and the date it was read.

The digest pulls the deltas

Once a week the changes across all six monitors collect into one read. That is the "what changed this week" section of the Aloha AI Dispatch, so you catch a shift without watching six pages yourself.

Method & provenance

Primary-source discipline, so a figure survives scrutiny

The reason a number holds up is that you can trace it. Every claim in a monitor carries where it came from and when it was pulled, because a figure without provenance is a liability in a regulated setting.

Source
The operative record itself: Federal Register, CFR, dockets, agency orders, court filings, peer-reviewed science.
Retrieval date
Every figure is stamped with when it was pulled, so it reads as a point in time you can re-verify.
Confidence
Each signal is scored as settled, contested, or projected, so certainty is never overstated.
Re-check cadence
On a retainer, sources are re-pulled on a schedule; a change in the record surfaces as an alert, not a surprise.

Time-dated regulatory content reflects the record as of its retrieval date and can move afterward. Confirm any figure against the operative source before relying on it. This is regulatory intelligence and AI systems work, not legal advice, and nothing here creates grounds for reliance.
