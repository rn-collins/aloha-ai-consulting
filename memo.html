I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The question. How should a psychedelic or entheogenic church define and structure “clergy counseling” as a formal role that withstands scrutiny from the DEA, the IRS, and — if employment claims arise — the courts?

The bottom line. A well-documented clergy counselor role is legally defensible and practically necessary, but it must simultaneously satisfy four distinct legal frameworks that do not map neatly onto one another: the ministerial exception (employment law), federal RFRA sincerity review (DEA), the IRS illegality doctrine (tax exemption), and state regulatory prohibitions on clinical language and scope creep (Oregon and Colorado). The draft charter provisions in Section VII are designed to serve all four functions at once.

II. ACADEMIC AND PROFESSIONAL BACKGROUND

Before addressing the legal definition of clergy counseling, the academic literature establishes what structured psychedelic support work requires. This context informs why the distinction between clergy counseling and licensed psychotherapy matters legally and practically.

The foundational safety framework for human psychedelic research is Johnson, Richards & Griffiths, Human Hallucinogen Research: Guidelines for Safety, 22 J. Psychopharmacology 603 (2008) (PMID 18593734). The paper establishes that responsible psychedelic work requires structured screening, deliberate preparation, monitored administration with at least two trained personnel, and meaningful follow-up. The exclusion of volunteers with a personal or family history of psychotic disorders is identified as a core safeguard, and persisting adverse reactions are noted to be rare when these guidelines are followed. This framework provides the evidence base for why psychedelic support roles — whether clinical or religious — must be defined, bounded, and distinct from ad hoc engagement.

Phelps, Developing Guidelines and Competencies for the Training of Psychedelic Therapists, 57 J. Humanistic Psych. 450 (2017), identifies six competencies required of psychedelic therapists: (1) empathetic abiding presence; (2) trust enhancement; (3) spiritual intelligence; (4) knowledge of the physical and psychological effects of psychedelics; (5) therapist self-awareness and ethical integrity; and (6) proficiency in complementary techniques. Phelps also delineates twelve fundamental curricular domains. This framework can usefully inform a well-drafted clergy counselor definition within a religious idiom, while the definition itself must remain legally distinct from licensed psychotherapy. Using this literature to “parallel” rather than merely inform the clergy role risks blurring the clergy/therapist boundary that Section VII works to establish.

The entheogenic ceremonial context is addressed in Labate & Cavnar eds., The Therapeutic Use of Ayahuasca (Springer 2014), which demonstrates that entheogenic practice commonly combines sacramental use, ceremonial leadership, communal norms, and post-experience integration — precisely what a clergy counselor performs.

For the baseline professional definition of pastoral counseling, the APA Dictionary of Psychology establishes that it sits at the intersection of spiritual care and psychological frameworks while remaining distinct from licensed mental-health treatment. The current institutional landscape is maintained by ACPE, which in 2019 consolidated with the former American Association of Pastoral Counselors (AAPC). ACPE reports that AAPC now exists within ACPE as the ACPE Psychotherapy Commission, which oversees the Spiritually Integrated Psychotherapy (SIP) Program. Counsel should verify that this organizational structure remains current before relying on it in client-facing documents, as the source reliability is assessed as Moderate in Appendix A.

III. LEGAL FRAMEWORK: CLERGY, MINISTERIAL STATUS, AND RELIGIOUS FREEDOM

A. The Ministerial Exception — What Courts Look For

There is no federal statutory definition of “clergy” specific to psychedelic or entheogenic churches. Courts evaluate the question functionally, using the ministerial exception jurisprudence as a useful template. An important threshold note: the ministerial exception is an employment law doctrine — it determines whether a church employee may bring employment discrimination claims against the church. It is borrowed here by analogy for the purpose of identifying what governing documents should include to define clergy status; it does not itself confer RFRA protection, determine DEA sincerity, or create IRS eligibility. Those are distinct doctrinal tracks addressed in Sections III.B, V, and VI respectively.

The Supreme Court first formally recognized the ministerial exception in Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church & School v. EEOC, 565 U.S. 171 (2012) (9-0, C.J. Roberts, Jan. 11, 2012). The Court identified as relevant: (1) formal religious title; (2) religious educational training; (3) formal commissioning or ordination; (4) the person’s own public holding-out as a minister; and (5) the religious functions the person actually performs. Hosanna-Tabor’s multi-factor inquiry should not, however, be treated as a rigid checklist. In Our Lady of Guadalupe School v. Morrissey-Berru, 591 U.S. 732 (2020) (7-2, J. Alito, July 8, 2020), the Court clarified that what an employee does — specifically, whether the role lies at the core of the religious institution’s mission — is the controlling inquiry, and no single factor is determinative.

For an entheogenic church, a court would most likely examine whether: (1) the governing documents recognize a clergy or ministerial office; (2) there is a documented process for appointment, ordination, commissioning, or authorization; (3) the clergy counselor has defined sacramental duties; (4) the role carries responsibility for religious instruction, ritual leadership, pastoral counseling, and congregational care; and (5) the role is embedded in church doctrine rather than a commercial service offering.

B. Federal RFRA and the Entheogenic Sacrament Cases

The Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993, 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000bb–2000bb-4, prohibits the federal government from substantially burdening a person’s sincere religious exercise unless it can demonstrate a compelling governmental interest pursued through the least restrictive means. Critical limitation: In City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U.S. 507 (1997), the Supreme Court held that RFRA is unconstitutional as applied to state and local governments. Federal RFRA therefore governs only federal law — including the federal Controlled Substances Act — and does not constrain Oregon’s ORS 475A or Colorado’s C.R.S. Chapter 12-170.

Gonzales v. O Centro Espírita Beneficente União do Vegetal, 546 U.S. 418 (2006) (8-0, C.J. Roberts, Feb. 21, 2006; Alito, J., recused), held that the government failed to demonstrate — at the preliminary injunction stage — a compelling interest under RFRA in prohibiting the UDV church’s sacramental hoasca use. The Supreme Court affirmed the Tenth Circuit’s affirmance of the district court’s preliminary injunction, remanding for further proceedings. This is the controlling precedent for federal CSA exemptions, though counsel should note that the holding technically addressed the preliminary injunction standard and did not issue a final merits determination on the government’s compelling interest across all circumstances.

Church of the Holy Light of the Queen v. Mukasey, 615 F. Supp. 2d 1210 (D. Or. Mar. 18, 2009), applied this framework to a Santo Daime ayahuasca church in Oregon and granted a preliminary RFRA exemption from the CSA. Vacatur characterization — resolved. The case was settled after the district court’s preliminary injunction issued, mooting the government’s appeal. The Ninth Circuit vacated the district court’s order as a consequence. Church of Holy Light of Queen v. Holder, 443 F. App’x 302 (9th Cir. 2011); Church of Holy Light of Queen v. Holder, No. 13-35058 (9th Cir. Aug. 4, 2014).

A critical caveat on the weight of this vacatur: under U.S. Bancorp Mortgage Co. v. Bonner Mall Partnership, 513 U.S. 18 (1994), vacatur is generally not available when mootness results from settlement, because the settling party voluntarily surrendered its legal remedy rather than having appellate review frustrated by circumstance. The 9th Circuit’s vacatur in a settlement-mooted posture was therefore potentially anomalous under Bancorp. Whether the vacatur was proper on those facts is a question this memo does not resolve — but it is immaterial to the citation problem. The vacatur is a fact of record regardless of whether it was correctly granted, and the 2009 analysis carries no binding precedential weight in either event. Counsel may cite it for persuasive value as an analysis that survived an unreviewable preliminary injunction stage; the Ninth Circuit’s vacatur, however procedurally characterized, does not erase the reasoning. Citation rule: always disclose the full subsequent history; never cite the 2009 opinion as binding or as having survived on the merits.

Soul Quest Church of Mother Earth, Inc. v. Attorney General, No. 22-11072 (11th Cir. Dec. 18, 2023), illustrates how the DEA applies sincerity review as a threshold gate. The DEA denied Soul Quest’s RFRA petition after concluding — in its administrative denial letter, not in any court ruling — that the church had not demonstrated sincere religious belief for its therapeutic ayahuasca use. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed solely on the jurisdictional ground that the district court lacked subject matter jurisdiction under 21 U.S.C. § 877 to review the DEA’s final decision. The court did not reach the RFRA merits. The case’s significance lies in the DEA’s sincerity analysis as recited in the briefing and the court’s recitation of the agency’s reasoning; the underlying DEA denial letter is the operative document and should be obtained before advising any client on the DEA’s sincerity framework.

C. RLUIPA — Land Use Protections

The Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act, 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000cc–2000cc-5 (enacted 2000), provides independent federal protection for religious exercise in the context of land use regulations. Unlike federal RFRA, RLUIPA does apply to state and local governments when they exercise their land use and zoning authority. Under 42 U.S.C. § 2000cc(a)(1), no government may impose or implement a land use regulation that imposes a substantial burden on the religious exercise of a person or institution unless the government demonstrates the burden furthers a compelling governmental interest through the least restrictive means.

RLUIPA’s equal-terms provision, § 2000cc(b)(1), is often the most accessible ground for an entheogenic church challenging a local zoning restriction: a municipality that permits comparable secular assembly uses — community meeting halls, secular wellness retreat centers, meditation centers — on the same or similar terms while restricting or excluding the entheogenic church creates a strong equal-terms claim without requiring the heavier lift of proving substantial burden. RLUIPA additionally prohibits regulations that unreasonably limit religious assemblies within a jurisdiction, § 2000cc(b)(3)(B).

For an entheogenic church seeking to establish a physical ceremonial space — whether a freestanding sanctuary, a residential structure used for ceremony, or a building in a commercially zoned area — local zoning ordinances may impose restrictions that would be challengeable under RLUIPA. RLUIPA analysis should be conducted as part of any site selection process. It operates independently of and in addition to federal RFRA. The Oregon DOJ memo referenced below acknowledges RLUIPA’s potential relevance but concludes it does not compel OHA to create differentiated entheogenic practitioner licenses under ORS 475A; that conclusion addresses the licensure framework, not the real property dimension that RLUIPA primarily governs. Depending on the scope of the client engagement, a standalone site-selection memo addressing RLUIPA may be warranted.

IV. STATE REGULATORY ANALYSIS

A. Oregon

Program structure. Oregon’s psilocybin program under ORS Chapter 475A is organized around licensed facilitators, service centers, manufacturers, and laboratories. Psilocybin may be consumed only at a licensed service center. Under OAR 333-333-5000, the preparation session requires a client information form, a transportation plan (OAR 333-333-5150), a safety and support plan (OAR 333-333-5080), and informed consent review. Following administration, a facilitator must contact the client within 72 hours to offer information on integration sessions. OAR 333-333-5250(7). OAR 333-333-5120(3) requires a nondirective facilitation approach, defined at OAR 333-333-1010(57) (renumbered effective January 1, 2026) as a client-centered approach to facilitation in which the client makes decisions related to their participation in psilocybin services and the facilitator maintains a consistent disposition with a client, while avoiding giving the client direct advice or offering an interpretation of a client’s statements, behaviors or needs unless appropriate for health and safety reasons. The definition expressly states that nondirective facilitation does not prohibit a facilitator from offering options, resources, referrals or providing health and safety support to clients within their scope of practice. Oregon’s rules also impose detailed ethical standards on vulnerability, touch, sexual boundaries, abuse-of-power issues, conflict disclosure, and harm reduction.

OAR 333-333-1010 therapy-prohibition citation — correction. Prior versions of this memo flagged as open a question about the current subsection number for a “psilocybin-assisted therapy” prohibition in OAR 333-333-1010. The current full text of OAR 333-333-1010 has been verified directly against the Oregon Secretary of State’s live division 333 listing (PH 21-2025, eff. Jan. 1, 2026). There is no therapy-prohibition definition anywhere in OAR 333-333-1010 under any subsection number. The prior academic citation to “OAR 333-333-1010(72)” for a therapy prohibition — cited in a 2024 Harvard Petrie-Flom Center piece — was a misattribution. OAR 333-333-1010(72) currently defines “Psilocybin extract,” a product category definition with no relation to the therapy prohibition. The subsection numbered (72) in the original 2022 rules was “Authority” (the Oregon Health Authority); subsequent amendments renumbered definitions entirely. At no point did a definition of “psilocybin-assisted therapy prohibition” exist in OAR 333-333-1010.

The prohibition on therapy-adjacent language operates through three operative rules that have not changed section numbers: OAR 333-333-6040(c) (Dishonest Conduct); OAR 333-333-5040 (Informed Consent form); and OAR 333-333-5130 (Facilitator Scope of Practice). These are the correct and current citations.

Nondirective facilitation subsection number — confirmed. OAR 333-333-1010(57) defines “nondirective facilitation” as a client-centered approach in which the client makes decisions related to their participation in psilocybin services and the facilitator avoids giving direct advice or interpreting client statements, behaviors or needs unless appropriate for health and safety reasons. The renumbering from the prior subsection (48) to (57) is confirmed against the current Oregon SOS text. This citation is stable and correct.

Prohibited terminology — material for Oregon churches. The Oregon rules prohibit clinical framing of psilocybin services through three provisions, each verified against current Oregon SOS text as of the date of this memo. OAR 333-333-5040 (Informed Consent) requires clients to acknowledge that psilocybin services “are not a medical or clinical treatment.” OAR 333-333-5130 (Facilitator Scope of Practice) prohibits facilitators from diagnosing or treating health conditions and from exercising the privileges of any separately held professional license while providing psilocybin services. OAR 333-333-6040(c) (Dishonest Conduct) prohibits facilitators from making untruthful or misleading statements, which OHA has treated as encompassing clinical claims about psilocybin services. Any entheogenic church operating in Oregon — whether inside or outside the licensed program — should audit all client-facing language to ensure no use of “therapy,” “treatment,” or clinical characterization that could trigger any of these three provisions.

Oregon DOJ memo — what it actually holds. The Oregon Health Authority previously solicited legal guidance on whether the psilocybin program could accommodate an “entheogenic practitioner” license category. An Oregon Department of Justice legal memorandum — dated May 25, 2022, Senior AAG Shannon K. O’Fallon (for public release) — concluded: (1) OHA has no statutory authority to exempt entheogenic practitioners from licensure; (2) OHA cannot exempt licensed entheogenic practitioners from all regulation; and (3) creating less restrictive standards specifically for entheogenic practitioners would likely violate the Establishment Clause of both the Oregon and U.S. constitutions, because applying fewer restrictions on entheogenic practitioners would be viewed as granting an impermissible privilege to religion not available on a secular basis. To understand why: the Establishment Clause prohibits not only government hostility toward religion but also preferential treatment available exclusively to religious actors that is not available to similarly situated secular actors. See Larson v. Valente, 456 U.S. 228 (1982); Texas Monthly, Inc. v. Bullock, 489 U.S. 1 (1989). The proposal was found to impermissibly favor religion — not to discriminate against it. It was not adopted into the final regulatory framework.

Oregon’s outside-the-program path — religious freedom theories. A church wishing to use psilocybin in ceremony outside ORS 475A must proceed on separate religious freedom grounds. There are two distinct legal layers, which must be analyzed separately:

Federal CSA exposure: The federal Controlled Substances Act classifies psilocybin as a Schedule I substance. Federal RFRA, 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000bb–2000bb-4, provides the mechanism for a church to seek a federal exemption via DEA petition or direct federal court challenge, following the O Centro framework. This is a claim against federal law; it operates independently of ORS 475A.

Oregon state law exposure: Federal RFRA does not apply to Oregon’s state law. A challenge to ORS 475A’s requirements as applied to ceremonial religious practice would be grounded in two independent provisions: the federal First Amendment Free Exercise Clause (as interpreted through Employment Division v. Smith, 494 U.S. 872 (1990) (J. Scalia, majority), which generally upholds neutral, generally applicable laws), and Article I, Section 3 of the Oregon Constitution, which provides: “No law shall in any case whatever control the free exercise, and enjoyment of religious opinions, or interfere with the rights of conscience.” Oregon courts have construed this provision as requiring that statutes treat all religious organizations equally, Newport Church of the Nazarene v. Hensley, 335 Or. 1, 56 P.3d 386 (2002) — though that case arose in an unemployment compensation context and would require careful analogical extension to the psilocybin licensing setting. Oregon does not have a state-level RFRA analog.

Oregon Art. I, § 3 analysis — state constitutional layer for outside-the-program operation. Oregon’s state constitutional protection for religious exercise is Article I, Section 3: “No law shall in any case whatever control the free exercise, and enjoyment of religious opinions, or interfere with the rights of conscience.” Oregon has no state-level RFRA analog. The primary adversarial precedent is Smith v. Employment Division, 310 Or. 376, 799 P.2d 148 (1990) (Oregon Supreme Court on remand from Employment Division v. Smith, 494 U.S. 872 (1990)), which upheld denial of unemployment benefits to sacramental peyote users.

A threshold doctrinal point that significantly strengthens the Art. I, § 3 challenge has not previously been analyzed in this memo: the Oregon Supreme Court’s 1990 decision on remand did not resolve the case under Oregon’s constitution at all. On the first remand from SCOTUS, the Oregon Supreme Court concluded that Oregon law prohibited sacramental peyote use and then held that prohibition unconstitutional under the federal First Amendment Free Exercise Clause. SCOTUS granted certiorari again precisely to reverse that federal constitutional holding. The Oregon Supreme Court never completed an independent analysis under Article I, Section 3. As a result, Oregon’s own constitutional provision has never been definitively applied to a sacramental controlled substance use case — the doctrinal field remains open at the state constitutional level.

The second and more significant distinction is structural. Smith on remand addressed Oregon’s straightforward criminal prohibition on sacramental peyote possession — a law that made the religious practice itself illegal. An Art. I, § 3 challenge to ORS 475A would contest something different: a licensing scheme that regulates but does not expressly prohibit ceremonial psilocybin use, and that has no religious-use exemption. Under Oregon constitutional doctrine, this distinction matters. Oregon courts have construed Article I, Section 3 as requiring equal treatment among religious groups. Newport Church of the Nazarene v. Hensley, 335 Or. 1, 56 P.3d 386 (2002). A church could argue that ORS 475A’s nondirective facilitation mandate, its prohibition on service centers in residences, and its requirement that consumption occur only in licensed premises each burden sacramental ceremonial use without comparable burden on secular wellness uses, and that no sufficient state interest justifies that differential burden in a non-criminal licensing context.

The practical bottom line on this analysis: the Art. I, § 3 constitutional path is a genuine one — not a foreclosed one — but it is not a clean one. No Oregon court has applied Article I, Section 3 to a controlled substance licensing scheme rather than a direct prohibition, and the doctrinal path from Newport Church (unemployment compensation) to ORS 475A challenge requires careful development that has not been litigated. Outside-the-program ceremonial operation in Oregon therefore remains inadvisable as a compliance posture pending either a DEA RFRA exemption covering the federal CSA layer or an affirmative legal development under Oregon state law. The state constitutional argument is available to assert as a defense if a client chooses to operate outside the program, but this memo cannot advise that it is likely to prevail on the current record. A standalone memo developing this argument in full, including analysis of the current Oregon Supreme Court’s approach to Article I, Section 3 and any intervening Oregon appellate decisions, should be prepared before advising any client to rely on it.

In practice, Oregon psilocybin church roles are therefore either: (1) church-internal religious roles inside the ORS 475A licensed program (facilitator + service center license), or (2) church-internal religious roles outside the licensed program, relying on a DEA RFRA exemption from the federal CSA plus an Art. I, § 3 state-law defense that has genuine doctrinal footing but has not been tested in court.

B. Colorado

Program structure. Colorado’s natural medicine program under the Natural Medicine Health Act (Prop. 122, 2022, as amended by SB 23-290, eff. July 1, 2023) is implemented through 4 CCR 755-1. The regulated system covers facilitators, clinical facilitators, training licensees, healing centers, cultivators, manufacturers, and testing facilities. Facilitators may not independently practice medicine or psychotherapy as part of facilitation.

Statutory religious ceremony carve-out. C.R.S. § 12-170-108(4), as amended by SB 23-290 § 7, eff. July 1, 2023, provides:

“Nothing in this section prohibits an individual from performing a bona fide religious, culturally traditional, or spiritual ceremony, if the individual informs an individual engaging in the ceremony that the individual is not a licensed facilitator and that the ceremony is not associated with commercial, business, or for-profit activity.”

This provision — in the statute, not in 4 CCR 755-1 (the administrative rules do not contain this language) — creates a genuine dual track: a regulated commercial track under 4 CCR 755-1, and a noncommercial religious/spiritual track outside licensure under § 12-170-108(4), conditioned on two required disclosures. This section has been verified as current through Fall 2025; the text and section number are unchanged from SB 23-290.

The two required disclosures — who must make them and how. The statute requires “the individual” to inform each person engaging in the ceremony of (1) non-licensed-facilitator status and (2) the noncommercial nature of the ceremony. In a one-on-one ceremony this is clearly the officiant. In a group ceremony, the statute does not specify whether congregational or group-level disclosure suffices or whether individual, participant-by-participant written disclosure is required. This is an open question that has not been addressed in Colorado regulatory guidance or litigation. Until it is resolved, a church relying on the § 12-170-108(4) carve-out should obtain written individual disclosure from each participant documenting both required elements, to minimize the risk that a court or regulator finds that a group announcement was insufficient.

Colorado substances expansion — important scope limitation. The § 12-170-108(4) noncommercial religious ceremony carve-out applies to ceremonies involving any “natural medicine” as that term is defined under C.R.S. § 12-170-104. The facilitated use track under 4 CCR 755-1 — the regulated commercial track — currently covers only psilocybin and psilocin. Dimethyltryptamine (DMT) and mescaline (excluding peyote) are eligible to be added to the facilitated-use program only after June 1, 2026, and only if the Natural Medicine Advisory Board recommends them and the relevant departments approve. See C.R.S. § 12-170-104(12); SB 23-290. Ibogaine carries no statutory date restriction and may be added to the regulated facilitated-use program at any time upon Board recommendation.

A clarification on scope is required: the § 12-170-108(4) carve-out applies to the statutory definition of “natural medicine” generally — it is not itself limited to psilocybin and psilocin. The facilitated-use program is what is currently capped at psilocybin and psilocin. This means an ayahuasca (DMT-containing) church may today invoke the § 12-170-108(4) noncommercial carve-out under Colorado state law. However, a church doing so must separately analyze federal CSA exposure, because DMT remains a Schedule I controlled substance under federal law regardless of Colorado’s classification, and C.R.S. § 12-170-108(4) provides no federal protection. A church using DMT in noncommercial ceremony in Colorado is protected from state prosecution by the carve-out but retains full federal CSA exposure and must rely on a DEA RFRA exemption or direct federal court relief for any federal protection.

Colorado outside-the-program constitutional framework. Like Oregon, Colorado has no state-level RFRA analog. The independent state constitutional protection for religious exercise is Article II, Section 4 of the Colorado Constitution, which provides: “The free exercise and enjoyment of religious profession and worship, without discrimination, shall forever hereafter be guaranteed; and no person shall be denied any civil or political right, privilege or capacity, on account of his opinions concerning religion.” The liberty of conscience secured by Art. II, § 4 “shall not be construed to dispense with oaths or affirmations, excuse acts of licentiousness or justify practices inconsistent with the good order, peace or safety of the state.” The application of Art. II, § 4 to natural medicine ceremonial practice outside the § 12-170-108(4) carve-out has not been litigated.

C. Comparative Summary

Oregon’s program is facilitator-centered and provides no clergy-specific category; ceremonial religious practice must fit inside the licensed program or proceed on separate federal and state constitutional theories as analyzed above. Colorado expressly preserves space for noncommercial bona fide religious or spiritual ceremonies outside facilitator licensure via C.R.S. § 12-170-108(4), while regulating commercial facilitation through 4 CCR 755-1. Colorado’s framework is materially more hospitable to a distinct, documented clergy counselor role.

Both states have no state RFRA analog. For federal CSA exposure in both states, federal RFRA and the O Centro framework remain the operative legal vehicle.

V. DEA SINCERITY REVIEW AND REQUIRED ORGANIZATIONAL DOCUMENTATION

The Soul Quest proceedings demonstrate that DEA sincerity review is a genuine threshold gate, not a formality. The DEA’s formal RFRA Guidance Document (DEA Diversion Control Division, Version 2, Nov. 20, 2020) establishes that a petition for a CSA religious exemption must include: (1) detailed information about the religion’s history, belief system, structure, practice, membership policies, rituals, holidays, organization, and leadership; (2) each specific religious practice involving manufacture, distribution, dispensing, importation, exportation, use, or possession of a controlled substance; and (3) the specific conditions and use of the controlled substance within those practices. The petition must be signed under penalty of perjury. See 28 U.S.C. § 1746.

The DEA’s denial of the Soul Quest petition illustrates specific sincerity factors that have been cited against newer organizations: inconsistent or sparse references to central religious texts by members; lack of membership requirements tied to doctrinal commitment; transient participation (retreat model versus congregation model); prior affiliations adopted for legal protection rather than genuine doctrinal continuity; and the absence of structured diversion controls. See DEA denial analysis recited in Soul Quest, No. 22-11072 (11th Cir. Dec. 18, 2023). For additional secondary commentary, see Harris Sliwoski, “How DEA Denies Religious Exemption Petitions,” Harris Sliwoski LLP Psychlaw Blog (2025), available at https://harris-sliwoski.com/psychlawblog/ (secondary/practitioner source; reliability: Moderate).

The two most recent successful exemptions illustrate what adequate documentation and operational structure look like in practice. In Church of the Eagle and the Condor v. Garland, No. 2:22-cv-1004-SRB (D. Ariz.), the church settled with the DEA in April 2024, becoming the first organization to obtain a RFRA ayahuasca exemption through litigation settlement. The settlement required the church to maintain stringent security protocols, detailed ceremony records, and ongoing DEA oversight as conditions of the exemption. In Church of Gaia (Spokane, WA), the DEA granted in May 2025 the first CSA religious exemption entirely through the non-litigation petition process, making it the first such exemption ever granted under the DEA’s Guidance Document pathway without a court order. (Source: Church of Gaia website, churchgaia.org/about-us/legal-information; confirmed by Harris Sliwoski LLP, May 20, 2025; Marijuana Moment, May 21, 2025; Psychedelic Week, May 19, 2025.) Reported key factors in the Church of Gaia approval included a suspension of sacramental ayahuasca use during the pendency of the petition — a strategy that carries its own doctrinal risk, as suspension of religious practice can be argued to undercut sincerity claims — robust internal safety and compliance protocols, and thorough organizational documentation. The Church’s counsel has not publicly disclosed how the suspension-of-use tension was resolved.

The lack of DEA transparency about what distinguished the Church of Gaia petition from the many denied or pending petitions is not merely an observation — it is a structural legal problem for any client attempting to tailor a petition to succeed. The DEA has provided no public explanation of its reasoning, and Church of Gaia’s agreement with the DEA may be subject to strict confidentiality requirements, meaning other churches cannot rely on its contents.

As of the date of this memo, a separate mandamus action — In re Iowaska Church of Healing, No. 25-1140 (D.C. Cir., argued Nov. 14, 2025, decision pending) — seeks to compel the DEA to act on a six-year-old petition, with the D.C. Circuit panel reportedly expressing open frustration with multi-year agency inaction at oral argument. Supervising attorney should calendar a review of this memo upon issuance of the D.C. Circuit’s decision, as the outcome will affect advice to any client contemplating the petition pathway.

Documentation checklist for DEA sincerity review. Any entheogenic church that may seek a federal RFRA exemption should maintain, from inception:

  1. Governing documents (articles, bylaws, or equivalent) formally establishing the church’s doctrine and identifying the clergy counselor role
  2. Appointment or commissioning records for each clergy counselor, including the doctrinal basis for the role
  3. Membership records demonstrating continuity of participation and doctrinal engagement beyond retreat attendance
  4. Ceremony records documenting the sacramental use of the entheogen as central — not incidental — to religious practice
  5. A written diversion control plan
  6. A documented safety protocol and referral framework

The clergy counselor documentation in Section VII directly supports items 1, 2, and 4.

VI. IRS § 501(c)(3) — THE CIRCULAR PROBLEM

A. The Controlling Rule: Iowaska Church of Healing v. Werfel

The IRS independently applies the illegality doctrine: an organization whose primary activities are illegal under federal law cannot satisfy the operational test for § 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status, regardless of the sincerity of its religious beliefs. The controlling authority is Iowaska Church of Healing v. Werfel, 105 F.4th 402 (D.C. Cir. June 21, 2024).

In Iowaska, the D.C. Circuit affirmed denial of 501(c)(3) status to an ayahuasca church on the grounds that, without a prior DEA CSA exemption or court-ordered RFRA exemption, the church’s sacramental ayahuasca use was illegal under federal law. The court held that O Centro does not itself confer 501(c)(3) eligibility — it stands only for the proposition that a CSA exemption is possible, not that it has been granted. The IRS has no statutory authority to determine whether a church’s substance use warrants a RFRA exemption from the CSA; that determination belongs to the DEA and the courts. The IRS is not the right defendant for the church’s underlying RFRA claim — the DEA is. The church’s RFRA claim against the IRS was therefore dismissed for lack of Article III standing because the injury was not traceable to the IRS denial (it was traceable to DEA inaction) and would not be redressed by a favorable IRS ruling. The D.C. Circuit declined en banc rehearing following an August 6, 2024 petition by the Church and a DOJ opposition dated October 11, 2024. The en banc denial order is reflected in the docket’s last known filing of November 13, 2024; the precise denial date is not publicly confirmed but the denial is a fact of record. Iowaska Church of Healing v. Werfel, No. 23-5122 (D.C. Cir. 2024) (en banc denied).

B. The Circular Structure

This creates a documented structural problem: no 501(c)(3) without a DEA or court exemption; DEA petitions pending for years without resolution; IRS will not wait. The UDV church (the O Centro respondent) did eventually obtain 501(c)(3) status — but only after winning at the Supreme Court. Church of Gaia has reportedly obtained 501(c)(3) status following its DEA exemption, but that model requires front-loading the entire exemption process before charitable status is available. The pending mandamus action in In re Iowaska, No. 25-1140 (D.C. Cir., argued Nov. 14, 2025, decision pending), is attempting to break this logjam by compelling the DEA to act — if the D.C. Circuit grants mandamus relief, it could change the pipeline significantly for psilocybin churches by forcing DEA to issue timely written determinations.

C. Practical Consequences for This Engagement

Colorado noncommercial carve-out. C.R.S. § 12-170-108(4) requires that the ceremony be “not associated with commercial, business, or for-profit activity.” A church operating without 501(c)(3) status that charges ceremony fees — even characterized as donations — runs the dual risk of losing the statutory carve-out (commercial activity) and IRS scrutiny. Churches relying on the carve-out should be structured as nonprofit entities and avoid fee-for-service models for ceremonial participation pending either 501(c)(3) recognition or a DEA exemption that would open the commercial facilitation track.

Triple-duty documentation. Because the IRS and DEA pathways are now operationally sequential (DEA exemption must come first), any church for which this firm is drafting governing documents, clergy counselor definitions, or operational protocols should be advised that those documents will need to serve triple duty: (1) satisfying the DEA’s sincerity petition requirements under the RFRA Guidance Document; (2) satisfying the IRS organizational and operational tests once DEA status is obtained; and (3) supporting a ministerial exception defense if clergy employment claims arise. The charter provisions in Section VII below are drafted with all three functions in mind.

Pending litigation. Because In re Iowaska, No. 25-1140, is pending before the D.C. Circuit with a decision expected in 2026, there is active litigation that could materially change the 501(c)(3) landscape for psilocybin churches by the time any new church formed today reaches the exemption stage. Supervising attorney should calendar a review of this memo upon issuance of that decision.

VII. DRAFT CHARTER LANGUAGE

The following provisions are proposed for a church charter, bylaws, or standard operating procedure. Before finalizing, supervising counsel should confirm with the client whether “clergy counseling” is the preferred terminology; some entheogenic churches deliberately avoid the word “counseling” because of its clinical connotations, preferring “pastoral care,” “spiritual guidance,” or “sacramental accompaniment.” The substantive framework below accommodates alternative terminology.

Note on section numbering: These provisions are numbered 7.1–7.5 to correspond to this memo’s section numbering and for ease of cross-reference. They should be renumbered to match the client’s governing document structure upon adoption.

§ 7.1 Definition — Clergy Counselor

Clergy Counselor means a person formally authorized by the Church under its governing documents to provide pastoral and spiritual guidance concerning sacramental practice and integration, subject to the Church’s ethical rules and referral obligations, and not acting as a licensed medical or mental-health provider unless separately credentialed and expressly disclosed. No person may hold themselves out as a Clergy Counselor of the Church, or represent to any third party that they are performing clergy counseling on behalf of the Church, without formal authorization as provided in § 7.5 of these governing documents.

Drafting basis: Tracks the functional inquiry in Our Lady of Guadalupe, 591 U.S. 732; reflects Oregon’s nondirective facilitation framework under OAR 333-333-1010(57) (eff. Jan. 1, 2026); aligns with the Colorado § 12-170-108(4) disclosure framework; supports DEA sincerity petition documentation under the RFRA Guidance Document; and supports IRS operational test compliance under Iowaska, 105 F.4th 402. The prohibition on unauthorized use of the “Clergy Counselor” designation closes the gap that arises when informal actors perform equivalent functions, which can undermine the distinctiveness of the formal role in DEA sincerity review and ministerial exception analysis.

§ 7.2 Extended Definition — Clergy Counseling

Clergy counseling means pastoral or spiritual guidance provided by duly recognized Clergy of the Church for the purpose of preparing congregants for sacramental participation, supporting them during religious practice, and assisting them afterward with spiritual reflection, integration, ethical discernment, and congregational care, all in accordance with the Church’s sincerely held doctrines and practices.

§ 7.3 Limitations Clause

Clergy counseling is not medical diagnosis, psychotherapy, psychological treatment, or legal advice. Clergy shall not represent clergy counseling as licensed healthcare or licensed mental-health treatment, and shall not use the terms “therapy,” “treatment,” or any clinical characterization for clergy counseling services, unless the Clergy member separately holds such professional credentials and clearly discloses in writing, prior to the first engagement in that professional capacity, that they are acting in their separate professional capacity and not as clergy of the Church.

Drafting basis: The prohibition on “therapy” language is required for Oregon-operating churches under three verified operative rules: OAR 333-333-6040(c) (Dishonest Conduct), OAR 333-333-5040 (Informed Consent), and OAR 333-333-5130 (Facilitator Scope of Practice). Note: contrary to some prior secondary sources, there is no separate “therapy prohibition” definition in OAR 333-333-1010 — that citation was a misattribution, corrected in this memo. OAR 333-333-5130 further prohibits facilitators from exercising professional healthcare licenses while providing psilocybin services. Colorado 4 CCR 755-1 prohibits facilitators from independently practicing medicine or psychotherapy as part of facilitation outside the clinical-facilitator framework. The disclosure requirement is specified as written to satisfy Oregon’s documentation standards and to create a clear record for DEA and IRS purposes.

§ 7.4 Duties Clause

The duties of a Clergy Counselor may include:

  1. preparation of congregants for sacramental participation, including discussion of doctrine, ritual meaning, congregational norms, and the spiritual intentions of the participant;

  2. support of congregants during religious practice;

  3. post-ceremony spiritual reflection and integration, including assistance with meaning-making, ethical discernment, and continued engagement with congregational community;

  4. maintenance of confidentiality consistent with applicable law; provided, however, that the Clergy Counselor shall be deemed to have no greater confidentiality privilege than is recognized under applicable state law for clergy-penitent communications, and the Church’s written confidentiality policy shall specify what information may be disclosed, to whom, under what circumstances, and what becomes of session records upon dissolution of the Church or departure of a Clergy Counselor;

  5. compliance with the Church’s written ethical guidelines governing supportive touch, dual relationships, financial conflicts, and sexual misconduct, which guidelines shall be adopted in writing before any Clergy Counselor begins performing duties under these governing documents; and

  6. referral to licensed medical or mental-health professionals where the Clergy Counselor reasonably believes such care is warranted.

Drafting basis: Duties (a)–(c) reflect the entheogenic ceremonial framework in Labate & Cavnar (2014) and the structured support model in Johnson et al. (2008) and Phelps (2017). Duty (f) is required by the DEA Guidance Document’s diversion and safety framework and by Oregon and Colorado facilitator referral rules. Duty (d) addresses the clergy-penitent privilege gap: unlike licensed therapists, clergy counselors do not automatically have a state-recognized confidentiality privilege in most jurisdictions, and the extent to which the privilege applies to entheogenic church counseling relationships has not been litigated; the language sets explicit notice to participants and a record-management obligation. Duty (e) requires that written ethical guidelines be adopted before any Clergy Counselor begins performing duties — not merely referenced as if they exist — to ensure the provision has operational force.

§ 7.5 Authorization and Commissioning

The Church shall maintain a formal record of each Clergy Counselor’s authorization, including the date of appointment or commissioning, the authorizing body or officer within the Church’s governance structure, and the doctrinal basis for the role. Authorization may be granted, modified, or revoked only in accordance with the Church’s governing documents. A Clergy Counselor whose authorization is to be revoked or modified shall receive written notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond before any revocation or material modification takes effect, except where immediate action is required to protect the safety or wellbeing of congregants.

Drafting basis: Hosanna-Tabor, 565 U.S. 171 (formal title, training, and commissioning strengthen ministerial status); Our Lady of Guadalupe, 591 U.S. 732 (functional religious mission centrality is the controlling inquiry); DEA RFRA Guidance Document (organizational structure and leadership documentation required in sincerity petition); Iowaska, 105 F.4th 402 (IRS organizational test requires articles of organization limiting purposes to exempt purposes — commissioning records support demonstrating the role is doctrinal, not commercial). The fair-process clause for revocation prevents retaliatory or pretextual revocations that could give rise to employment-adjacent claims and pre-empts any challenge to the commissioning process as arbitrary.

VIII. CONCLUSION AND NEXT STEPS

The four-pillar framework. A well-documented clergy counselor role rests on: (1) formal recognition in the church’s governing documents; (2) a defined, doctrine-grounded set of functions centered on sacramental preparation, support, and integration; (3) explicit limits that separate clergy counseling from licensed psychotherapy, medical practice, and — in Oregon — therapy-adjacent terminology prohibited by OAR 333-333-1010; and (4) a referral obligation and ethical framework that parallel the safety and ethics requirements applicable to licensed facilitators under state law and to DEA sincerity review under federal RFRA.

Colorado is currently the stronger jurisdiction. C.R.S. § 12-170-108(4) offers the strongest current statutory support for a documented, noncommercial clergy counseling role operating outside the facilitator licensure framework. The carve-out follows the statutory definition of “natural medicine” — it is not itself capped at psilocybin and psilocin. It is the facilitated-use program under 4 CCR 755-1 that is currently limited to psilocybin and psilocin, pending the Natural Medicine Advisory Board’s action after June 1, 2026. A church performing DMT ceremonies today under the noncommercial carve-out has Colorado state-law protection but retains federal CSA exposure.

Oregon requires a DEA exemption and carries litigation risk. Outside-the-program ceremonial use in Oregon requires a federal RFRA exemption from CSA exposure. The Art. I, § 3 state constitutional argument is real but untested in the licensing-scheme context: Smith on remand resolved only the federal First Amendment question, leaving the Oregon constitutional field genuinely open. A church that operates outside the ORS 475A program can assert Art. I, § 3 as a defense, but cannot today be advised that it will prevail. A standalone brief developing the state constitutional argument in full — including current Oregon Supreme Court jurisprudence on Article I, Section 3 and any intervening decisions — should be prepared before any client is counseled to rely on it.

The IRS/DEA interlock is a structural constraint. A church cannot obtain § 501(c)(3) status under the illegality doctrine until it first obtains a DEA CSA exemption or a court-ordered RFRA exemption. The DEA exemption process has a documented backlog. The charter language in Section VII is designed to serve the DEA, IRS, and ministerial exception functions simultaneously, with an eye toward front-loading the strongest possible petition documentation from the moment of formation.

Two items that require active monitoring.

⚑ Calendar item 1: In re Iowaska Church of Healing, No. 25-1140 (D.C. Cir.) — argued November 14, 2025, decision pending. Outcome will affect the DEA petition pathway timeline and the § 501(c)(3) pipeline. Supplement this memo when the decision issues.

⚑ Calendar item 2: Colorado 4 CCR 755-1 rulemaking — hearing scheduled April 22, 2026. Monitor for amendments affecting the facilitated-use program, substance scope, and any guidance on the § 12-170-108(4) disclosure requirements.

Standing assignments for additional work, if authorized: - Standalone brief: Oregon Art. I, § 3 full development — the analytical foundation is in Section IV.A of this memo; the brief requires current Oregon Supreme Court jurisprudence survey and analysis of any post-2002 Art. I, § 3 decisions - DEA RFRA petition outline for a specific client - Supplemental memo upon In re Iowaska decision - Revision of charter language for a specific client matter

Tort liability and privilege — counsel’s attention required. This memo does not address civil tort liability. A clergy counselor who provides pre- or post-ceremony support to a congregant who later experiences a serious adverse event is not protected by RFRA, the ministerial exception, or any of the doctrines analyzed here. Both Oregon and Colorado require licensed facilitators to carry liability insurance; a church operating outside the licensed program without comparable coverage may expose itself and its clergy counselors to uninsured civil liability. Additionally, unlike licensed therapists, clergy counselors do not automatically hold a state-recognized confidentiality privilege, and the applicability of the clergy-penitent privilege to entheogenic church counseling relationships has not been litigated. Churches should not represent session communications as legally privileged without independent analysis of the applicable state privilege statutes.
